## The Essential Question

> "What does it mean to live well — and is the question itself the most important thing you can teach?"

Socrates is the only figure in Western history who built an entire philosophy out of not knowing. He claimed to possess no doctrine, no system, no answers. He possessed only a method: the relentless questioning of every assumption until either truth emerged or the questioner was forced to admit ignorance. His life was that method, practiced publicly, until Athens killed him for it at age seventy-one.

He is also the first figure in Western history to die for a philosophical commitment — to model, with his death, the same consistency between word and deed that he had spent his life demanding of others.

In the Nova Schola sequence, Socrates appears after Plato and Aristotle — the two great figures he formed — because understanding the teacher requires understanding what the students became. You see the weight of a river by studying what it carved, not by watching it at its source. Now we return to the source.

## Historical Context

### Athens in the Fifth Century BCE

Socrates was born into the most intellectually fertile city in human history at its most intellectually fertile moment. Athens in 470 BCE was twenty years past the Persian Wars — specifically the battles of Marathon (490) and Thermopylae and Salamis (480). The defeat of Persia had given Athens enormous prestige, material resources, and a sense of exceptional destiny. The city was building the Parthenon. It was producing Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides. It was the home of Pericles, who would make it the dominant Greek polis.

Athens was also a radical democracy — the first in history at significant scale. The assembly of male citizens voted on war and peace, made law, exiled dangerous individuals by the peculiar procedure of ostracism. Power flowed through speech. The ability to persuade a crowd of several thousand citizens was the most valuable skill in Athens — which is why rhetoric was the most prestigious education, why the Sophists made their fortunes teaching it, and why Socrates's opposition to the Sophists was not merely an intellectual difference but a political and economic provocation.

### The Sophists

The Sophists were professional teachers of rhetoric who charged high fees to teach wealthy Athenians how to argue convincingly. Their most provocative figures — Protagoras, Gorgias, Thrasymachus — taught (or were understood to teach) a kind of sophisticated relativism: that truth is what you can make others believe, that justice is the interest of the stronger, that conventional morality is just a set of social agreements with no deeper grounding.

Socrates was not a Sophist, but he was often confused with one. He also moved in public spaces, argued with everyone, and questioned conventional assumptions. The difference was everything: the Sophists charged money; Socrates charged nothing. The Sophists claimed to teach virtue; Socrates denied he knew what virtue was, and questioned whether it could be taught. The Sophists gave polished lectures; Socrates asked questions and claimed only to follow where the argument led.

### The Peloponnesian War

From 431 to 404 BCE — roughly Socrates's middle age through his late sixties — Athens was at war with Sparta and its allies. The war went badly. A disastrous plague killed roughly a quarter of Athens's population, including Pericles. An expedition to Sicily in 415-413 BCE, championed by Alcibiades (Socrates's most famous pupil), ended in the near-total destruction of the Athenian force. Alcibiades defected to Sparta, then to Persia, then back to Athens, then was murdered in exile. Athens surrendered in 404 BCE. The Thirty Tyrants (oligarchs backed by Sparta) seized power for eight months of terror; Critias, another of Socrates's former associates, was their most violent leader.

Democracy was restored in 403 BCE. It was in this atmosphere — traumatized, paranoid, newly restored, haunted by two of Socrates's associates having brought such catastrophe — that Socrates was tried in 399 BCE.

## Life Overview

### Early Life and Formation (c. 470–440 BCE, ages 0–30)

**Origins**

Socrates was born to Sophroniscus, a stonemason or sculptor, and Phaenarete, a midwife. He was not born into poverty but also not into wealth. His mother's profession was important: Socrates would famously describe his own philosophical method as a kind of intellectual midwifery — he did not birth ideas himself but helped others birth the ideas already inside them. This is not merely a metaphor. It is a full account of how he thought knowledge worked.

We know almost nothing about his childhood. He was an Athenian citizen by birth. He received the standard Athenian education: reading, writing, music, gymnastics. He seems to have learned the stonemason's trade from his father, and ancient tradition credits him with a carved group of the Three Graces on the road to the Acropolis, though this is probably legendary.

Somewhere in his youth or early adulthood he became intensely interested in the natural philosophers — Anaxagoras, Parmenides, Zeno — and was for a time excited by the project of explaining the cosmos through material causes. He grew disillusioned when he found that natural philosophy, however clever, could not answer what he considered the most important question: *how should a human being live?* He turned away from cosmology toward ethics — a turn he describes in Plato's *Phaedo* as the decisive intellectual event of his early life.

**Physical Appearance and Personal Manner**

Socrates was famously ugly. Ancient sources consistently describe him as bald, with bulging eyes, a wide flat nose like a satyr's, thick lips, a potbelly, and a shuffling walk. He went barefoot in all seasons, wore the same rough cloak year-round, and ate and drank with remarkable moderation — except that he was also capable of drinking enormous quantities of wine without visible effect, a detail Alcibiades reports in Plato's *Symposium* with something between admiration and bafflement.

This appearance was not incidental to his philosophical project. Socrates seems to have understood and cultivated the contrast between his outward ugliness and the beauty he claimed to pursue — the beauty of well-ordered souls and true understanding. He also seems to have understood that his refusal to look the part of a respectable Athenian was itself a teaching: real worth is not visible. You have to look inside.

### Military Service (c. 432–422 BCE, ages ~38–48)

Socrates served as a hoplite (heavy infantry soldier) in three campaigns during the Peloponnesian War, and he served with extraordinary distinction.

**Potidaea (432–430 BCE):** Socrates's first recorded campaign. He served alongside the young Alcibiades, who was then a teenager. During this campaign, Socrates stood motionless in one spot from dawn to dawn — twenty-four hours — absorbed in a philosophical problem, drawing a crowd of soldiers who set up their bedrolls to watch him and see if he would move. He finally moved the next morning, offered a prayer to the sun, and walked on. This was not performance; it was the complete absorption of an active mind. He also saved Alcibiades's life at Potidaea when Alcibiades was wounded — took the young man under his arm and carried him off the field.

**Delium (424 BCE):** The Athenians were routed. Socrates was in the infantry retreating under pressure. Alcibiades — now a cavalry officer — reports in Plato's *Symposium* that Socrates retreated with total composure, glancing calmly around, so unafraid that even the enemy soldiers were reluctant to attack him. The image Alcibiades uses is a comparison to Aristophanes's comic portrayal of Socrates, adjusted: on the real battlefield, "walking like a pelican," looking "lordly" and making it clear he would not be hurried and would not be alarmed.

**Amphipolis (422 BCE):** Less detailed records, but Socrates served honorably.

This military service is important. It establishes beyond doubt that the courage Socrates demonstrated at his trial — when he refused every compromise that might have saved his life — was not new. He had been courageous in the field when he was a young man. His equanimity in the face of death at seventy-one was the same equanimity he had demonstrated in retreat at Delium at forty-eight.

### The Oracle and the Mission (c. 430–399 BCE, ages ~40–71)

**The Delphic Oracle**

The story Socrates tells in his defense (*Apology*) is this: his friend Chaerephon went to the Oracle at Delphi and asked whether anyone was wiser than Socrates. The Oracle said no.

Socrates was puzzled. He knew he was not wise. So he set out to find someone wiser — and thereby refute the Oracle. He went first to the politicians. They seemed wise, but when questioned, they could not give an account of what they claimed to know. They had confident opinions without real understanding. He moved to the poets. The poets could produce beautiful work, but they could not explain why their work was good or what it meant; they worked by divine inspiration, not by knowledge, and they thought their facility with words meant they understood everything — which they did not. He moved to the craftsmen. The craftsmen were better: they genuinely knew their crafts. But they too believed that because they could make good pots, they therefore understood virtue and beauty and justice — which they did not.

After this survey, Socrates concluded that the Oracle was right, but for an unexpected reason: *he was wiser than the others because he knew what he didn't know, while they didn't know what they didn't know.* His ignorance was conscious. Theirs was invisible to them.

This became his mission: to help the Athenians see the limits of their own knowledge — not as a destructive act but as a pious one. The god had appointed him to this task. He would go on questioning until he died.

**The Daemon**

Socrates reports a private inner sign — a *daimonion*, or daemon — that he had heard from childhood. It never told him what to do; it only told him when *not* to do something, a kind of internal veto. On the morning of his trial, the daemon said nothing — which Socrates interpreted as evidence that his death was not an evil.

The daemon is philosophically interesting for several reasons. It shows Socrates did not believe himself to be purely rational — he took seriously an interior signal that was not the product of argument. It also shows his relationship to piety: he believed, apparently sincerely, that he had a divine mission, and the daemon was part of the evidence for it. Whether this was metaphorical, psychological, or literal is a question that exercised ancient commentators and has not been settled since.

**The Daily Practice**

Socrates had no school. He owned no building. He charged no fees. He met people wherever they were — the agora (marketplace), the gymnasium, a craftsman's shop, a wealthy Athenian's dinner table — and he talked to them. The conversation always followed the same basic structure: someone made a confident claim about something important (what is courage? what is piety? what is justice? what is beauty?), and Socrates began asking questions about it. Within a few exchanges, the confident claim would be exposed as confused, self-contradictory, or dependent on assumptions the speaker had never examined. The interlocutor would be left in a state of *aporia* — a Greek word meaning "no way through," the philosophical impasse of realizing you don't know something you thought you knew.

This was not always welcome. Many people found it humiliating. The young men who watched — and there were always young men watching — found it delightful. To watch a pompous general be exposed as unable to define "courage" was enormously entertaining, especially if you were sixteen and brilliant and ambitious, as Alcibiades was.

**Marriage and Family**

Socrates married Xanthippe, who by ancient report had a formidable temper and found her philosopher husband's complete neglect of income a source of reasonable marital friction. They had three sons: Lamprocles, Sophroniscus, and Menexenus. Socrates was not a model of domestic attentiveness; he spent his days in philosophical conversation, not in providing. Xanthippe has been treated harshly by tradition, but it is worth noting: she raised three children while her husband wandered Athens arguing about justice. Whatever her temper, her grievances were legitimate.

### The Trial (399 BCE, age ~71)

**The Charges**

In 399 BCE, three Athenians — Meletus (a poet), Anytus (a wealthy politician), and Lycon (an orator) — brought charges against Socrates before an Athenian jury of 500 citizens. The formal charges were: (1) impiety, specifically for not honoring the gods the city honored and for introducing new divine beings, and (2) corrupting the youth.

The charges were serious. They were also, almost certainly, politically motivated. Anytus had been a leading figure in the restoration of democracy and was associated with the prosecution of oligarchs after the Thirty Tyrants. Critias — the most violent of the Thirty Tyrants, who had killed some 1,500 Athenians in eight months — had been associated with Socrates. Alcibiades — the brilliant, reckless, traitorous general whose strategic catastrophes had done much to lose the war — had also been associated with Socrates. The restored democracy had passed an amnesty for actions during the tyranny (you couldn't prosecute someone for what they'd done under the Thirty), but there was no amnesty for having produced the men who had done those things.

**The Defense**

Socrates gave a defense recorded (perhaps freely) by Plato as the *Apology*. It is one of the most remarkable speeches in Western history, and it is notable primarily for what Socrates refused to do. He did not grovel. He did not weep. He did not bring his wife and children before the jury in tears (a standard Athenian courtroom tactic). He did not offer to go into exile, a compromise that would almost certainly have saved his life.

He argued that his philosophical practice was a pious service to Athens, appointed by Apollo through the Oracle. He argued that the men who thought they knew what they didn't know were the real danger to the city, not the man who spent his life exposing that ignorance. He argued that he could not stop doing philosophy even if threatened with death, because to stop would be to disobey the god. When the jury voted to convict (280–220, a relatively close vote), and the penalty was left for Socrates to propose, he initially suggested the city give him free meals for life — the honor given to Olympic champions — since that is what his service merited. Under pressure from friends, he eventually proposed a substantial fine. He did not propose exile.

The jury sentenced him to death, by a larger margin than the conviction.

**The Waiting**

Athens had sent its sacred ship to Delos on a religious mission; executions were not permitted until it returned. This left Socrates in prison for approximately thirty days. His friend Crito came to him in prison with a fully arranged plan for escape — bribed guards, a safe house in Thessaly, an argument that Socrates owed his family and friends the duty to live. In Plato's dialogue *Crito*, Socrates dismantles the escape plan methodically. He refuses to violate the laws of Athens even when those laws are unjust in their application to him, because he has lived his whole life under those laws and implicitly agreed to abide by them. He also argues that doing wrong in response to wrong is never justified: the soul must not be corrupted, and fleeing would corrupt it.

**The Death**

On the day the ship returned, Socrates spent his last hours in philosophical conversation — arguing in Plato's *Phaedo* that the soul is immortal and that death is therefore not an evil for the philosopher. He bathed, spent time with his weeping family, sent them away, and then accepted the cup of hemlock from the executioner with a composure that, according to Plato's account, broke the composure of everyone in the room except Socrates himself.

He drank it. He walked around the room until his legs grew heavy. He lay down. As his body grew cold, he said his last words: *"Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius. Pay it and don't forget."* (A cock was the offering given when someone recovered from illness; the conventional reading is that Socrates saw death as recovery from the disease of life.)

Then he was still.

He was approximately seventy-one years old.

## The Philosophical Core

### The Socratic Method (Elenchus)

The word *elenchus* means "cross-examination" or "refutation." Socrates's method involved taking a confident claim — "courage is standing your ground in battle" — and asking a series of apparently simple follow-up questions until the claim collapsed or required substantial revision.

The method is not merely negative. It does two things: it exposes false certainty (which Socrates thought was the root of most human wickedness — people do wrong because they wrongly believe wrong things are good), and it drives the interlocutor toward genuine self-examination. The person who is left in *aporia* at the end of a Socratic conversation is not in a worse position than before; they are in a better position because their false certainty has been replaced by honest ignorance, from which genuine inquiry can begin.

The method also makes a deep claim about how knowledge works: it cannot be poured from one person into another. A teacher cannot give a student the answer to "what is virtue?" because the answer must be *discovered*, not received. The teacher's job is to create the conditions — the questions — in which the student discovers what they already, in some sense, know. This is why Socrates called himself a midwife: he did not birth anything; he helped the birthing along.

### Virtue and Knowledge

Socrates held what seems to most people a strange view: that virtue is a form of knowledge, and that no one does wrong willingly. If you truly *know* what is good, you will do it; if you do something bad, it is because you are confused about what is actually good. Ignorance is the root of vice; knowledge is the root of virtue.

This view has been criticized from antiquity onward (Aristotle thought it failed to account for akrasia — weakness of will, knowing the right thing and doing the wrong thing anyway), but it has a harder logic than it first appears. Socrates's position is not that people never make mistakes; it is that all mistakes are, at bottom, cognitive: you are mistaken about what is good. The cure is not willpower but clarity — better thinking about what truly matters.

This is why his method — questioning, exposing false beliefs — was for him an act of deep moral service. He was not just intellectually interesting; he was, as he saw it, genuinely helping people by showing them the errors in their understanding.

### The Examined Life

The most famous line attributed to Socrates: *"The unexamined life is not worth living"* (*Apology* 38a). This appears in his defense speech as the reason he cannot stop doing philosophy even under threat of death. It means what it says: a life in which you never stop to ask whether what you believe is true, whether what you value is worth valuing, whether the way you live is actually the way you should live — that life may be biologically alive, but it is not a fully human life. Consciousness, for Socrates, carries a responsibility: use it.

### Piety

The charge against Socrates included impiety. He took the charge seriously, which is one reason his defense is interesting. He argued that what appeared to be impiety — his questioning, his daemon, his refusal to defer to conventional religious authority — was in fact the deepest piety. To take the Oracle seriously was to follow its instruction even when it was costly. To have a daemon was to have a direct relationship with the divine, not to reject it. He was not impious; he was pious in a way the city couldn't recognize.

Whether you believe him or not, this is coherent. Socrates is not an atheist and not a skeptic in the modern sense. He took the divine seriously. He just refused to let conventional religion be a substitute for genuine thinking.

## Socrates and the Students He Made (and Ruined)

### Plato

The clearest success. Plato was approximately twenty-nine when Socrates died. He had been a follower since his late teens. The effect of Socrates on Plato was total and lifelong: Plato's entire philosophical corpus is, in the most literal sense, Socrates speaking. Plato rarely writes in his own voice; he gives Socrates the voice.

What Plato took from Socrates: the method, the conviction that philosophy is a way of life not just a subject, the belief that genuine knowledge of the good would produce good action, and — crucially — the dramatic fact of the death. The trial and execution of Socrates shaped Plato's political philosophy profoundly: it gave him an extreme distrust of democracy, a conviction that the crowd would always destroy the philosopher-king, and a determination to build an institution (the Academy) that might produce philosopher-kings who could be protected.

### Xenophon

Another significant follower, Xenophon wrote his own *Memorabilia* (memoirs of Socrates) and *Symposium*. Xenophon's Socrates is somewhat less philosophically startling than Plato's — more practical, more interested in household management and military virtue — but Xenophon was not a philosopher; he was a general and historian, and his Socrates is the portrait of how a practical, honorable man heard the philosophical conversations. Both portraits are useful for triangulating who Socrates actually was.

### Alcibiades

The catastrophic case. Alcibiades was the most gifted, beautiful, and ultimately destructive politician in Athenian history. He was clearly deeply attached to Socrates — his speech in Plato's *Symposium* is one of the most remarkable confessions in ancient literature: he admits that Socrates is the only person who has made him feel ashamed of himself, and that he has repeatedly fled back to the crowd because he cannot face what Socrates's presence demands of him. He knew what was right. He could not bring himself to do it. He chose popularity, ambition, and glory instead.

The question of Socrates's responsibility for Alcibiades is genuinely difficult. The prosecution at Socrates's trial clearly had Alcibiades and Critias in mind even if they could not name them (the amnesty prevented it). Socrates had spent significant time with both. Did he corrupt them, or did he fail to form them, or were they simply, as Plato might say, the wrong material for philosophical formation?

The most honest answer: Socrates's method could reveal the soul's condition; it could not repair it. Alcibiades knew perfectly well what he was doing. He chose it anyway. You can lead a man to the question, but you cannot make him answer it honestly.

### Critias

Less sympathetic than Alcibiades. Critias became the most violent leader of the Thirty Tyrants, responsible for mass killings and property confiscations. He had been part of Socrates's circle in youth. That a man who had spent time in Socratic conversation could become a murderous oligarch is either the strongest argument against Socrates's method or the strongest argument for the position that the method only works on souls that are, at bottom, oriented toward truth. Critias was not.

## Why He Matters for Formation

### The Anti-Curriculum

Socrates provides the most important corrective in the Nova Schola sequence to a potential misunderstanding of the whole project. The sequence studies great men. It studies their achievements, their methods, their disciplines. The risk of such a study — explicitly addressed in the Leonardo profile — is that greatness becomes a product to be replicated by following the right steps.

Socrates refuses this. He insists that no one can be given knowledge; it must be drawn out. He insists that the most important work is interior. He insists that the examination of one's own assumptions is prior to any other kind of education. He is the figure in the sequence who says: *before you study what these men did, you need to know whether you have examined what you believe about why any of it matters.*

### The Question Before the Answer

The Socratic position is that asking good questions is more important than having good answers. In a child's formation, this means something specific: the child who has been taught to ask "is this true, and how do I know?" about every proposition they encounter — including propositions in this curriculum — is more deeply formed than the child who has correctly memorized a thousand facts about great men. The facts matter. But the habit of questioning them matters more.

### Consistency Between Word and Deed

Socrates died for his philosophy. Not metaphorically — he literally chose death over the abandonment of his practice. This is the ultimate formation challenge: do you believe what you claim to believe enough to act on it when the cost is real? Socrates at seventy-one is the same Socrates who stood motionless at Potidaea and retreated calmly at Delium. The character that was formed over decades was the same character that drank the hemlock. Formation, done rightly, goes all the way down.

### The Limits of Transmission

Alcibiades and Critias are the permanent cautionary note in any curriculum about great men and their formation. Not every student becomes Plato. Some become Alcibiades — dazzlingly talented, philosophically aware, morally unable to act on what they know. Some become Critias — exposed to the best, choosing the worst. The teacher cannot control this. The student must choose. The method helps. It does not determine.

## Primary Sources

**Plato** (*Apology*, *Crito*, *Phaedo*, *Symposium*, *Meno*, *Phaedrus*, *Republic* — Socrates appears in nearly all of Plato's dialogues, though scholars distinguish the early "Socratic" dialogues, where Plato likely preserves the historical Socrates most faithfully, from the later dialogues, where Plato uses "Socrates" as a mouthpiece for his own developed views): **Best editions**: G.M.A. Grube translation (Hackett Publishing Company) for reliability; Benjamin Jowett for readability; modern scholarly use of Cooper's complete Plato (Hackett). For formation purposes: begin with the *Apology* and *Crito*; these are the closest to what the historical Socrates actually said. Add the *Symposium* for Alcibiades's confession. Add *Meno* for the teaching problem (can virtue be taught?). Add *Phaedo* for the death scene.

**Xenophon** (*Memorabilia*, *Symposium*, *Apology of Socrates*): A useful corrective to Plato's philosophical Socrates — Xenophon's Socrates is more practical, more conventionally pious, more concerned with daily virtue. Some scholars argue Xenophon is more reliable on who the historical Socrates was; others argue Plato understood him more deeply. Both are probably partly right.

**Aristophanes** (*Clouds*): A hostile source — the play mocks Socrates as a "Thinkery" operator, a combination of Sophist and natural philosopher. Socrates mentions it in the *Apology* as having shaped public opinion against him. It is interesting primarily as evidence that even in Socrates's lifetime, educated Athenians were confused about what he was doing and suspicious of it.

**Secondary sources of merit**: Gregory Vlastos, *Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher* (Cambridge, 1991) — still the best analytical treatment. C.D.C. Reeve, *Socrates in the *Apology** (Hackett, 1989). Paul Johnson, *Socrates: A Man for Our Times* (Viking, 2011) — accessible popular biography. Thomas C. Brickhouse and Nicholas D. Smith, *Socrates on Trial* (Princeton, 1989).

## The Formation Signature

**Socrates's gift to students is not content. It is a method of consciousness.**

Where Leonardo gave his students the habit of *noticing*, and Plato gave his students the habit of *ordering*, and Aristotle gave his students the habit of *classifying*, and Marcus gave his students the habit of *practicing*, Socrates gives his students the habit of *questioning*. Not the habit of doubting everything (which leads to paralysis) but the habit of examining the grounds for what you believe before you act on it — and then being willing to revise.

The formation signature is this: **a student formed in the Socratic tradition is a student who, before accepting any proposition, asks: How do I know this? What would I have to believe for this to be true? Have I examined this, or have I merely received it?**

This habit, once formed, cannot be unformed. It is also, as Athens discovered, inconvenient. The examined life produces people who are harder to govern by authority alone, harder to manipulate by convention, harder to silence by social pressure. That is its value. That is also why Athens killed the man who taught it.

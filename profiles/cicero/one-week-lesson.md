# Cicero: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Cicero in a home or small-group setting. It is designed for approximately **45-75 minutes per day**, adaptable by age. The plan is calibrated primarily for **ages 8-14**, with extensions for **ages 15-18** throughout.

The week moves through this sequence:

**The Outsider Who Built Himself → Speech as Justice → Catiline and Emergency Politics → Duty and the Moral Life → The Last Voice of the Republic**

That sequence matters. Cicero should not first be presented as a marble bust or a collection of quotations. He should be encountered as a formed human being whose education made possible a certain kind of public action — and whose age tested that formation almost to destruction.

The week's central question is:

**What are words for?**

Cicero's answer: words are for truth, law, persuasion toward the good, and the preservation of common life.

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for each student — this week's notebook is the **Civic Speech Journal**
- A map of the Roman Republic around the first century BCE
- Optional printed excerpts from:
  - the First Catilinarian
  - *On Duties* 1.22 and nearby sections
  - *On the Orator* Book I
- Colored pencils or markers for younger students to map Rome's institutions or draw scenes

### Choose Your Age Band

Select the student lesson appropriate to your child:
- [Ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md)
- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

### The Civic Speech Journal Setup

Each day has two entries:
1. **Observation:** What public issue, conflict, or duty did we discuss today?
2. **Articulation:** Explain it clearly in 4-8 sentences. What is happening? What is right? What should be done?

This is the week's core practice. Cicero is learned not only by reading about him but by practicing his central conviction that thought must be put into ordered speech.

---

## Monday: The Outsider Who Built Himself

### Morning: Story and Discussion (25-35 min)

Introduce Cicero's background:
- born at Arpinum, not Rome
- not from the old ruling nobility
- intensely educated in law, rhetoric, and philosophy
- rose through public office by talent and discipline

**Key emphasis:** Cicero did not inherit authority. He built it.

**Questions to ask:**
- What disadvantages did Cicero begin with?
- What did he do about them?
- Is hard study a real source of strength, or just schoolwork?

**For ages 15-18:** introduce the term *novus homo* and discuss outsider ambition. How can insecurity both help and harm greatness?

### Afternoon: Formation Exercise (20-30 min)

**Journal prompt:**
Write about one area where you do not have an easy advantage. What could disciplined formation do there over time?

**Practical exercise:**
Have the student explain, in one paragraph, why education mattered for Cicero's rise. Require clarity rather than length.

---

## Tuesday: Speech Used for Justice — The Verres Case

### Morning: The Case (25-35 min)

Tell the story of Verres:
- corrupt governor of Sicily
- abuse of power and plunder
- Cicero gathered evidence and prosecuted him
- Verres fled rather than endure the full force of the case

**Key emphasis:** rhetoric is at its best when tied to truth and justice.

**Questions to ask:**
- Why is persuasive speech dangerous if not tied to facts?
- Why was it important that Cicero prepared carefully instead of just speaking dramatically?
- What is the difference between exposing corruption and merely attacking someone?

**For ages 8-10:** simplify to "Cicero used his words to protect people from a dishonest ruler."

### Afternoon: Evidence + Speech Exercise (20-30 min)

Choose a small household or school issue: chores, fairness, screen time, shared space, group rules.

Ask the student to make a short case using:
- **2 facts**
- **1 principle** (fairness, honesty, responsibility, etc.)
- **1 clear recommendation**

This models Ciceronian preparation: facts first, then principle, then persuasion.

---

## Wednesday: Catiline — Can You Save a Republic and Hurt It at the Same Time?

### Morning: Crisis Discussion (30-40 min)

Explain Catiline's conspiracy in age-appropriate terms:
- failed politician
- secret plot and violence
- Cicero exposed the danger
- conspirators were executed without trial

This is the week's hardest day.

**Key emphasis:** emergency politics creates moral pressure.

**Questions to ask:**
- What should leaders do when danger is immediate?
- Are there rules that should never be broken, even in emergencies?
- If you break a law to save the system, what happens to the system?

**For ages 8-10:** keep it simple: "Cicero stopped a dangerous plot, but some people later said he acted too harshly."

**For ages 15-18:** require both sides of the argument. Do not let the student settle too quickly.

### Afternoon: Structured Debate (20-30 min)

Prompt:
**"Cicero was justified in what he did during the Catiline crisis."**

Have the student either defend or oppose the statement.

Requirements:
- state the position clearly
- give two reasons
- acknowledge one objection
- explain why the objection does or does not change the conclusion

---

## Thursday: Duty — We Are Not Born for Ourselves Alone

### Morning: Reading and Reflection (25-35 min)

Read and discuss:
> *"We are not born for ourselves alone."*

Unpack it slowly.

Questions:
- What does it mean to belong partly to other people?
- What do we owe family, friends, neighbors, classmates, community?
- Is duty the same as doing what you feel like doing?

Connect this to *On Duties*: Cicero's belief that honor and real usefulness belong together.

**For older students:** introduce the distinction between the honorable and the merely advantageous.

### Afternoon: Duty Inventory (20-30 min)

Have the student list three duties:
- one to self
- one to family
- one to community

Then ask them to choose one and act on it today.

**Journal prompt:**
What is one duty I usually avoid because it is inconvenient?

---

## Friday: The Last Voice of the Republic

### Morning: The Final Stand (30-40 min)

Tell the end of the story:
- Caesar assassinated
- Cicero hoped the republic might revive
- he attacked Antony in the *Philippics*
- Antony, Octavian, and Lepidus took power
- Cicero was killed and displayed on the rostra

**Key emphasis:** duty does not guarantee victory.

Questions:
- Was Cicero foolish, brave, or both?
- What can a person still do when the political world is going bad?
- Can writing and speaking preserve things that politics fails to protect?

**For ages 15-18:** discuss whether Cicero's greatest achievement was political or civilizational.

### Afternoon: Final Synthesis Exercise (20-30 min)

Ask the student to complete one of these:
- **Ages 8-10:** Draw Cicero speaking in the Senate and write 3 sentences about what words are for.
- **Ages 11-14:** Write one paragraph answering: *Why does a republic need truthful speakers?*
- **Ages 15-18:** Write a one-page response to: *What does Cicero teach about duty in a declining regime?*

---

## Extension Ideas

### For younger students
- Make a "truthful speech" badge or poster
- Practice saying hard things kindly and clearly
- Memorize one short Cicero quotation

### For older students
- Compare Cicero with Isocrates on rhetoric and civic formation
- Read a short excerpt from *On Duties* and apply it to a contemporary issue
- Compare the *Philippics* with Demosthenes or Churchill

---

## End-of-Week Questions for Parents

At the end of the week, ask:
- Is my child more careful about words?
- Is my child more alert to the difference between clarity and manipulation?
- Has my child thought more seriously about duty beyond self-interest?
- Has my child practiced turning thought into ordered speech?

If yes, the week has done its work.

---

## Final Note

Cicero week should leave students with a stronger sense that language is morally serious.

A child who learns this early gains a major protection against becoming either a manipulator or a passive spectator. Cicero's central lesson is that the good society needs people who can think clearly, speak courageously, and act under duty.

That remains true.

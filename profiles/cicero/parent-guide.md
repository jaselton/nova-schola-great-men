# Cicero: Parent Teaching Guide

---

## Overview: What This Profile Teaches

Cicero is one of the most important figures in the Nova Schola sequence because he joins together several themes that have been building since Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, and Isocrates:
- the relationship between thought and speech
- the question of whether philosophy should remain contemplative or become civic
- the role of education in forming statesmen rather than merely literate people
- the difficulty of acting honorably inside political decline

With Cicero, these themes move from Greek theory into Roman crisis.

He is not just a great speaker. He is the great case study in what public speech is for when a republic is under pressure.

This profile teaches five major things.

### 1. Eloquence is a moral instrument
Cicero refuses to treat speaking as a neutral skill. The same verbal power can either defend justice or protect corruption. Your child should come away seeing that articulate speech carries responsibility.

### 2. Education must unite breadth, judgment, and expression
Cicero's ideal orator is not a clever debater. He is a broadly formed human being — trained in law, history, philosophy, ethics, and human nature — who can then bring all of that to bear in public speech. This is one of the clearest classical arguments against narrow, purely technical education.

### 3. Duty is not the same as success
Cicero could not save the Roman Republic. This makes him extremely valuable pedagogically. Many "great man" stories can tempt children into a simplistic equation: virtue + courage = victory. Cicero breaks that equation. He shows that one may act nobly and still lose. This is a harder and more mature formation lesson.

### 4. Political decline tests character differently than peace does
Ordinary civic virtues operate differently when institutions are stable. In a collapsing order, law, loyalty, prudence, courage, speech, and compromise all become more difficult to judge. Cicero's life gives older students a serious introduction to this problem.

### 5. Books can preserve a civilization
When Cicero lost political power, he did not stop serving. He wrote. He translated Greek philosophy into Latin, articulated Roman republican ideals, and left behind texts that nourished later centuries. This teaches that intellectual labor is not secondary to history; sometimes it is one of the ways history survives.

## Age-Appropriate Emphases

### Ages 8-10: Words Should Help, Not Trick
At this age, focus on the simple moral clarity of Cicero's story.

**Teach:**
- He worked hard to become an excellent speaker
- He used words to defend people and expose wrongdoing
- He believed we are not born only for ourselves
- He kept speaking for what he thought was right even when it became dangerous

**Key practice:** ask the child to distinguish between words that clarify and words that manipulate.

**Do not overcomplicate:**
- Roman factional politics
- the full constitutional issue around Catiline
- philosophical school distinctions

The goal here is formation in speech: truthful, brave, helpful language.

### Ages 11-14: The Public Meaning of Speech
This is the ideal age to show why Cicero thought rhetoric required moral and intellectual depth.

**Teach:**
- the *novus homo* story: outsider strength and outsider insecurity
- Verres as a case of speech used for justice
- Catiline as a case of emergency politics
- why Cicero thought a real speaker needed history, law, and philosophy
- the idea that duty can cost something real

**Key practice:** have students prepare short arguments with facts, principles, and objections — not just opinions.

### Ages 15-18: Statesmanship Under Regime Failure
At this level Cicero becomes one of the richest profiles in the sequence.

**Teach:**
- the constitutional problem of emergency action
- the limits of eloquence in an age dominated by armies and personal rule
- Cicero's philosophical project as civilizational preservation
- the *Philippics* as both magnificent and politically doomed
- *On Duties* as practical moral philosophy under collapse

**Key practice:** comparative constitutional reasoning. Ask the student to weigh legality, necessity, prudence, and honor in concrete cases.

## The Central Teaching Tension

The biggest tension in teaching Cicero is this:

**He was right about many essentials and still lost.**

Parents often want clean examples. Cicero is not clean.

He can be vain. He overestimates his own influence. He makes morally ambiguous decisions in the Catiline affair. He misjudges Octavian. And yet he remains one of the noblest public men in the classical world.

That complexity is not a bug. It is the lesson.

Use Cicero to teach your child that greatness is not the absence of flaw, and moral seriousness is not the same as infallibility. He is a major figure precisely because he combined genuine virtue, major gifts, public courage, and unmistakable limitations.

---

## The Hardest Question: Was Cicero Right to Execute the Conspirators?

If your child is old enough, do not dodge this.

Cicero uncovered a genuine conspiracy. Rome was in danger. Several captured conspirators were executed without trial.

**One side:** emergency required action; delay might have meant catastrophe.

**The other side:** once the defender of legality suspends legality, the republic is damaged even while being defended.

This question is pedagogically valuable because it prevents hero worship from becoming intellectual laziness.

You do not need to force a single answer. You do need to make clear that serious statesmanship involves tragic choices, not just obvious ones.

## Practical Teaching Moves for the Week

### 1. Make speech concrete
Every day, ask your child to explain one issue clearly: what happened, what is just, what duties are involved, what should be done. This applies Cicero immediately.

### 2. Link eloquence to responsibility
Do not praise verbal smoothness by itself. Praise clarity, fairness, proportion, and truthfulness.

### 3. Keep asking, "Who is this helping?"
Cicero's standard is civic. Language should serve persons and communities, not just the speaker's self-image.

### 4. Distinguish courage from noise
The brave speaker is not simply the loudest one. Courageous speech is measured by whether it says what is true and necessary when pressure makes silence attractive.

### 5. Let older students sit with failure
Resist the urge to rescue Cicero from tragedy. He did not rescue himself from it. Let your student feel the cost of trying to preserve something good in a bad age.

## Connections to the Sequence

**Backward:**
- **Socrates** — seriousness about truth and conscience
- **Plato** — the philosophical demands behind public reasoning
- **Aristotle** — practical judgment and rhetoric taken seriously as a civic art
- **Isocrates** — the clearest direct predecessor; education as formation for public speech and action

**Forward:**
- **Quintilian** — turns Cicero's ideal into a full educational system
- **Augustine** — deeply influenced by Cicero; his lost *Hortensius* helped turn Augustine toward philosophy
- **Erasmus** — Ciceronian humanism revived in Christian humanist form
- **Churchill** — another figure whose words functioned as instruments of civilizational defense

## Suggested Primary Sources

For family use, short excerpts are enough.

- **First Catilinarian** — opening paragraphs
- **On Duties** 1.22 — "We are not born for ourselves alone"
- **On the Orator** Book I — selections on the complete speaker
- **Philippics** — selected passages for older students

---

## A Final Note for Parents

Cicero is a particularly useful figure if you want your children to understand that intelligence and verbal ability are not toys.

A clever child can become manipulative. A verbally gifted child can become vain. A morally earnest child can become politically naive. Cicero's life addresses all three dangers.

At his best, he shows what a human being looks like when mind, language, and duty are integrated. At his worst, he shows how vanity and misjudgment can deform even a serious man.

That combination makes him one of the most educationally valuable figures in the entire project.

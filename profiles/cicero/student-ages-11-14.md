# Cicero: Student Lesson (Ages 11-14)
## Eloquence, Duty, and the Fight to Save a Republic

---

## Why Cicero Is a Major Figure

Cicero matters because he stands at the point where rhetoric, philosophy, law, and politics all meet.

He was not just Rome's greatest speaker. He was also one of the most serious thinkers about what speech is *for*. For Cicero, eloquence was not verbal decoration and not merely a competitive advantage. It was one of the chief tools by which a republic could govern itself.

That claim matters because republics do not run mainly by force. They run by argument, persuasion, law, memory, and public trust. If citizens and leaders cannot think clearly or speak clearly, then public life starts to fill with manipulation, slogans, fear, and private ambition.

Cicero lived during exactly that kind of breakdown.

His life asks a difficult question: **What does a serious, educated, morally ambitious man do when the political order he loves is collapsing around him?**

---

## The Outsider Who Refused to Stay Outside

Cicero was born in 106 BCE in Arpinum, a town outside Rome. He was not from one of the old aristocratic families that had ruled Roman politics for generations. This made him a *novus homo* — a "new man." That phrase meant more than just "someone new." It meant a man without inherited political authority.

So Cicero had to build authority another way.

He did it through education and discipline. He studied law, rhetoric, philosophy, and history. He trained with major teachers in Rome and Greece. He learned that if he could not inherit greatness, he could construct it.

This gave him one of the deepest formation lessons in the sequence so far:

**social disadvantages can be real without being final.**

But they leave marks. Cicero never forgot that he was an outsider, and that helps explain both his strength and one of his weaknesses. It gave him tremendous drive, but it also fed a kind of vanity. He wanted not only to do great things but to be seen doing them.

---

## Verres: Using Speech Against Corruption

Cicero first became internationally famous by prosecuting Verres, the corrupt former governor of Sicily.

This was more than an ordinary legal case. Provincial governors often exploited their territories, and powerful men usually found ways to escape consequences. Cicero did something different. He built the case carefully, gathered evidence thoroughly, and used his oratory not to obscure the truth but to expose it.

Verres fled before the full proceedings could finish.

This matters because it shows Cicero's best vision of rhetoric:
- rhetoric tied to facts
- rhetoric used for justice
- rhetoric aimed at public moral clarity

In other words, Cicero shows what eloquence looks like when it is under discipline.

## The Consulship and Catiline

In 63 BCE Cicero reached the consulship, the highest office in Rome. This alone would have made him famous. What happened during that year made him immortal.

Lucius Sergius Catilina — Catiline — had failed politically and turned toward conspiracy. He planned violence, insurrection, and seizure of power. Cicero uncovered the plot and delivered the famous speeches now called the **Catilinarians**.

The opening is still one of the great moments in political speech:

> *"How long, O Catiline, will you abuse our patience?"*

The speech was more than dramatic language. It was a public act of exposure. Cicero named the threat, identified the danger, and forced Rome to see what many preferred not to see.

But there is a complication, and it is essential.

Several conspirators were later executed without trial. Cicero believed the emergency justified it. Many Romans agreed. Others believed this action violated the very republican legality Cicero claimed to defend.

So the Catiline episode is not a simple heroic story. It is a case study in the moral danger of emergency politics.

**Question:** if you save the state by stepping outside the law, have you preserved the republic or weakened it?

Cicero's life never escaped that question.

## Why Cicero Thought Eloquence Required More Than Technique

Cicero's educational vision appears most clearly in *On the Orator*.

He rejects the idea that an orator is just someone who knows verbal tricks. The complete speaker, he argues, must know:
- history
- law
- philosophy
- ethics
- human psychology
- the habits of political communities

Why? Because public speech deals with human beings making serious decisions. If you do not understand justice, law, fear, hope, memory, and duty, then your eloquence will eventually become empty.

This makes Cicero the Roman continuation of the Isocratean ideal. Like Isocrates, he insists that rhetoric cannot be separated from moral and intellectual formation.

The true speaker is not a manipulator. He is an educated citizen serving the common good.

At least, that is the ideal.

## Exile, Civil War, and the Turn to Philosophy

Cicero's career after the consulship was unstable and painful.

Because of his role in executing the Catilinarian conspirators, he was exiled in 58 BCE. Later he returned, but Rome was increasingly dominated by extraordinary men with armies: Caesar, Pompey, Crassus, and then later Antony and Octavian.

Cicero kept trying to work through law and constitutional politics in a world that was becoming less and less constitutional.

This is where his story becomes tragic.

He could still speak brilliantly. He could still write brilliantly. But the kind of political order in which speech could guide civic life was disappearing.

So Cicero increasingly wrote philosophy and political theory:
- *On the Republic*
- *On the Laws*
- *On Ends*
- *Tusculan Disputations*
- *On Friendship*
- *On Old Age*
- *On Duties*

These works were not private hobbies. They were an attempt to preserve a moral and civic inheritance that politics could no longer secure.

This is one of Cicero's deepest lessons:

**when institutions weaken, the work of preserving the ideas that made them possible becomes even more important.**

---

## The Philippics and the Fatal Last Stand

After Caesar was assassinated in 44 BCE, Cicero hoped the republic could be restored. He turned against Mark Antony in a series of speeches called the **Philippics**, modeled on Demosthenes's speeches against Philip of Macedon.

The speeches are among the bravest things Cicero ever did.

They are also among the least prudent.

Cicero believed the Senate could use the young Octavian against Antony. He underestimated Octavian completely. Antony, Octavian, and Lepidus formed the Second Triumvirate, issued proscriptions, and marked Cicero for death.

He was killed in 43 BCE.

His head and hands were displayed on the rostra — the platform from which he had addressed Rome.

The symbolism is almost too perfect. Rome destroyed the very voice that had tried to preserve its public reason.

## What Cicero Teaches

### 1. Public speech is a moral act.

Speech is never "just words" in public life. It shapes decisions, laws, fears, loyalties, and memories. Cicero teaches that speaking well is a civic responsibility.

### 2. Education should produce judgment, not just fluency.

A smooth talker is not the same thing as a serious speaker. Cicero insists that language without moral and intellectual depth is dangerous.

### 3. Duty may demand costly action.

Cicero kept speaking when silence would have been safer. Whether every decision he made was wise is debatable. That he believed duty could cost him dearly is not.

### 4. A civilization can be partially saved in books.

Cicero failed politically. He succeeded civilizationally. Later centuries learned republican thought, natural law, rhetoric, and moral obligation partly through him.

## Questions to Work Through

### 1. Did Cicero preserve or damage the republic in the Catiline crisis?
Try to argue both sides fairly.

### 2. Why does Cicero think a great speaker must study philosophy and law?
What goes wrong if rhetoric is taught only as technique?

### 3. Is Cicero tragic because he failed, or admirable because he kept acting even when failure was likely?
Maybe both — but explain why.

### 4. What is the difference between eloquence and manipulation?
Give a real example if you can.

## Things to Do This Week

**1. Read a short excerpt from the First Catilinarian.**  
Ask what makes the speech powerful beyond mere anger.

**2. Define duty.**  
Write one paragraph answering: *What do I owe other people?*

**3. Try Ciceronian preparation.**  
Before speaking on any issue, gather three facts, one principle, and one possible objection. Then speak.

**4. Compare speech styles.**  
Listen to one modern speech and ask: is this speaker aiming at clarity, excitement, fear, or manipulation?

**5. Memorize this:**  
> *"We are not born for ourselves alone."*

Then explain what it would mean if a whole class, family, or country actually believed it.

---

## One Final Provocation

Cicero's life suggests that a republic needs more than laws. It needs citizens and leaders who can think historically, speak truthfully, and act under duty.

If those people disappear, the laws may remain on paper for a while.

But the republic is already mostly gone.

# Cicero
## The Last Great Voice of the Roman Republic

**Born:** January 3, 106 BCE, Arpinum, Latium  
**Died:** December 7, 43 BCE, near Formiae, Italy  
**Era:** Late Roman Republic  
**Domain:** Oratory, law, philosophy, statesmanship

---

## The One-Minute Story

Cicero was not born into Rome's ruling nobility. He came from Arpinum, a provincial town, and had to build his entire public life without the inherited prestige that most Roman politicians relied on. He did it through education, discipline, and the mastery of speech.

He studied law, rhetoric, and philosophy until he became the greatest orator in Rome. He exposed corrupt governors, rose through every public office at the earliest legal age, and became consul in 63 BCE — a nearly unbelievable achievement for a *novus homo*, a "new man" from outside the old governing families.

That same year he uncovered Catiline's conspiracy to overthrow the Roman state. His speeches against Catiline made him famous forever. Rome hailed him as the man who saved the republic.

But the republic he saved was already dying.

Over the next twenty years, Cicero watched constitutional government buckle under ambition, military force, and civil war. He tried to hold it together with law, persuasion, alliances, and finally philosophy. When politics failed, he wrote books — *On the Orator*, *On the Republic*, *On the Laws*, *On Duties* — that preserved Rome's best understanding of civic life for future generations.

After Caesar's assassination, Cicero made one final attempt to stop tyranny by speaking against Mark Antony. He lost. In 43 BCE he was murdered, and his head and hands were nailed to the speaker's platform in Rome.

He could not save the republic. But he became its greatest voice, and one of the chief teachers of the Western tradition on duty, eloquence, and public life.

## Why He Matters for Your Child

**Cicero teaches:**

- That words are tools of civilization, not ornaments for self-display
- That speaking well requires more than technique — it requires moral seriousness, judgment, and knowledge
- That public courage often costs something real
- That even when institutions are failing, it still matters to preserve what is true, honorable, and worth handing on
- That education should prepare a person not just to succeed privately but to serve the common good

**The formation challenge Cicero poses:**
Can your child learn to use language as an instrument of truth, clarity, and service rather than manipulation, vanity, or performance? Cicero's life insists that eloquence without character is dangerous, but character without the ability to articulate and defend it is politically weak. The formed person needs both.

## In the Nova Schola Sequence

Cicero follows Isocrates because he is, in many ways, the Roman fulfillment of the Isocratean educational ideal. Isocrates argued that education should form good men capable of speaking effectively for the common good. Cicero inherits that vision, expands it with Roman law and philosophy, and lives it under conditions of acute political breakdown.

Connections:
- **To Isocrates:** Cicero is the great Roman heir of the rhetorical-humanistic tradition. He agrees that the ideal speaker must be broadly educated, morally serious, and politically active.
- **To Socrates and Plato:** Cicero takes philosophy seriously, but refuses to leave it in the school. He insists that wisdom must enter the courts, the senate, and the civic order.
- **Forward to Quintilian:** Quintilian will turn Cicero's ideal into an educational system. The famous Roman formula — *the good man skilled in speaking* — is Ciceronian through and through.

## Formation Signature

> *"We are not born for ourselves alone."*  
> — Cicero, *On Duties* 1.22

**Core practice:** Civic articulation — choose one issue that affects other people, not just yourself, and explain it clearly in speech or writing: what is happening, what is just, what duties are involved, and what should be done. This trains the Ciceronian habit of connecting thought, language, and public responsibility.

**The irreducible lesson:** Civilization depends partly on whether enough people can speak truthfully, courageously, and persuasively for the common good. Cicero teaches that eloquence is not mainly a career advantage. It is a public trust.

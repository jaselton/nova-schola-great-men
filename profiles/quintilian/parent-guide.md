# Quintilian: Parent Teaching Guide

---

## Overview: What This Profile Teaches

Quintilian is one of the most important educational figures in the entire Nova Schola sequence because he turns an ideal into a formation method.

Socrates teaches seriousness about truth. Plato teaches the architecture of the soul and the city. Aristotle teaches practical judgment and rhetorical understanding. Isocrates teaches civic formation through speech. Cicero embodies the noble Roman orator in public life.

Quintilian then asks the practical parental question:

**How do you actually raise such a person?**

His answer is one of the great achievements of classical pedagogy. He insists that eloquence must be formed from early childhood through habits, models, reading, discipline, memory, affection, and moral seriousness. He is therefore not merely a rhetorician. He is one of the founders of humane education.

This profile teaches five major things.

### 1. Formation begins before formal instruction
Quintilian starts early because he knows that the child is already being educated by tone, example, language, imitation, and expectation long before advanced subjects appear. Parents should feel this immediately: the home is not preparation for education; it is already the first school.

### 2. Speech and character cannot be separated
Quintilian's defining conviction is that the true speaker must be a good man skilled in speaking. For parents, this means verbal ability should never be praised in isolation. A child who can argue, impress, or charm without moral seriousness is not well formed. He is merely more dangerous.

### 3. Humane authority is stronger than cruelty
Quintilian is remarkably sane about discipline. He is not permissive. He expects effort, correction, and serious standards. But he knows harshness can humiliate, harden, or distort a child. He opposes routine corporal punishment and favors a teacherly authority that combines firmness with affection and discernment.

### 4. Models matter immensely
Children imitate constantly. Quintilian understands this at a deep level. They imitate teachers, parents, siblings, books, entertainers, classmates, and now screens. This makes the question of educational atmosphere decisive. Your child will imitate someone. The practical task is to make the worthy models more available and more admirable than the corrupt ones.

### 5. Education aims at honorable usefulness
Quintilian does not want articulate children merely because articulate children appear impressive. He wants formed human beings who can someday use language responsibly in the service of truth, justice, and public life. This rescues education from both vanity and narrow careerism.

## Age-Appropriate Emphases

### Ages 8-10: Good Words from Good Hearts
At this age, keep the lesson simple and concrete.

**Teach:**
- words are powerful
- good speakers should be good people
- children become like what they imitate
- strong teachers do not have to be cruel
- learning should include both effort and delight

**Key practice:** help the child notice the difference between helpful speech and show-off speech.

**Do not overcomplicate:**
- Roman imperial politics
- the technical divisions of rhetoric
- literary-critical details

The goal is early moral-linguistic formation.

### Ages 11-14: Habits, Models, and Serious Schooling
This is the ideal age to show Quintilian's educational wisdom more directly.

**Teach:**
- why he begins with early childhood
- why teachers matter as examples, not just explainers
- why style without substance is weak
- why imitation is unavoidable
- why discipline and humane treatment belong together

**Key practice:** have students collect and imitate excellent sentences, while also learning to criticize shallow or manipulative language.

### Ages 15-18: Educational Philosophy in a Thinning Culture
Older students can handle the depth of Quintilian's historical position.

**Teach:**
- the ideal of the *vir bonus dicendi peritus*
- rhetoric as public expression of moral formation
- the educational consequences of living after the republic
- the problem of preserving noble standards in degraded conditions
- the difference between system and soul in schooling

**Key practice:** evaluate modern educational practices through a Quintilian lens. What actually forms judgment, taste, courage, and trustworthy speech?

## The Central Teaching Tension

The key tension in teaching Quintilian is this:

**He offers a magnificent educational ideal inside a politically shrunken world.**

He comes after Cicero, not before him. The republic has already failed. The old orator-statesman ideal no longer has the same room to act. So what is Quintilian doing?

He is preserving the type even when the regime no longer fully supports the type.

That is deeply relevant now. Many parents feel they are trying to form children for seriousness inside a culture that rewards distraction, vanity, and performance. Quintilian is an ally precisely because he understands that educational transmission may become more important when public life grows weaker.

## Practical Teaching Moves for the Week

### 1. Audit the speech atmosphere of the home
Ask honestly: what tone dominates your household language? Sarcasm? Hurry? Complaint? Precision? Encouragement? Quintilian would tell you this matters more than many purchased curricula.

### 2. Praise clarity and truth, not just cleverness
When your child speaks well, praise not just fluency but fairness, accuracy, courage, and usefulness.

### 3. Use imitation intentionally
Read excellent prose aloud. Memorize short noble lines. Have your child repeat good sentences with attention to meaning and tone.

### 4. Correct without contempt
Firm correction is necessary. Contempt is corrosive. Quintilian's insight is that the child should experience authority as morally serious and basically trustworthy.

### 5. Connect words to service
Keep asking, "Who is this helping?" Language should not become a private ornament or a weapon for domination.

## Connections to the Sequence

**Backward:**
- **Isocrates** — education for civic life through speech
- **Cicero** — the Roman ideal speaker whom Quintilian constantly honors
- **Aristotle** — rhetoric tied to judgment and human realities rather than mere display

**Forward:**
- **Augustine** — takes classical rhetoric into Christian formation
- **Erasmus** — revives humane educational ideals through language and letters
- **Modern liberal education** — especially wherever character formation remains linked to literary and rhetorical training

## Suggested Primary Sources

For family use, short selections are enough.

- **Institutio Oratoria, Book I** — early education and the treatment of children
- **Institutio Oratoria, Book X** — reading and imitation
- **Institutio Oratoria, Book XII** — the character of the true orator

Even a few paragraphs can be fruitful if discussed slowly.

---

## A Final Note for Parents

Quintilian is especially valuable if you want your child to understand that education is not mainly about information or achievement signals.

It is about becoming the kind of person whose words can be trusted.

That requires more than curriculum. It requires atmosphere, example, correction, memory, and love of what is noble.

Quintilian knew that. That is why he still belongs in any serious conversation about formation.

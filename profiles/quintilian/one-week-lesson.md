# Quintilian: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Quintilian in a home or small-group setting. It is designed for approximately **45-75 minutes per day**, adaptable by age. The plan is calibrated primarily for **ages 8-14**, with extensions for **ages 15-18** throughout.

The week moves through this sequence:

**Words Matter → Formation Starts Early → Teachers and Models → Style vs. Substance → The Good Man Skilled in Speaking**

That sequence matters. Quintilian should not first be taught as a dry Roman schoolmaster. He should be encountered as the thinker who understood that noble public speech must be built over years by means of habit, affection, reading, discipline, and example.

The week's central question is:

**How do you form a person whose words can be trusted?**

Quintilian's answer: by forming the whole person from early on.

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for each student — this week's notebook is the **Formation and Speech Journal**
- A map of the Roman Empire in the first century CE
- Optional printed excerpts from *Institutio Oratoria* Book I, Book X, and Book XII
- A few short passages of excellent prose or speeches for imitation exercises

### Choose Your Age Band

Select the student lesson appropriate to your child:
- [Ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md)
- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

### The Formation and Speech Journal Setup

Each day has two entries:
1. **Observation:** What did I notice today about speech, teaching, or imitation?
2. **Practice:** Write or speak 3-8 sentences that are clear, fair, and useful.

This journal matters because Quintilian is learned not only by reading about him but by practicing attentive, orderly, morally serious language.

---

## Monday: Why Words Need Character

### Morning: Story and Discussion (25-35 min)

Introduce Quintilian:
- born in Roman Spain
- trained in Rome
- became the empire's greatest rhetoric teacher
- wrote a massive work on how to educate a speaker from childhood onward

**Key emphasis:** Quintilian did not think speaking well was enough. He thought the speaker had to be good.

**Questions to ask:**
- What is the difference between sounding impressive and being trustworthy?
- Why would character matter in speech?
- Can a dishonest person still sound persuasive?

**For ages 15-18:** introduce the phrase *vir bonus dicendi peritus* and unpack its order.

### Afternoon: Formation Exercise (20-30 min)

**Journal prompt:**
Describe one person whose words you trust. Why do you trust them?

**Practical exercise:**
Have the student say one important thing as clearly as possible in three sentences. Remove exaggeration and vagueness.

---

## Tuesday: Formation Starts Earlier Than We Think

### Morning: Early Education (25-35 min)

Explain Quintilian's startling decision to begin with early childhood:
- caregivers and language
- memory and play
- atmosphere and affection
- good habits before advanced study

**Key emphasis:** education starts long before formal rhetoric.

**Questions to ask:**
- What shapes children before they can study big ideas?
- Why do habits matter before talent appears?
- What kind of words should fill a home?

**For ages 8-10:** simplify to "Children learn from what they hear every day."

### Afternoon: Atmosphere Audit (20-30 min)

As a family or group, identify:
- 3 kinds of speech you want more of
- 3 kinds of speech you want less of

Examples: patience, clarity, gratitude / sarcasm, shouting, dramatizing.

**Journal prompt:**
What words do I hear often that are shaping me?

---

## Wednesday: Teachers, Models, and Imitation

### Morning: The Power of Example (25-35 min)

Teach Quintilian's view that students imitate constantly.

Discuss:
- parents as models
- teachers as near-parental guides
- books as silent teachers
- friends and media as powerful formers

**Key emphasis:** imitation is unavoidable, so choose models carefully.

**Questions to ask:**
- Who are you imitating right now?
- What makes a teacher worthy of imitation?
- Why is admiration educationally powerful?

**For ages 15-18:** discuss whether modern schooling takes imitation seriously enough.

### Afternoon: Noble Imitation Exercise (20-30 min)

Choose one excellent short paragraph or sentence.

Have the student:
- read it aloud
- copy it by hand
- explain what makes it good
- write one original sentence in a similar spirit

This gives Quintilian's method practical form.

---

## Thursday: Style, Substance, and the Danger of Showiness

### Morning: Corrupt Style Discussion (30-40 min)

Explain Quintilian's criticism of flashy rhetoric:
- verbal fireworks without depth
- cleverness without judgment
- performance without usefulness

**Key emphasis:** style is good when it serves truth and proportion.

**Questions to ask:**
- What makes language shallow even when it sounds exciting?
- Why do people often reward showiness?
- How can you tell when style is helping substance rather than replacing it?

**For ages 8-10:** simplify to "Some words sparkle but do not help."

### Afternoon: Compare and Revise (20-30 min)

Give the student two short passages, or create two versions of the same idea:
- one dramatic and exaggerated
- one clear and serious

Ask which is better and why.

Then rewrite the weaker one.

---

## Friday: The Good Man Skilled in Speaking

### Morning: Synthesis Discussion (30-40 min)

Bring the whole week together.

Review:
- speech needs character
- education begins early
- teachers and models matter
- style should serve substance
- the goal is a good person who can speak well

**Key emphasis:** eloquence is a public trust.

**Questions to ask:**
- What kind of person should be given influence through words?
- How does a child become that kind of person?
- What does Quintilian teach that schools still need?

**For ages 15-18:** discuss whether Quintilian's ideal is still possible in a culture driven by speed, image, and distraction.

### Afternoon: Final Synthesis Exercise (20-30 min)

Ask the student to complete one of these:
- **Ages 8-10:** Draw a teacher helping children use good words and write 3 sentences about it.
- **Ages 11-14:** Write one paragraph answering: *Why must a good speaker first be a good person?*
- **Ages 15-18:** Write a one-page response to: *Can humane education still produce honorable speakers in a degraded public culture?*

---

## Extension Ideas

### For younger students
- Practice reading aloud with calm tone and clear diction
- Memorize a short noble quotation
- Play a "helpful words / harmful words" sorting game

### For older students
- Read selections from *Institutio Oratoria* Book I and Book XII
- Compare Quintilian with Isocrates or Cicero
- Evaluate a modern school, speech, or influencer using Quintilian's standards

---

## End-of-Week Questions for Parents

At the end of the week, ask:
- Is my child more careful about the difference between clever speech and good speech?
- Has my child thought more seriously about imitation and models?
- Have we improved the speech atmosphere of the home at all?
- Has my child practiced clear, fair, and useful expression?

If yes, the week has done its work.

---

## Final Note

Quintilian week should leave students with a more serious understanding of education itself.

The point is not merely to learn about a Roman teacher. The point is to see that formation is slow, humane, demanding work — and that the ability to speak honorably is one of its most visible fruits.

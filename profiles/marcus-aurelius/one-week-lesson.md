# Marcus Aurelius: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Marcus Aurelius to one or more children. It is designed to be used by a parent or teacher at home, with no specialized materials beyond a notebook and — ideally, for older students — a paperback copy of the *Meditations*. Total time commitment: roughly **45–75 minutes per day**, divisible into morning and afternoon sessions.

The plan is calibrated for **ages 8-14** as its default. Extensions and substitutions for ages 15-18 are marked throughout.

The week moves through: **The Serious Boy → The Long Apprenticeship → Philosophy Meets Power → The Journal in the Tent → The Son Who Did Not Listen**. This follows the developmental logic of Marcus's life: early formation first, apprenticeship second, crisis third, daily practice fourth, the limits of formation fifth.

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for the child — this is the **"Marcus notebook"** for the week and, ideally, for months after. Choose something the child likes and will keep.
- A separate notebook for the parent if you will practice alongside (strongly recommended)
- Pens the child likes writing with
- Optional: a paperback copy of the *Meditations* — recommended translations: Gregory Hays (Modern Library, the most readable), Martin Hammond (Penguin), or Christopher Gill (Oxford, most precise)
- Optional for 15-18: a copy of Epictetus's *Enchiridion* (any translation; Penguin Classics is fine)
- Optional: a world map or a map of the Roman Empire to show the Danube frontier

### Choose Your Age Band

Pick the student lesson appropriate to your child(ren):
- [Ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md)
- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

If you have multiple children across bands, each can read their own lesson; the week's activities below work across ages with minor adjustment.

### Set the Journal Expectation on Day One

This is the most important structural element of the week. Tell the child, at the start of Monday:

> *"This week we are studying a man whose most important practice was writing in a notebook to himself, every night, to become a better person. We are going to do exactly what he did. You will start a notebook on Day One. You will write in it every night this week. I will not read it. No one will read it. It is for you. At the end of the week, you decide whether to keep doing it."*

Then make sure you do, in fact, not read it. This is the single most important promise of the week.

---

## Monday: The Serious Boy — The Formation Before the Throne

### Morning: Read Together (20–30 min)

Read the age-appropriate student lesson aloud, or have the student read it independently. Then discuss.

**Key moments to pause on:**
- The father's death when Marcus was three — *"How do you think being raised by a grandfather instead of a father changes a boy?"*
- Hadrian's nickname "Verissimus" — *"The emperor was watching a child and giving him a name for what he saw. What might someone who watched you name you?"*
- The philosopher's cloak at twelve — *"Marcus decided what kind of person he wanted to be at twelve. How young is too young to decide what you want to be?"*

**For 15-18:** Additionally read *Meditations* I.1–I.6 (the opening of Marcus's gratitude list — what he learned from his grandfather, his father's reputation, his mother, his great-grandfather, his tutor, and Diognetus). Notice the specificity. Each entry is a particular virtue from a particular person. This is gratitude with edges sharp.

### Afternoon: The First Journal Entry (20–30 min)

Hand each person their notebook. On the first page, have them write the date and the heading: *"My Marcus Notebook."*

Then the first entry, using three prompts:

1. **What went well today?** (At least two specific things.)
2. **What went badly today?** (At least one specific thing. Nobody is reading this. Be honest.)
3. **What will I try to do better tomorrow?** (At least one specific thing. Not "be better." Something concrete.)

The entry should be three to eight sentences total. Not an essay. Notes.

Share this much only: *"We each wrote. We each have our own notebook. We will do this again tomorrow."* Do not share content.

**For 15-18:** Add a fourth prompt: *"What did I observe in myself today that Epictetus would call an attempt to control what is not in my control?"* (If Epictetus hasn't been introduced yet, substitute: *"What did I spend emotional energy on today that was not actually in my control?"*)

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight, before bed, each person writes a second entry — this one very short, just three sentences. This establishes the nightly rhythm immediately.

---

## Tuesday: The Long Apprenticeship — Imitation as Formation

### Morning: The Story of Antoninus (20–30 min)

Re-read aloud (or have the student re-read) the section of the student lesson on the twenty-three years with Antoninus Pius.

**Key discussion points:**
- Marcus was seventeen when he moved into the imperial household. He was forty when Antoninus died. That is *twenty-three years* of watching a good emperor.
- *"Why do you think Hadrian and Antoninus kept Marcus close instead of sending him off to govern a province on his own?"*
- *"What are you learning by watching the people around you? Name one thing you are learning from one person right now."*

**For 15-18:** Read *Meditations* I.16 in full (the portrait of Antoninus). Note how Marcus learned by *observation and memorization* of a living model — he did not learn how to be a good emperor by reading; he learned by watching one and then rehearsing the lessons in memory. Discuss: *"What is the closest thing in your life to a living model of a virtue you want to acquire?"*

### Afternoon: The Book I Exercise (30–45 min)

Today the child begins their own version of *Meditations* Book I.

Instructions:

1. Turn to a new page in the Marcus notebook. Title it: *"People Who Have Formed Me."*
2. Think carefully. List **at least seven people** who have taught you something real.
3. For each person, write a specific virtue or practice you learned from them, in the form: *"From [name] I learned [specific thing]."*

Examples (to get started, but the child should write their own):
- *From my grandmother I learned to be patient when I am tired.*
- *From my soccer coach I learned to keep practicing something even when I am bad at it.*
- *From my math teacher I learned that you are not finished with a problem just because you got an answer.*

This should take 20–30 minutes of real thought. Do not rush it.

**For 8-10:** Reduce to five people. Allow more help with specificity.

**For 15-18:** Require at least twelve people. Require a mix: family, teachers, friends, authors read. Require that at least one be someone the student does not personally like — because even people we dislike sometimes teach us.

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight's journal entry (three sentences as before) should include: *"One thing I learned today from someone around me."*

---

## Wednesday: Philosophy Meets Power — The Throne and the Crises

### Morning: The Story of the Reign (25–35 min)

Today, focus on what Marcus did with power. Re-read or reteach the section on 161 CE onward: co-emperorship with Lucius Verus, the Tiber flood, the Parthian war, the Antonine plague, the Germanic invasions.

**Key discussion points:**
- *"Marcus refused to be sole emperor. He insisted his brother share the throne. Why would anyone voluntarily give away power?"*
- *"When the plague hit, Marcus sold his wife's jewelry in the Forum to buy grain, rather than raise taxes. What does this tell you about him?"*
- *"Marcus was almost fifty and not very healthy. He still went to war for ten years, sleeping in tents in the snow, because it was his duty. What do you think kept him going?"*

**For 15-18:** Introduce the Avidius Cassius episode in detail. Read a short summary of the revolt. Ask: *"Marcus refused to look at the head of the rebel, and burned the conspirators' letters unread. Was this wisdom or weakness? Defend your answer."* This is an excellent dinner-table or walk-and-talk discussion topic.

### Afternoon: The Dichotomy of Control (25–35 min)

Today, introduce (or for older students, deepen) Epictetus's core teaching: *some things are in our power, and some things are not*.

Walk through three concrete examples with the child:

1. **A test score.** Ask: *"What parts of a test score are in your power? What parts are not?"* (In your power: how you prepare, how carefully you read the questions, how you respond if you do badly. Not in your power: which questions are asked, how other students do, whether the teacher grades fairly.)

2. **A friendship problem.** Ask: *"When a friend is mad at you, what is in your power? What is not?"* (In your power: how you respond, whether you apologize if you did something wrong, whether you stay calm. Not in your power: whether they accept your apology, how they feel about you, what they say to other people.)

3. **A disappointment.** Ask: *"When something you were looking forward to gets cancelled, what is in your power?"* (In your power: how you respond, what you do instead, whether you let it ruin your day. Not in your power: the cancellation itself.)

For each example, notice: the *only* thing in our power, every time, is our own judgment and response. Marcus spent his life practicing this.

**For 8-10:** Simplify to two examples. Use very concrete, recent situations from the child's actual week.

**For 15-18:** Read Epictetus's *Enchiridion* Chapter 1 in full. Then have them identify three real situations from their recent week and classify what was in their power and what was not in each.

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight's journal entry should include: *"One thing today I was upset about — and which parts of it were actually in my power."*

---

## Thursday: The Journal in the Tent — Marcus as Philosophical Practice

### Morning: The *Meditations* as a Working Document (25–35 min)

Today the focus is the book itself. Explain — carefully, slowly — that the *Meditations* was not a book Marcus wrote to teach anyone. It was his private notebook. He never intended anyone else to read it. It survived by accident.

Read aloud, together, these three passages:

**Morning meditation (*Meditations* II.1):**
> *"Begin the morning by saying to yourself, I shall meet with the busybody, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial. All these things happen to them by reason of their ignorance of what is good and evil. But I, who have seen the nature of the good, that it is beautiful, and of the bad, that it is ugly... I can neither be injured by any of them, for no one can fix on me what is ugly, nor can I be angry with my kinsman, nor hate him."*

**On duty (*Meditations* V.1):**
> *"In the morning when you rise unwillingly, let this thought be present — I am rising to the work of a human being. Why then am I dissatisfied if I am going to do the things for which I exist and for which I was brought into the world? Or have I been made for this, to lie in bedclothes and keep myself warm?"*

**On the shortness of things (*Meditations* X.16):**
> *"Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one."*

After each passage, pause. Ask: *"Say this back in your own words."* Make sure the child can.

**For 15-18:** Add *Meditations* VIII.47 on judgment ("If you are distressed by anything external...") and IV.3 on the inner retreat. Discuss Hadot's three disciplines (assent, desire, action) at introductory level.

### Afternoon: The Morning Meditation Practice (20–30 min)

Today you practice what Marcus taught. Have the child write out, on a fresh page in the Marcus notebook, their own version of II.1 — a morning meditation, in their own words, anticipating the difficulties of their typical day.

Template (for younger children):
> *"Today I will probably meet someone who ____. I will probably be tempted to ____. I will probably want to ____. I will instead try to ____. I will remember that I am the kind of person who ____."*

Have them write it out. Have them read it aloud. Tomorrow morning, they will use it before getting out of bed.

**For 8-10:** Keep it short — three to four sentences. The template above is already enough.

**For 15-18:** Write a fuller version, at least half a page, that includes both the specific difficulties of the day ahead and a philosophical frame (e.g., reference to what is in your power). Commit to reading it every morning for the coming month.

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight's journal entry should include: *"Did I become a better person today, or a worse one? In what specific way?"*

---

## Friday: The Son Who Did Not Listen — The Limits of Formation

### Morning: The Commodus Story (25–35 min)

Today is the hardest day, and the most important. Tell — or have the child read — the story of Commodus.

Marcus had a son. The son was given every advantage. The son had the best philosophical teachers in the empire. Marcus made him co-emperor at sixteen to train him for the throne. When Marcus died, Commodus — at nineteen — inherited the empire. Within months he abandoned the German campaigns. Within years he had turned the imperial office into a theatrical joke. He fought as a gladiator. He renamed Rome after himself. He executed senators. Twelve years after Marcus's death, Commodus was strangled in his bath, and Rome fell into civil war.

**Key discussion points:**
- *"Marcus did everything right. His son turned out wrong. Whose fault is that?"*
- *"Can a parent force a child to be good?"*
- *"If the answer is no, does that mean formation is useless?"*

The answer to the third question is the key lesson of the week. Formation is not useless. It is necessary. But it is never sufficient. The student must *accept* what the teacher offers. No one else can do that for them.

This is the honest truth about formation. Nova Schola teaches it because it is true. And it is the reason that formation matters so much — because what the student decides, at every age, about whether to accept the formation, is the most important thing about who they will become.

**For 15-18:** Introduce the wider question: was Marcus's choice to leave the empire to Commodus (rather than adopt a better successor, as the four previous emperors had done) a free choice or a forced one? The best evidence is that it was free. Marcus could have chosen otherwise. He did not. Discuss: *"Marcus's single greatest political failure was an act of love for his son. What does this tell us about the relationship between private love and public duty?"*

### Afternoon: The Week in Review (25–35 min)

Today is the week's summation. Have the child go back through the Marcus notebook from Monday through Thursday. Re-read their own entries privately.

Then, on a new page, write a short reflection — maybe half a page — addressing:

1. *"What did I learn about Marcus this week?"*
2. *"What did I learn about myself this week?"*
3. *"Will I keep doing the journal after this week? Why or why not?"*

This is the hinge moment. If the week is going to become a permanent practice, this is where it is decided.

Encourage — do not require — the child to keep the practice going. If they choose yes, mark the date in their notebook: *"Day 1 of the Marcus practice."* If they choose no, let it rest. In a month, ask gently whether they'd like to try again.

**For 15-18:** In addition to the reflection, assign one of the following essays (to be written this weekend or over the next week, your choice):

- *"Plato imagined the philosopher-king. Marcus was one. How did the reality compare to the imagination?"*
- *"Marcus formed himself and failed to form Commodus. What does this tell us about the limits of a mentor's power?"*
- *"Is Marcus's Stoicism spiritually healthy, or is it a form of emotional suppression?"*

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight's journal entry: the final entry of the week. Three sentences as before, plus one additional line: *"One thing from Marcus that I want to carry with me."*

---

## After the Week

### The Sustaining Question

The measure of this week is not whether your child can recite Marcus's biography. The measure is whether they picked up the journal and kept writing in it.

If they did: the week succeeded. Let the practice continue without pressure. Ask only: *"Still writing?"* occasionally. Do not read. Do not inspect. Do not turn it into homework.

If they did not: do not treat this as failure. The seed was planted. In six months, ask again. In a year, ask again. The journal practice is the kind of thing that often catches on the second or third exposure.

### Further Reading

**For ages 8-10:** No further reading is required. The biography and the journal practice are the lesson. If they want more, read them a different section of the *Meditations* aloud occasionally — short, digestible pieces.

**For ages 11-14:** *Meditations* Book I in full, over the course of a month. Then selections from Books II and VIII. One section at a sitting. Let them marinate.

**For ages 15-18:** The full *Meditations* over the course of a year, ideally alongside Epictetus's *Enchiridion* and Pierre Hadot's *Inner Citadel*. This is not a week's work. This is a year's slow engagement with one of the great ethical texts in human history.

### Connections to the Rest of the Sequence

Marcus does not stand alone. He is deeply connected to the other figures in Nova Schola.

- **Back to Plato:** Read the *Republic* Books V–VII on philosopher-kings alongside Marcus's life. Is the thought experiment vindicated or refuted?
- **Back to Aristotle:** Compare Aristotle's failure with Alexander to Marcus's failure with Commodus. What is the same? What is different?
- **Back to Leonardo:** Compare Leonardo's notebooks to Marcus's *Meditations* as private working documents. How are they alike? How different?
- **Forward:** Marcus is a foundational reference point for every later figure who will have to decide how to hold power. Keep him in mind when you reach Washington, Jefferson, Churchill — all of whom, in their way, faced the Marcus question: *can I hold power and remain good?*

---

## A Final Word to the Parent

The week will change your child. If you practice alongside them — if you keep your own Marcus notebook, if you do the morning meditation, if you work the dichotomy of control into your own reactions — the week will also change you.

Marcus was not writing for children. He was writing for a tired middle-aged man trying to be better than he was yesterday. You are closer to his original audience than your child is. Take the week as seriously as you are asking your child to take it.

At the end of the week, when your child decides whether to keep writing, you will be deciding the same thing. Whichever way you both decide, decide honestly.

That, too, is Marcus.

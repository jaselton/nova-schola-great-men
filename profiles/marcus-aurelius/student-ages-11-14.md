# Marcus Aurelius: Student Lesson (Ages 11-14)
## The Journal on the Danube

---

## The Essential Question

What happens when the person who least wants power is given the most of it?

This is the question that Marcus Aurelius's life answers. And the answer is stranger, and more encouraging, than you might expect.

---

## A Serious Boy

Marcus Aurelius was born in Rome on 26 April 121 CE, into one of the most prominent senatorial families of the Roman Empire. His father died when Marcus was about three. He was raised in the house of his grandfather — Marcus Annius Verus, a three-time consul and one of the most powerful men in Rome.

This was not a happy, playful childhood. It was a serious one. Marcus grew up surrounded by older men carrying heavy responsibilities, in a household where duty and self-control were expected rather than praised.

The emperor Hadrian noticed Marcus when he was very young. He called him **Verissimus** — "most truthful" — a pun on the family name *Verus*. Hadrian was already thinking about the succession, and the serious little boy in his grandfather's house had caught his attention.

At twelve, Marcus did something remarkable for a rich Roman boy. He put on the rough wool cloak of a philosopher and began sleeping on the floor as an ascetic practice. His mother persuaded him, eventually, to sleep on a cot. But the gesture was not childish playacting. It was the first visible sign of a philosophical vocation that would shape his entire life.

Marcus did not yet know he would one day be emperor. He did know that he wanted to live seriously, deliberately, for something larger than himself.

---

## The Adoption That Changed Everything

In 138 CE, Hadrian was dying. He needed a successor. His first choice had died. He now arranged an elaborate double adoption.

Hadrian adopted a respected senator named **Antoninus Pius** — on the condition that Antoninus, in turn, adopt two younger men: the seventeen-year-old Marcus and a seven-year-old boy named Lucius Verus. The plan was clear: Antoninus would rule, and when he died, both Marcus and Lucius would rule together.

Marcus, by his own later account, was *sad* when he moved into the imperial household. He had wanted to become a philosopher. Now he had to become a statesman.

He did not resist. Roman aristocratic culture took duty with deadly seriousness. If the emperor said *you will be the next emperor*, you did not refuse. You prepared.

And so Marcus began the longest and most careful training program any future emperor had ever received.

---

## Twenty-Three Years of Apprenticeship

From 138 to 161 CE — for **twenty-three years** — Marcus served as the adopted son, trusted companion, and understudy of Emperor Antoninus Pius.

This is almost impossible to imagine in modern terms. Imagine that, at seventeen, you were told you would one day become president, and that for the next twenty-three years you would live in the White House, sit in on every meeting, watch every decision, travel nowhere on your own, and gradually take on more and more responsibility until the current president died.

That is what happened to Marcus.

He was made consul three times. He was initiated into every part of Roman government. He watched Antoninus hear petitions, receive ambassadors, make laws, manage provinces, and — most importantly — **live simply and well as emperor**. Antoninus was not a tyrant, not a pleasure-seeker, not a warmonger. He was a careful, modest, hard-working ruler. The affection with which Marcus later described him in the *Meditations* is one of the most moving portraits in ancient literature.

> *"In my father I observed mildness of temper, and unchangeable resolution in the things which he had determined after due deliberation... his readiness to give way without envy to those who possessed any particular faculty..."*
> — *Meditations*, I.16

Marcus did not learn to be a good emperor from a book. He learned by watching a good emperor, up close, for a generation.

This is one of the most important lessons in the whole history of teaching: **proximity to a good model is worth more than any lecture**.

---

## The Book That Formed Him

During these same years, Marcus's philosophical training continued. He studied with several Stoic philosophers. The most important was **Junius Rusticus**, a Stoic senator who became a personal friend.

Rusticus gave Marcus a gift that changed his life: a copy of the **Discourses of Epictetus**.

Who was Epictetus? Here is the fact that matters: he had been a slave. He had been born into slavery in Asia Minor, brought to Rome as a boy, given to a master who allowed him to study philosophy, and eventually freed. He became the greatest Stoic teacher of his generation. His student Arrian transcribed his lectures, and those transcriptions survive today as the *Discourses*.

Epictetus's central teaching is simple and brutal:

> *"Some things are in our control, and some are not. In our control are our opinions, pursuits, desires, aversions — in short, whatever are our own actions. Not in our control are body, property, reputation, command, and, in one word, whatever are not our own actions."*

Everything painful in human life, Epictetus said, comes from confusing these two categories — from trying to control what is not in our power (what other people think, what fate does, whether we get sick), and neglecting to control what *is* in our power (our own judgments and choices).

Marcus absorbed this teaching so deeply that the *Meditations*, written thirty years later, can be read as a long, continuing conversation with Epictetus. A slave formed an emperor. This is one of the most remarkable facts in the history of philosophy.

---

## The Throne Arrives

Antoninus Pius died on 7 March 161 CE. Marcus was forty years old.

He immediately did something surprising. The Senate tried to make him sole emperor. Marcus refused. He insisted that his adoptive brother **Lucius Verus** be made co-emperor with equal formal powers. That had been Hadrian's original plan, and Antoninus had carried it forward. Marcus would not break faith with it, even though he was plainly the more capable of the two.

This was the first public demonstration of his philosophical formation in action. Lesser men take more power because they can. Marcus gave away power because it was the right thing to do.

---

## The Avalanche

Then the disasters began. Not one, not two — an avalanche.

**161 CE:** A Tiber flood destroyed grain stores. Rome faced famine.

**161–166 CE:** The Parthians invaded the eastern provinces. A Roman legion was annihilated in Armenia. Marcus sent Lucius east with the empire's best generals. After four brutal years, Rome won.

**165 CE:** The returning armies brought back a plague. The **Antonine Plague** — probably smallpox — swept through the empire. Estimates of the death toll range from five to ten million people — perhaps ten percent of the empire's population. Whole legions were gutted. Cities emptied. The plague continued, in waves, for the rest of Marcus's reign.

**166 CE:** Germanic tribes crossed the Danube frontier. By 170 CE, they had invaded Italy itself and besieged Aquileia — the deepest invasion since Hannibal four hundred years earlier.

Marcus, now close to fifty, packed up and went to war.

---

## The Philosopher at War

For most of the last fourteen years of his life, Marcus Aurelius — a philosopher with a delicate constitution — lived in military camps on the Danube frontier, in the territory of modern Austria, Hungary, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic. The winters were brutal. The campaigns were grueling. The plague kept coming back.

He was not a natural soldier. He did it anyway.

And it was in those military camps, on those cold nights, by the light of oil lamps in leather tents, that Marcus wrote the book we now call **The Meditations**.

---

## A Book Never Meant to Be Read

The Greek title of the *Meditations* is *Ta eis heauton* — literally, *"Things to Himself"* or *"To Himself."*

Marcus did not write it for publication. He did not write it for students. He did not write it for history. He wrote it for **himself** — private notes to himself, late at night, to remind himself how to be a better man tomorrow.

The book survived by pure accident. Some secretary, some friend, some librarian kept the notes after Marcus's death. Centuries later, Byzantine scholars preserved them. The book came to Europe during the Renaissance. It has been translated into every major language and has never gone out of print.

You are reading, today, the private journal of an emperor who did not want you to read it.

This is why the book is so strange and so good. There is no performance in it. Marcus is not trying to impress anyone. He is just trying to remind himself of the same things, over and over, because he keeps forgetting them:

- You will be dead soon. So will everyone you know. So will the empire. Live accordingly.
- The only things in your power are your own judgments. Everything else is not yours.
- Other people will be difficult, foolish, ungrateful, and cruel. Do not be surprised. Do your duty anyway.
- The universe is rational and interconnected. You are a small part of a large order. Play your part.
- Do not waste the present moment on anxiety about the future or regret about the past.

The themes repeat because Marcus needed to remind himself of them again and again. That is also why the book is so useful: it is not a system. It is a *practice*. Anyone can imitate it.

---

## The Revolt That Tested Everything

In 175 CE, one of Marcus's most trusted generals — **Avidius Cassius**, the governor of Syria — received a false report that Marcus had died. Cassius declared himself emperor.

How did Marcus respond?

He did not panic. He did not launch a vengeful campaign. He prepared for war but hoped to avoid it. Within three months, Cassius's own officers assassinated him and brought his head to Marcus.

Marcus refused to look at it.

He executed none of Cassius's family. He burned, unread, the correspondence that would have identified Cassius's co-conspirators. He did not want to know who had betrayed him.

This is Stoicism in action. It is not abstract philosophy. It is a real emperor, in a real political crisis, doing in real life what he had been writing in his notebook for years.

---

## The Death and the Failure

Marcus fell ill on campaign in early 180 CE and died on 17 March, either at Vindobona (modern Vienna) or at Sirmium. He was fifty-eight.

His last words, according to Cassius Dio, were addressed to the officer of the watch:

> *"Go to the rising sun; for I am already setting."*

And then came the failure that haunts his legacy.

Marcus had a biological son: **Commodus**, nineteen years old. Marcus loved him. Marcus had given him every philosophical and imperial advantage. And Marcus broke with the precedent of the previous four emperors — who had all adopted their best available successor — and left the empire to his son.

Commodus was a catastrophe. He abandoned the Germanic campaigns. He returned to Rome. He renamed Rome after himself. He fought as a gladiator in the arena. He executed senators. He ruined the work of his father's reign within twelve years. In 192 CE, he was strangled in his bath by his own wrestling partner, and the empire collapsed into civil war.

The most philosophical father in Roman history produced the most dissolute son.

This is not a minor footnote. It is the defining irony of Marcus's life. And it contains one of the most important lessons of the Great Man tradition.

---

## Formation Equips — It Does Not Determine

Here is something you will see, again and again, in the lives of great mentors:

- Socrates formed Plato (who succeeded brilliantly) and Alcibiades (who betrayed Athens).
- Aristotle formed Alexander (who became a conqueror but also sometimes a monster).
- Marcus formed Commodus (who turned out to be a disaster).

The pattern tells us something hard and important. The mentor can do everything right. The mentor can be a saint. The mentor can provide the best teachers, the best books, the best examples. And the student can still refuse to accept the formation.

This is because formation is not programming. A student is a free agent. You cannot force someone to become good. You can only equip them, show them, and hope.

Marcus knew this. He wrote, in the *Meditations*:

> *"A man should be upright, not be kept upright."*

You must hold yourself up, in the end. No one — not even an emperor-philosopher father — can do it for you.

## What You Can Learn from Marcus

### 1. Duty is a higher form of freedom.

Our culture often says: *follow your passion*. Marcus's life says something different, and harder, and truer: *accept your post*. Marcus wanted to be a philosopher. He was given an empire. He did not sulk. He did not run. He did his job — for nineteen years, through war and plague and betrayal.

Sometimes the most important thing you will do in your life will be something you did not choose and did not want. Marcus shows you how to do it anyway.

### 2. Proximity to a good model beats any textbook.

Marcus had twenty-three years next to Antoninus Pius. That is why he could be a good emperor — he had *seen* one. If you want to learn how to do anything well, find someone who already does it well, and get as close to them as you can, for as long as you can. Books help. Watching helps more.

### 3. The evening journal is the single most imitable practice in the history of philosophy.

You can start tonight. A small notebook. Three questions:

- What did I do well today?
- What did I do badly today?
- What will I try to do better tomorrow?

Marcus did this for years. His notebook became one of the most important books in the world. Yours will not become that. But it will change *you* — and that is the whole point.

### 4. Gratitude is a practice, not a feeling.

Book I of the *Meditations* is a gratitude exercise: Marcus names the specific people who formed him and exactly what he learned from each. His grandfather. His mother. His teacher Rusticus. His adoptive father Antoninus. Sixteen or more named individuals, each with a specific debt recorded.

You can do this too. Not a vague "I am grateful." A *specific* list: *From my father I learned X. From my grandmother, Y. From my math teacher, Z.* This is gratitude with its edges sharp.

### 5. Only your own judgments are in your power.

You cannot control the weather. You cannot control whether people like you. You cannot control whether you get sick. You cannot even control whether you live through next week.

You *can* control what you decide to do, how you respond to what happens, what kind of person you choose to become. Epictetus taught this to Marcus. Marcus taught it to the whole Western tradition after him. You can use it today.

## Discussion Questions

1. Marcus did not want to be emperor. He did it anyway. Can you think of a time in your own life when you had to do something you did not want to do — not because you were forced, but because it was the right thing? What did that feel like?

2. Marcus refused to look at the head of the general who had betrayed him, and burned the letters that would have identified other conspirators. Was this wise, or was it weakness? Explain.

3. Marcus formed his son Commodus with the best teachers and philosophy in the world, and Commodus turned out to be a terrible emperor. Whose fault was that? Marcus's? Commodus's? No one's?

4. Marcus wrote his *Meditations* for himself, with no intention of anyone else reading them. Do you think he wrote them better because no one was watching — or worse?

5. Which is harder: ruling an empire, or ruling yourself?

## Practice for the Week

**Daily:**
- **Morning meditation.** Before getting out of bed, say to yourself: *"Today I will meet people who are difficult. I will do my work anyway. I will be the kind of person I have decided to be."*
- **Evening journal.** Three sentences every night: what went well, what went badly, what you will try tomorrow.

**Once this week:**
- **Write your own Book I.** List ten people who have formed you (living or dead, family or teachers or authors). For each person, write one specific thing you learned from them.
- **Read *Meditations*, Book II, Section 1.** ("Begin the morning by saying to yourself...") Memorize at least the first sentence.

**Once this week, harder:**
- **Pick one thing you do not want to do but that is your duty.** Do it this week. Do not complain. When you want to complain, write the complaint in your journal instead, and then go do the duty.

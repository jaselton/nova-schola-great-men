# Marcus Aurelius: Student Lesson (Ages 15-18)
## The Philosopher-King and the Limits of Formation

---

## The Question at the Center

In Book V of the *Republic*, Plato imagines a state ruled by philosopher-kings: men and women who have been formed, over decades of careful education, into beings who combine wisdom with power. Nearly every reader of the *Republic* has concluded that such a thing is impossible — a thought experiment, not a real option. Plato himself tried to form one (Dionysius II of Syracuse) and failed. Aristotle tried to form one (Alexander the Great) and partially failed.

Then, five hundred years after Plato wrote the *Republic*, the thought experiment actually ran. An empire of fifty million people — probably the largest political unit ever brought under a single ruler up to that point — was handed to a man who had been trained from adolescence in Stoic philosophy, who wrote Stoic exercises to himself every night, who wore the cloak of a philosopher at twelve, and who did not want the throne. He held it for nineteen years.

His name was Marcus Aurelius. And the most important question about his life is: *did the experiment work?*

The short answer is yes. The longer answer is yes, but with qualifications that illuminate everything important about the relationship between formation and character, power and restraint, fathers and sons.

---

## The Biography in Condensed Form

You already know the skeleton from the younger-age lessons. Here is the essential shape, with the pieces that matter at your age:

**121 CE:** Born in Rome into a wealthy Spanish-Roman senatorial family. His father dies when Marcus is about three. He is raised by his paternal grandfather, a three-time consul. Emperor Hadrian notices him young; nicknames him *Verissimus*, "most truthful."

**c. 133 CE:** At twelve, adopts the philosopher's cloak and begins ascetic practices. The vocation comes early.

**138 CE:** Hadrian, dying, arranges a double adoption: Antoninus Pius is adopted as Hadrian's heir, and Antoninus in turn adopts seventeen-year-old Marcus and seven-year-old Lucius Verus. Marcus is now the heir-presumptive-once-removed.

**138–161 CE:** Twenty-three years of apprenticeship under Antoninus Pius. Marcus is given three consulships, sits in on imperial business, and watches at close range what good rule looks like. Simultaneously he deepens his Stoic studies. Junius Rusticus gives him Epictetus's *Discourses*. This book shapes him more than any other.

**161 CE:** Antoninus dies. Marcus insists on sharing the throne with Lucius Verus.

**161–180 CE:** The reign. Tiber flood, Parthian war (161–166), Antonine plague from 165, Marcomannic wars from 166, revolt of Avidius Cassius in 175, death of Faustina in 175, Commodus co-emperor in 177, return to the Danube in 178, death in 180.

**The *Meditations*:** Written on campaign between roughly 170 and 180 CE. Private notes. Never meant for publication. Survived by accident. Among the most influential ethical texts in human history.

**After:** Commodus succeeds, ruins the Antonine legacy, is assassinated in 192 CE. Civil war follows. The "Pax Romana" that the five good emperors built is over.

## The Formation: What Actually Happened

The temptation is to explain Marcus by pointing to the *Meditations* and saying: *he was formed by Stoicism*. That is true but incomplete. Stoicism was the language in which Marcus articulated his formation. It was not the sole cause.

Look closely at Book I of the *Meditations*. It is not a list of doctrines. It is a list of **people**.

> *"From my grandfather Verus I learned good morals and the government of my temper."*
> *"From the reputation and remembrance of my father, modesty and a manly character."*
> *"From my mother, piety and beneficence, and abstinence, not only from evil deeds, but even from evil thoughts; and further, simplicity of life, far removed from the habits of the rich."*

The list continues for sixteen or more individuals. Each person contributes a specific virtue. Marcus is, by his own account, the **composite product** of a whole system of formation — not the fruit of one mentor, not the disciple of one school, but the distillation of many living models.

This is worth pausing on. The Nova Schola Great Man framework often emphasizes the mentor-student pair: Socrates and Plato, Plato and Aristotle, Aristotle and Alexander. Marcus's formation shows a different pattern — formation by **a field of models**, each specialized, each observed carefully, each consciously absorbed.

If you want to be formed like Marcus, you are not looking for one perfect mentor. You are looking for a *set* of excellent people, each of whom does one thing well, each of whom you can consciously learn from. Your English teacher for prose. Your grandfather for patience. Your coach for discipline. Your uncle for generosity. Keep a list. Write it down.

### The Two Decisive Formations

Within this general pattern, two specific influences matter most.

**Junius Rusticus and the *Discourses* of Epictetus.** Rusticus — a Stoic senator and personal friend — gave Marcus a copy of Epictetus's lectures, transcribed by Arrian. Epictetus had been a Phrygian slave brought to Rome, educated in philosophy, and eventually freed. He became the dominant Stoic teacher of his generation.

Epictetus's teaching, stripped to its core, is:

> *"Some things are in our control, and some are not. In our control are our opinions, pursuits, desires, aversions — in short, whatever are our own actions. Not in our control are body, property, reputation, command — whatever are not our own actions. The things in our control are by nature free, unrestrained, unhindered; but those not in our control are weak, slavish, restrained, belonging to others. Remember, then, that if you suppose that things which are slavish by nature are also free, and that what belongs to others is your own, then you will be hindered, you will lament, you will be disturbed..."*
> — *Enchiridion*, 1

The philosophical system that emerges from this — the *dichotomy of control*, the discipline of assent, the distinction between *hēgemonikon* (the ruling part of the soul) and externals — became Marcus's working ethical operating system for the rest of his life. When you read the *Meditations*, you are reading Epictetus applied to an emperor's life.

**Antoninus Pius.** For twenty-three years, Marcus lived with and watched a good emperor. The portrait of Antoninus in *Meditations* I.16 and VI.30 is one of the most careful observations ever made of a teacher by a student:

> *"Take as an example... the things which are related of Antoninus Pius: his compliance, his constancy even in those things which, upon inquiry, appeared to be just; his freedom from all vanity in regard to external honors; his love of labor and perseverance; his readiness to hear those who had anything to propose for the common weal; his undeviating firmness in giving to every man according to his deserts..."*

The emperor does not become a philosopher by reading. He becomes one by *watching* a good ruler and rehearsing that ruler's moves in memory until they become native. Marcus names this explicitly: when tempted to act against his own standards, he asks what Antoninus would have done.

This is **imitation as philosophical method**. It is the oldest teaching technology in the world. And it works.

## The Reign as Philosophical Laboratory

Consider what Marcus actually did with power. This is where the thought experiment is tested.

### The Co-Emperorship

Hadrian had intended both Marcus and Lucius Verus to rule. Antoninus had adopted both. When Antoninus died, the Senate offered Marcus sole power. Marcus refused and insisted Lucius share the throne as co-Augustus with equal formal standing — the first time in Roman history. Lucius was by any measure less capable and less disciplined than Marcus. Sharing power with him was both politically risky and philosophically correct. Marcus chose the risk.

This is one of the clearest documented cases of someone with supreme power voluntarily reducing it because it was the right thing to do. Political theorists call this behavior almost impossible. Marcus did it on day one.

### The Plague Response

When the Antonine Plague hit, Marcus did not flee Rome. He raised money by auctioning imperial treasures — including his wife's silks and the golden goblets of the palace — in the Forum rather than raise taxes on a devastated population. He expanded public grain distribution. He personally attended the dying.

A lesser emperor would have retreated to an imperial estate in Campania. Marcus stayed.

### The Marcomannic Wars

For the last ten winters of his life, Marcus — a philosopher in his late forties and fifties, with a delicate constitution — lived in military camps on the Danube. He did this because duty required it. He did not enjoy it. The *Meditations* occasionally show the strain:

> *"In the morning when you rise unwillingly, let this thought be present — I am rising to the work of a human being. Why then am I dissatisfied if I am going to do the things for which I exist and for which I was brought into the world? Or have I been made for this, to lie in bedclothes and keep myself warm?"*
> — *Meditations*, V.1

This is not the complacent satisfaction of a man who loves his duty. It is the deliberate self-correction of a man who would rather be somewhere else and refuses to allow that preference to determine his action. Stoicism is not the absence of feeling. It is feeling's refusal to govern.

### The Revolt of Avidius Cassius

In 175 CE, the general Avidius Cassius — Marcus's trusted governor of Syria — received a false report of Marcus's death and declared himself emperor. Within three months, Cassius's own officers killed him and brought his head to Marcus.

Marcus refused to look at it. He executed none of Cassius's family. He burned, unread, Cassius's correspondence — correspondence that would have identified the other senators involved in the plot.

No other Roman emperor, before or after, responded to a rebellion this way. A standard imperial response would have been a years-long purge. Marcus refused the purge.

This is not squeamishness. It is *applied Stoicism*. Marcus did not want the information because he did not want the temptation. Epictetus had taught him that the only thing in his power was his own judgment. He would not allow his judgment to be clouded by knowledge that would demand vengeance. He destroyed the evidence to preserve himself, not to preserve Cassius's friends.

This episode, more than any other in Marcus's reign, shows Stoicism functioning not as theory but as political practice. It is the closest thing we have to proof that the philosopher-king is possible.

## The *Meditations* as Spiritual Exercise

Pierre Hadot's *Inner Citadel* (the essential modern commentary on the *Meditations*) makes the case that the *Meditations* should not be read as a treatise — they are not organized, they do not argue, they repeat — but as a working example of what the Stoics called *askesis*: spiritual exercise.

Stoic spiritual exercises, according to Hadot, involve three disciplines:

### 1. The Discipline of Assent (Judgment)
Epictetus taught that every impression that comes to you is tested before you assent to it. If someone insults you, the *impression* is: *"this is an outrage."* The discipline of assent says: *pause before believing the impression*. Is it actually an outrage? Or is it simply a sound that I have decided to interpret as an outrage? The impression is not me. My assent is me.

Marcus practices this constantly:
> *"If you are distressed by anything external, the pain is not due to the thing itself, but to your estimate of it; and this you have the power to revoke at any moment."*
> — *Meditations*, VIII.47

### 2. The Discipline of Desire (Acceptance of Fate)
The Stoics believed the universe was rational and interconnected — a single living system in which everything is woven together. They called this *sympatheia*. The wise person desires only what the universe provides and rejects only what the universe removes. This is not passivity. It is the recognition that your desires, aimed at things outside your control, are a constant source of suffering.

Marcus:
> *"Everything harmonizes with me, which is harmonious to thee, O Universe. Nothing for me is too early nor too late, which is in due time for thee. Everything is fruit to me which thy seasons bring, O Nature: from thee are all things, in thee are all things, to thee all things return."*
> — *Meditations*, IV.23

### 3. The Discipline of Action (Duty to Others)
The Stoics believed all rational beings were members of a single community — the *cosmopolis*, or world-city. Your duty is to act for the common good, even when the others in the community are difficult, foolish, or cruel.

Marcus:
> *"We are made for cooperation, like feet, like hands, like eyelids, like the rows of the upper and lower teeth. To act against one another, then, is contrary to nature."*
> — *Meditations*, II.1

These three disciplines are not separable. Every entry in the *Meditations* practices at least one of them. The book is not a record of Marcus's philosophy. It is a record of Marcus *doing* philosophy.

This is why the *Meditations* can still be read today as a living document. You cannot read the *Republic* and immediately become a better person. You can read the *Meditations* and immediately have a method for this evening.

## The Failures

An honest assessment of Marcus Aurelius requires engaging his failures. There are at least four.

### 1. The Persecution of Christians

Under Marcus's reign, Christians were persecuted more systematically than under any previous emperor except Nero. The martyrdoms at Lyon in 177 CE were officially sanctioned. The philosopher who wrote that all rational beings are members of one body did not extend that brotherhood to Christians.

Why? Partly because, to an educated Roman, Christianity looked like a stubborn superstition whose adherents refused the minimal civic gesture — a token sacrifice to the state gods — that Roman politics required. Christians, to Marcus, looked anti-civic: people who would not participate in the basic rites that held the empire together. From a Stoic perspective, which emphasized duty to the community, this was not merely religious eccentricity. It was civic refusal.

But this is a rationalization. The honest truth is that Marcus, like every ancient, could not see outside his cultural frame. The Christians were the new thing. Marcus, like most elites in any age, could not recognize the new thing's legitimacy. This is his cultural blindness, and it is real.

It is also the exact parallel to Aristotle's defense of slavery. Every great man — every one — has one such blind spot. The lesson for us is not to dismiss Marcus but to ask *what is ours*. What looks, to us, like obvious civic refusal but is, in fact, something new that we are failing to see?

### 2. The Failure with Commodus

Marcus broke the pattern of the Five Good Emperors. His four predecessors — Nerva, Trajan, Hadrian, Antoninus — had all adopted an unrelated successor on the basis of merit. Marcus had a biological son, Commodus, and left the empire to him.

Commodus was nineteen when Marcus died. He had been given the best teachers, trained in philosophy, made co-emperor at sixteen to prepare him. None of it took. He abandoned the German campaigns, returned to Rome, abandoned serious administration, renamed Rome after himself, and fought as a gladiator. He was strangled in 192 CE, and the civil wars that followed ended the Antonine peace.

Was Marcus's choice forced? Almost certainly not. He could have passed over Commodus. He chose not to. The reasons are debated: perhaps he believed sons were easier to protect than heirs; perhaps he believed Commodus would come around; perhaps he simply could not disinherit his own child. Whatever the reason, it was the wrong decision, and Marcus must be judged for it.

But — and this is the deeper point — *even if Marcus had disinherited Commodus*, his formation of his son had already failed. Commodus had been given every advantage. He did not accept them. The mentor cannot force the student's assent.

This is the central lesson of the Great Man tradition's self-awareness. Formation equips. It does not determine. The student is free. Commodus is the proof.

### 3. The Tolerance of Unworthy Friends

Marcus's wife Faustina was the subject of extensive gossip about infidelity. His brother Lucius Verus was famous for dissipation. Various courtiers took advantage of Marcus's reluctance to move against people he knew personally.

Marcus praised all of them. In the *Meditations* he calls Faustina "obedient, affectionate, simple." Whether she was or was not, his public loyalty to her was absolute.

Is this a virtue or a flaw? It is both. Stoic restraint from anger shades, in Marcus, into an occasional unwillingness to make hard personnel decisions. The emperor who refused to look at Cassius's head also refused, for years, to see clearly what some of his courtiers were doing. This is Stoicism's failure mode. Equanimity can become evasion.

### 4. The Occasional Slide Toward Coldness

The *Meditations* are sometimes dark. "Soon you will have forgotten all things; soon all things will have forgotten you." "The stench of death is around you." "What is all this but the ceremony of a corpse being carried out of a house?"

These meditations on mortality are traditional Stoic exercises — the *praemeditatio malorum*, the premeditation of evils — and at moderate dose they are spiritually useful. They strip away illusion. They force us to value the present.

But the Stoics themselves warned about the slide from *apatheia* (freedom from destructive passion) into simple coldness. Marcus occasionally slides. He is not always warm. The *Meditations* are sometimes a man trying to talk himself out of grief rather than actually grieving.

The lesson is not to reject the practice. It is to know the dosage. Used well, Stoic mortality-meditation is liberating. Used poorly, it is a kind of emotional asceticism that refuses to love.

## What Marcus Teaches That No One Else Teaches

Every great figure in the Nova Schola corpus teaches something specific that the others do not. Here is Marcus's distinctive contribution:

### 1. Philosophy as Practice, Not System

Plato gives you a dialogue to read. Aristotle gives you a treatise to study. Marcus gives you an *exercise* to do tonight. The *Meditations* are not meant to be mastered. They are meant to be **imitated**. You open a notebook and start writing to yourself. You do it again tomorrow. That is the discipline. That is the whole thing.

No other ancient philosopher offers this directly. This is Marcus's gift to every subsequent generation: a practice so simple a child can begin it, so deep a lifetime cannot exhaust it.

### 2. Power Without Corruption

Acton's famous line — *power corrupts, and absolute power corrupts absolutely* — has one devastating counterexample in human history, and Marcus Aurelius is it. For nineteen years he held more power than almost any human who has ever lived. He did not use it for personal aggrandizement. He did not build monuments to himself. He did not execute rivals. He sold his wife's jewelry rather than raise taxes in a plague year.

Every political philosophy that assumes power necessarily corrupts must reckon with Marcus. He is the counter-example. He is the proof that formation *can* survive power.

### 3. Duty as Freedom

Our culture teaches a version of freedom as the absence of constraint — freedom to do what you want. Marcus teaches a different version: freedom as the capacity to do what must be done, when you do not want to do it.

The first kind of freedom is easy and cheap. The second kind is rare and dignified. Marcus did not want to be emperor. He did not want to spend ten winters on the Danube. He did not want to manage a plague. He did these things because they were his to do. This is not slavery to duty. It is the only real freedom: the freedom to be the kind of person you have decided to be, even when circumstances press against the decision.

### 4. The Honest Counterweight to the Formation Thesis

The Great Man framework can easily slide into a kind of pedagogical triumphalism: *if only we form children properly, they will become great*. Marcus's failure with Commodus is the permanent check on this. Even perfect formation, by a philosopher father with an empire's resources, could not guarantee a great son.

This is not an argument against formation. It is an argument for *humility within formation*. The mentor does the work. The student must accept it. No shortcut. No guarantee.

Nova Schola must teach this explicitly. Marcus is the figure who forces us to say it out loud.

## Primary Reading (Do This)

You should read the following texts this year, if at all possible:

1. **The *Meditations***, Books I, II, IV, VI, and VIII, in a good translation. Gregory Hays (Modern Library, 2002) is the most readable for modern English. Christopher Gill (Oxford) is the most philosophically precise. Martin Hammond (Penguin) is a good middle option. Read slowly. Aim for one or two sections per sitting. Pause. Write.

2. **Epictetus's *Enchiridion*** — the short, pocket-sized "Handbook" of Stoic practice. This is the distilled Epictetus. Read it twice. Once for orientation, once for practice.

3. **Pierre Hadot, *The Inner Citadel*** — the single most illuminating modern study. Hadot reads the *Meditations* as spiritual exercise. Once you read him, you cannot read the *Meditations* the same way.

4. **Cassius Dio, *Roman History*, Book LXXII** — the historical context. Short. Useful for the political narrative.

5. **Optional but recommended: Anthony Birley, *Marcus Aurelius: A Biography*** — the standard modern historical biography. Careful, judicious, excellent on the military campaigns.

## Essays to Consider Writing

1. **"Plato imagined the philosopher-king. Marcus was one. How did the reality compare to the imagination?"** This is the biggest and most rewarding question on the list. Engage *Republic* V–VII seriously alongside the *Meditations*.

2. **"Marcus formed himself and failed to form Commodus. What does this tell us about the limits of a mentor's power?"** A direct engagement with the Great Man framework's central tension.

3. **"The Avidius Cassius response as Stoic practice."** How does Marcus's refusal to launch a purge cash out the *Meditations*' teaching about justice, anger, and the common good?

4. **"Is Marcus's Stoicism spiritually healthy, or is it a form of emotional suppression?"** Engage both the tradition and the critique. Hadot on one side, modern therapeutic critiques on the other.

5. **"Marcus on Christians — the cultural blindness of a good man. What is ours?"** A difficult and important essay. Model humility and historical rigor simultaneously.

6. **"Duty and freedom in Marcus Aurelius."** Contrast with the modern therapeutic model of freedom as self-expression.

## Practices to Adopt

**Daily for the next month:**

- **Morning meditation.** Before rising, rehearse mentally the difficulties of the day and your intended response. Use *Meditations* II.1 as a template.
- **Evening journal.** Three sections: the day's failures, the day's successes, tomorrow's resolves. Do not let anyone else read it. Do not write for anyone else. Write for you.
- **Memento mori.** Once a day, take thirty seconds to remember that you will die. Not morbidly. Clarifyingly.

**Weekly:**

- **Book I exercise.** Once, at the start of this practice: write your own Book I. Name the people who have formed you. For each, name the specific virtue they taught you. Be specific, not vague.
- **Review the disciplines.** End of each week, ask: did I practice the discipline of assent (watching my judgments)? The discipline of desire (accepting what I could not control)? The discipline of action (doing my duty to others)?

**Once, this year:**

- Read the entire *Meditations* through, start to finish.
- Read Hadot's *Inner Citadel*.
- Write one of the essays above.

## The Final Question

Cassius Dio records that Marcus's last words to his officer of the watch were:

> *"Go to the rising sun; for I am already setting."*

It is a philosopher's ending — composed, unsentimental, focused on the next thing rather than the departing one.

But consider: a few months later, Commodus abandoned the German campaigns, dissolved the philosophical court, and began the long decline that ended the empire's golden age.

Did Marcus's life succeed or fail?

I think the honest answer is: it succeeded completely at what was in his power, and failed partially at what was not. This is exactly what his own philosophy would have predicted. The only things in his power were his own judgments and actions. Those he governed to the end. The judgments and actions of his son were not in his power. Those he could only offer — offer through the best teachers, the best books, the best examples, his own lived presence — and hope that Commodus would accept the offer.

Commodus did not.

This is the deepest lesson Marcus leaves us. **The philosopher can be a king without corruption. But even the philosopher-king cannot force another soul into virtue. The student is free.**

That freedom is terrifying and glorious. It is the reason formation can fail. It is also the reason formation matters — because if it could not fail, it would not be formation of a *person*.

Every time you pick up the notebook tonight and write three sentences to yourself, you are doing exactly what Marcus did. You are accepting the offer. You are choosing, freely, to be formed.

That is the whole of the philosophical life. Marcus practiced it. Commodus refused it. The choice, every night, is yours.

# Marcus Aurelius: Parent Teaching Guide

---

## Overview: What This Profile Teaches

Marcus Aurelius is the figure in the Nova Schola sequence who shows what philosophy looks like when it is *practiced* rather than merely studied. Where Plato gives us dialogues, Aristotle gives us treatises, and Socrates gives us a life of questioning, Marcus gives us something more directly imitable than any of them: a private journal, written at night, that shows a man using philosophy as a daily discipline to become a better person.

This is the most imitable figure in the entire Nova Schola corpus to date. Any child over eight can start, this evening, the exact practice Marcus used for the last decade of his life: open a notebook, review the day, plan the next day, do it again tomorrow.

This profile teaches five things that no other Nova Schola profile teaches as clearly:

1. **Philosophy as practice, not system.** Marcus gives your child a *method*, not a doctrine. The *Meditations* are not an argument to follow but an exercise to perform.
2. **Duty as a higher form of freedom.** Marcus did not want to be emperor. He did it anyway, for nineteen years, with remarkable seriousness. In a culture that teaches children to "follow their passion," Marcus teaches them to *accept their post*.
3. **Power without corruption.** Marcus is the strongest ancient case that a well-formed person can hold absolute power without being corrupted by it. This matters for every child who will eventually hold some form of authority — parent, manager, citizen, voter.
4. **Gratitude as discipline.** Book I of the *Meditations* is a specific, named, practiced gratitude exercise. It is not a feeling. It is a list.
5. **The limits of formation.** Marcus's failure with his son Commodus is the honest counterweight to the Great Man framework. Even a philosopher-father with an empire's resources cannot guarantee a great son. Formation equips; it does not determine.

## Age-Appropriate Emphases

### Ages 8-10: The Serious Boy, the Notebook, Duty

**Core story:** The orphaned boy raised by his grandfather, the nickname "Verissimus," the philosopher's cloak at twelve, the adoption by Antoninus, the twenty-three years of watching, becoming emperor, the plague and the wars, the notebook in the tent, Commodus, death.

**Secondary stories:** The slave philosopher Epictetus who taught the emperor. The co-emperorship with Lucius Verus. The refusal to look at Cassius's head.

**What to teach:**
- You can be serious even when you are little (the philosopher's cloak at twelve)
- Duty is sometimes more important than what you want
- Writing to yourself at night makes you better in the morning
- Gratitude is a list, not a feeling — list the people who formed you

**What to leave for later:**
- The persecution of Christians
- The complexities of the Faustina marriage
- Hadot's three disciplines of Stoic practice
- The theoretical structure of Stoicism as a philosophical system
- The *Meditations* in any volume — at this age, only the most concrete passages

**Success at this age looks like:** your child can tell the story of Marcus's life, has started a simple evening journal, has memorized at least one *Meditations* quotation, and can explain what "duty" means in their own words.

---

### Ages 11-14: The Journal, the Apprenticeship, the Limits of Formation

**Core story:** The double adoption by Hadrian, the twenty-three years with Antoninus Pius, the gift of Epictetus's *Discourses* from Rusticus, the co-emperorship, the plague and wars, the writing practice, the Avidius Cassius response, the failure with Commodus.

**Secondary stories:** The Avidius Cassius revolt as Stoic practice. The three disciplines (judgment, desire, action) at introductory level. Selling Faustina's jewelry to pay for grain.

**What to teach:**
- The Stoic dichotomy of control (what is in my power vs. what is not)
- The evening journal practice as sustained discipline (a full month, not just a week)
- Duty over preference as a form of strength
- Imitation of a living model (twenty-three years with Antoninus) beats any book
- Formation has limits — even Marcus failed with Commodus

**What to complicate:**
- The persecution of Christians (introduce as Marcus's cultural blindness; parallel with Aristotle on slavery)
- Stoicism's failure mode (coldness vs. equanimity)
- The co-emperorship (why share power you could have alone?)
- The choice to leave the empire to Commodus (was it forced or freely chosen?)

**Success at this age looks like:** your child has maintained an evening journal for at least one month, can articulate the dichotomy of control in their own words, has read *Meditations* Book I and written their own gratitude list, and can engage the Commodus question with honest uncertainty.

---

### Ages 15-18: Stoic Practice, the Philosopher-King, the Hard Questions

**Core story:** The full philosophical biography. The formation by a field of models (Book I as system). The relationship to Epictetus as philosophical operating system. The three disciplines of assent, desire, and action. The reign as philosophical laboratory. The Avidius Cassius response as Stoic practice in action. The *Meditations* as *askēsis* (spiritual exercise). The failures: Christians, Commodus, coldness, unworthy friends.

**What to teach:**
- The *Meditations* as a living, practicable philosophical text, not a historical artifact
- Hadot's three disciplines as an actual framework for daily life
- Power without corruption as a real historical possibility (counter to Acton's law)
- Duty as freedom in its highest form
- The limits of formation as a permanent Nova Schola humility
- Stoicism's genuine value and its genuine failure modes, held in both hands

**What to engage head-on:**
- The persecution of Christians (cultural blindness in a good man — and what is ours?)
- The Faustina questions (what was the marriage actually like? Where is the evidence?)
- The choice of Commodus (freely chosen; therefore culpable; but was it avoidable?)
- The Stoic critique (suppression vs. practice; where does equanimity shade into coldness?)
- Plato's philosopher-king vs. Marcus's actual reign (did the thought experiment work?)

**Success at this age looks like:** your teenager has read substantial portions of the *Meditations* and at least selections of Epictetus, has maintained the practice over multiple months, has written at least one serious essay on Marcus (preferred topic: Plato's philosopher-king vs. Marcus's reality), and can hold the virtues and flaws of Marcus simultaneously without collapsing into either hagiography or dismissal.

## Core Practices (Cross-Age)

Marcus is the first figure in the Nova Schola sequence who offers **specific, imitable daily practices**. Use them.

### 1. The Evening Journal

This is *the* Marcus practice. Start it this week.

- A small notebook, used only for this.
- Every night, three sections:
  - **What did I do well today?** (Specific. Not "I was good." Rather: "I helped with the dishes without being asked.")
  - **What did I do badly today?** (Specific. Not "I was bad." Rather: "I yelled at my sister when she interrupted me.")
  - **What will I try to do better tomorrow?** (Specific. Not "Be good." Rather: "When my sister interrupts me, I will take a breath before I respond.")
- No one else reads it. This is essential. The privacy is what makes honesty possible.

**Parental note:** You should not read your child's journal. Not ever. If your child knows you might, the practice dies — it becomes performance, not self-examination. The whole value of the practice is that it is for the writer alone.

### 2. The Morning Meditation

Before rising, rehearse the day mentally. Name the likely difficulties. Commit to the response.

The model is *Meditations* II.1:
> *"Begin the morning by saying to yourself, I shall meet with the busybody, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial..."*

For a child, this might be:
> *"Today I will probably meet someone who is rude. I will probably be tired or bored at some point. I might not want to do my homework. I will still do my work. I will still be kind. I will still be the kind of person I want to be."*

Do this before getting out of bed. Thirty seconds. Every morning.

### 3. The Book I Exercise

This is a one-time (or annual) practice. Once, carefully, name the people who have formed you and the specific virtue each one taught you.

- At least ten named individuals
- One specific virtue or practice per person
- Written in full sentences: *"From my grandfather I learned X."*

Marcus's own Book I runs for sixteen-plus individuals. Your child's list can be shorter but should follow the form.

This can be repeated annually on the child's birthday. Each year the list grows. By the time they are twenty, they will have a living record of their own formation — and they will have practiced the habit of gratitude specifically and concretely rather than vaguely.

### 4. The Dichotomy of Control Drill (Ages 11+)

When something upsets your child, ask them two questions:

1. **Is this in my power?**
2. **What is the part of it that is in my power?**

Example: A test score was lower than they wanted. "Is the score itself in your power now? No, it is fixed. What part is in your power? How I prepare for the next test. How I respond to this one."

This is not rationalizing. It is redirecting emotional energy from what cannot be changed to what can. Repeated over years, it becomes a reflex that serves the child for life.

## Common Pitfalls in Teaching Marcus

### Pitfall 1: Making Stoicism Grim

The caricature of Stoicism is emotional suppression — the stiff upper lip, the denial of feeling. This is the failure mode of Stoicism, not its essence.

Stoicism is not the refusal to feel. It is the refusal to let feelings govern. Marcus felt the cold on the Danube. He felt the grief when Faustina died. He felt the strain of being on campaign at fifty-five when he would rather have been reading. The *Meditations* record these feelings and redirect them toward action.

When you teach Marcus, make sure your child understands that the point is not to become emotionally numb. The point is to become emotionally *clear*: to see what is in your power and what is not, and to invest yourself in the first category only.

### Pitfall 2: Hagiography

The temptation is to present Marcus as perfect. He was not. The Christian persecutions happened on his watch. Commodus was his catastrophic failure. Some of his tolerance of unworthy friends shaded into evasion.

Teach the flaws along with the virtues. Nova Schola's formation thesis is strengthened, not weakened, by honesty about the limits of even the best figures. A flawless Marcus is less useful than a real Marcus.

### Pitfall 3: Skipping the Biography for the Ethics

The *Meditations* are extraordinary. But they make much more sense after your child knows the story — the serious boy, the adoption, the twenty-three years with Antoninus, the plague, the wars in the snow. The text is dense; the biography makes it intelligible.

Teach the life first. The text is then commentary on a life, not disconnected philosophy.

### Pitfall 4: Treating the Notebook Practice as Optional

The single most important thing Marcus teaches is the evening journal. If your child leaves this profile without starting the practice, the profile has underperformed. Every other lesson in Marcus is intellectual content. The journal is formation.

Make the journal non-negotiable. Not as homework — as *the* practice. Model it yourself if you can.

### Pitfall 5: Ignoring Commodus

The story is not complete without Commodus. Every version of the Marcus story that ends at the emperor's death is incomplete. The failure with the son is essential because it tells the truth about formation: even the best of it can fail when the student refuses.

Teach this explicitly. It is the permanent Nova Schola humility.

## Suggested Reading for Parents

Before teaching this profile, parents should ideally:

1. Read **Book I of the *Meditations*** in a good translation (Hays, Hammond, or Gill). One hour. This single book gives you the formation-by-many-teachers model that shaped Marcus.

2. Read **Books II, IV, and VIII** as a sampler. Another one to two hours. You will now have the flavor of the whole.

3. Read at minimum the **first chapter of Pierre Hadot's *The Inner Citadel*** (if you can get a copy). Hadot transforms how you read Marcus. He is not optional for serious engagement with this figure.

4. Optionally, read **Donald Robertson's *How to Think Like a Roman Emperor*** — a popular, practical introduction that is especially useful if you have never engaged Stoicism before. It makes the practice operational for modern life.

Total prep time: four to six hours. Given that this profile can serve your family for a year or more, the investment is trivial.

## The Connection to the Wider Sequence

Marcus is Day 5 in the Nova Schola sequence. He connects to what came before and what is coming:

- **Connection to Leonardo:** Both figures combined wide-ranging intellect with disciplined practice. Leonardo's notebooks and Marcus's *Meditations* are cousin documents — each a record of a great man reasoning privately on the page.
- **Connection to Alexander:** Marcus is the philosopher-king Aristotle tried and partly failed to make Alexander into. The contrast between Alexander and Marcus is one of the most illuminating in the ancient world: both had enormous power, both were educated philosophically, both changed history. One wept for more worlds to conquer. The other wrote in a tent about how to meet difficult people with kindness.
- **Connection to Plato:** Marcus is the historical test of Plato's philosopher-king thought experiment. The *Republic* imagined it; Marcus lived it. Teach these two together if possible.
- **Connection to Aristotle:** The failure with Commodus mirrors Aristotle's failure with Alexander. The lesson is the same: formation equips, it does not determine.

Forward connections (figures still to come): Marcus is a foundational figure for understanding later Christian synthesis (the moral seriousness of Stoicism feeds directly into figures like Augustine and Boethius), for the Renaissance rediscovery of the classics (Erasmus, Galileo), and for the Enlightenment examination of virtue and power (Jefferson, Washington, Frederick the Great). Marcus is the common reference point for every subsequent ruler who asks: *can I hold power and remain good?*

## If You Have Limited Time

If you can only do one thing with this profile, do this:

**Start the evening journal, as a family, this week. Your child writes theirs. You write yours. Nobody reads anyone else's. You do it every night for a month.**

That single practice, maintained, is worth more than any amount of reading about Marcus Aurelius. It is, in fact, *exactly* what Marcus did. The rest of the profile is context for the practice.

If you can do a second thing: read the biography together using the one-week lesson plan.

If you can do a third thing: memorize *Meditations* X.16 as a household line: *"Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one."*

---

## Warning: This Profile Changes Parents Too

A final note. If you take this profile seriously and begin the practices alongside your child, be warned: the evening journal will change you. The morning meditation will change you. The dichotomy-of-control drill will change you.

Marcus was not writing for children. He was writing for a tired, middle-aged man trying to be better. You are closer to his original audience than your child is. Do not be surprised if, three months in, you find that you have inherited something you did not expect.

That is the point. Nova Schola's thesis is not that children need formation. It is that humans need formation — and children are the ones we still have time to form deliberately. But formation is a family project. Marcus, perhaps more than any other figure in the sequence, teaches us that.

Begin tonight. Open a notebook. Write three sentences. Do it again tomorrow.

That is Marcus.

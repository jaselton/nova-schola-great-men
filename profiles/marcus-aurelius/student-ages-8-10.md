# Marcus Aurelius: Student Lesson (Ages 8-10)
## The Boy Who Did Not Want to Be King

---

## The Story

Almost nineteen hundred years ago, in the city of Rome, a little boy was born whose family was very rich and very important. His name was Marcus.

Marcus's father died when Marcus was only three years old. After that, Marcus was raised by his grandfather, who lived in a big house with marble floors and many servants. The grandfather was one of the most important men in Rome — he had been **consul** (a kind of chief senator) three different times.

Rome was ruled by an emperor. In those days the emperor was a man named **Hadrian**. Hadrian noticed little Marcus when Marcus was very small. He saw something serious and truthful about the boy. Hadrian gave him a nickname: **Verissimus** — which means "Most Truthful."

The emperor was watching.

---

### The Boy Who Chose to Sleep on the Floor

When Marcus was about twelve years old, he did something strange for a rich Roman boy.

He decided he wanted to be a **philosopher** — a person who tries, very seriously, to figure out how to live a good life.

Philosophers in ancient Rome often wore a rough wool cloak and lived plain lives, even if their families were rich. Marcus put on the rough cloak. He decided he would sleep on the hard floor instead of a comfortable bed.

His mother was worried that her son would get sick from the cold floor. So she made him sleep on a small cot, at least. But Marcus kept wearing the philosopher's cloak. He was twelve years old. He had already made up his mind about what kind of person he wanted to be.

---

### The Emperor's Plan

When Marcus was seventeen, Emperor Hadrian did something that changed Marcus's life forever.

Hadrian was getting old and sick. He had no son. He needed to pick the next emperor. He chose a kind, serious man named **Antoninus Pius** — and he told Antoninus, *"I will only make you the next emperor if you promise to adopt Marcus as your son, so that one day Marcus will be emperor after you."*

Antoninus agreed.

So, without having any choice about it, seventeen-year-old Marcus became the adopted son of the next emperor. He was now, officially, the future ruler of the Roman Empire.

**Here is the most important thing to understand: Marcus did not want this.**

He wanted to be a philosopher. He wanted to read, and think, and write. He did not want to run an empire of fifty million people. He did not want to command armies. But he had no choice. The emperor had decided. And Marcus came from a family that believed, very deeply, that when **duty** calls, you answer — even if you would rather be doing something else.

So Marcus said yes. And he moved into the imperial palace.

---

### Twenty-Three Years of Watching

From age seventeen to age forty, Marcus lived with Emperor Antoninus Pius. That is *twenty-three years* — probably more than twice as long as you have been alive.

For twenty-three years, Marcus watched a good man be a good emperor.

He watched Antoninus listen carefully before making decisions.
He watched Antoninus refuse to live in luxury.
He watched Antoninus be kind even to servants.
He watched Antoninus make the boring, careful, everyday decisions that good government requires.
He watched Antoninus *not* do the things most emperors did — no murdering rivals, no fancy palaces, no wars for glory.

Later, when Marcus became emperor, he would not have to *learn* how to be a good ruler. He had been watching one for twenty-three years.

---

### The Book That Changed Him

While Marcus was growing up, he had many teachers. One of them was named **Junius Rusticus**.

One day, Rusticus gave Marcus a very special gift: a book of the teachings of a philosopher named **Epictetus**.

Who was Epictetus? Here is the amazing thing: **Epictetus had been a slave**. He was not rich, not famous, not powerful. He was a slave who had become a great philosopher after he was freed.

Epictetus taught something simple and strong:

> *There are some things you can control, and some things you cannot control. The only thing you can truly control is your own mind — your own thoughts, your own choices. Everything else — money, health, what other people say about you — is not really in your power. If you are going to be happy, you must only care, really care, about the things you can control.*

Marcus read this book and read it and read it. For the rest of his life — almost fifty more years — he kept thinking about what Epictetus taught. A slave, it turns out, can teach an emperor the most important things in the world.

---

### When Marcus Became Emperor

When Marcus was forty years old, Antoninus died. Marcus was now the emperor of the biggest empire in the world.

He did something nobody expected.

Hadrian had wanted Marcus and Marcus's adopted brother, **Lucius Verus**, to *both* be emperors, together. The Senate tried to make Marcus the only emperor. But Marcus refused. He said, *"No. The plan was for both of us. My brother will be emperor with me."*

This had never happened in Rome before. Two emperors at the same time, with equal power. Marcus insisted because it was the right thing to do.

---

### Troubles, Troubles, Troubles

As soon as Marcus became emperor, terrible things started happening.

First, the river Tiber **flooded** and ruined all the grain stored in the city. People were starving.

Then, a powerful enemy called the **Parthians** invaded Roman lands in the east. Marcus sent his brother Lucius with the army. After four years of hard fighting, they won — but when the soldiers came home, they brought something terrible with them.

A **plague**.

The Antonine Plague — probably a disease called smallpox — spread all over the empire. It killed millions of people. Nobody knew how to cure it. Marcus stayed in Rome even when the plague was at its worst. He did not run away. He sold the empire's gold and his wife's jewels to buy grain for starving, sick people.

And *then*, while the plague was still killing people, Germanic tribes from the north — the **Marcomanni** and others — crossed the Danube River and invaded Italy itself.

The philosopher who had never wanted to be emperor now had to become a general.

---

### The Philosopher in the Snow

Marcus was almost fifty years old, and not very strong. But he packed up and went to the northern frontier. He spent the last ten years of his life mostly in military camps on the Danube River — in places that are now Austria and Hungary. The winters were freezing. The fighting was hard. The plague kept coming back.

And every night, in his tent, by candlelight, after the day's marches and battles and meetings, Marcus did something amazing.

**He wrote in a little notebook.**

He did not write a book for other people. He did not write to become famous. He wrote for **himself** — to remind himself, over and over, how to be a good man tomorrow.

He wrote things like:

- *"When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: today I will meet rude people and foolish people and mean people. They act this way because they do not know what is really good. I will not be angry at them. I will do my work anyway."*

- *"Don't live as if you had ten thousand years left. Death is close. While you are still alive, while you still can — be good."*

- *"Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one."*

Marcus never wanted anyone else to read these notes. They were for *him*, to make him better in the morning.

But, somehow, the notebook survived. After Marcus died, someone kept it. And then someone else kept it. And then someone else. For almost two thousand years, people have kept copying out that little notebook.

Today we call it **The Meditations**. And millions of people all over the world still read it — to learn how to be a better person tomorrow, the same way Marcus was trying to be a better person tomorrow.

---

### The Sad Ending

In the year 180, when Marcus was fifty-eight years old, he got very sick at his military camp. He knew he was going to die.

His last words, people say, were: *"Go to the rising sun. For I am already setting."*

And then something sad happened.

Marcus had a son. The son's name was **Commodus**, and Commodus was nineteen years old. Marcus loved his son very much, and he left the empire to him.

But Commodus was not like his father. Commodus had been taught philosophy, but he had not *listened*. He had been given the best teachers, but he had not paid attention. When he became emperor, he became one of the *worst* emperors Rome ever had. He was cruel. He was vain. He spent more time fighting as a gladiator in the arena, to show off, than running the empire.

Twelve years after Marcus died, Commodus was killed. The empire fell into a civil war.

This is very sad. It is also a very important lesson: **even the best father cannot force his son to be good**. Marcus had all the wisdom in the world. He could not give it to his son. His son had to *choose* to accept it — and his son did not choose.

---

## Why Marcus Matters

Marcus's life teaches us four big things:

### 1. You can be serious even when you are little.

Marcus put on the philosopher's cloak at twelve. He was already trying to be a good person before he was even a teenager. You do not have to wait until you are grown-up to begin.

### 2. Duty is sometimes more important than what you want.

Marcus wanted to be a philosopher. Instead, he had to be an emperor. He did not complain. He did his duty. Sometimes, in your life, you will have to do things you do not *feel* like doing — because it is the right thing, because your family needs it, because someone is counting on you. Marcus shows us that doing your duty, even when you do not want to, is a kind of strength.

### 3. The only things truly in your control are your own thoughts and choices.

You cannot control the weather. You cannot control what other people say. You cannot control whether people like you. What you *can* control is what you decide to do, how you react, what kind of person you try to be. That is where all your energy should go.

### 4. Writing to yourself at night makes you better in the morning.

You do not need to be an emperor to do what Marcus did. You only need a notebook. Every night, write three things:

- **What went well today.**
- **What I messed up today.**
- **What I want to do better tomorrow.**

That is the exact practice Marcus used. It is one of the most powerful habits in the history of the world.

---

## Things to Do This Week

**1. Start your Marcus notebook.** Get a small notebook. Every night for seven nights, write three sentences: what went well, what went badly, what you will try tomorrow. No one has to see it. It is for *you*.

**2. Memorize this line:**
> *"Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one."*
— Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*

**3. Try the morning meditation.** Before you get out of bed tomorrow, say quietly to yourself: *"Today I will probably meet someone who is rude, or tired, or unfair. I will still do my work. I will still be kind."* Then get up and try.

**4. Make your own gratitude list.** Make a list of five people who have helped form you — parents, grandparents, teachers, coaches, friends. Write one specific thing each person has taught you. This is what Marcus did in the first book of his *Meditations*.

**5. Pick one thing you do not *want* to do but that is your duty** — practicing an instrument, doing a chore, being kind to a sibling. Do it this week without complaining, the way Marcus did his duty without complaining.

---

## A Question to Think About

Marcus wrote in his notebook, almost every night, for ten years, when no one was looking. Why do you think writing something down — even when no one else will read it — might change the way you act?

---

## One Last Thing

There is a famous painting, and many statues, of Marcus Aurelius on his horse. He looks strong and serious, the way emperors are supposed to look.

But that is not the real Marcus.

The real Marcus is a tired man in a small tent, late at night, writing in a notebook by the light of an oil lamp, trying — one more time — to figure out how to be better tomorrow.

**That** is the Marcus to remember.

And every night, when you open your own notebook, you are doing exactly what he did.

# Aristotle: Student Lesson (Ages 15-18)
## Systems, Virtues, and the Limits of Formation

---

## The Central Problem

When you have spent twenty years absorbing the best education available — when you have been formed by the greatest teacher of your era, in the greatest school in the world — what do you owe that teacher? And what do you owe the truth?

Aristotle's answer was unambiguous: you owe the teacher respect, gratitude, and honest engagement. You owe the truth everything else. If the two conflict, truth wins.

This is not an easy position to hold. Intellectual loyalty to a mentor is one of the strongest forces in academic life. The temptation to defend your teacher's system, to smooth over its problems, to build within its framework even when you can see its cracks — this temptation is real and powerful. Most students yield to it. The history of philosophy is littered with schools that decayed into orthodoxy because the students could not bring themselves to say: *the teacher was wrong about this*.

Aristotle said it. He said it publicly, carefully, and with extraordinary precision. The result was not the destruction of Plato's legacy. It was its *completion*. Aristotle took what was right in Plato — the ethical seriousness, the political ambition, the commitment to systematic inquiry — and corrected what was wrong — the otherworldly metaphysics, the insufficient attention to empirical evidence, the resistance to systematic organization.

Your question in this lesson:

> **When you find that your formation — the education, the values, the intellectual habits you have absorbed — contains genuine errors, what do you do? How do you honor the formation while correcting its failures? And what are the limits of what formation can achieve?**

## The Philosophical Architecture

### The Critique of Forms

Aristotle's critique of Plato's theory of Forms is one of the most important intellectual events in Western history. It is also a model of how to disagree with a teacher.

**Plato's claim:** Universal concepts (justice, beauty, triangularity, horseness) exist as independent entities — the Forms — in a separate realm. Particular things (this just act, this beautiful sunset, this triangle, this horse) are copies or participants in the Forms. True knowledge is knowledge of the Forms, not of particulars.

**Aristotle's critiques** (primarily in *Metaphysics* Books I, VII, and XIII):

1. **The Third Man Argument.** If a horse resembles the Form of Horse, then there must be a third thing — a "super-Form" — that explains the resemblance between the horse and the Form. And then a fourth thing to explain the resemblance between the horse, the Form, and the super-Form. And so on to infinity. The theory generates an infinite regress.

2. **The separation problem.** How can the Form of Horse, which exists in a separate realm, be *in* this horse? How can something absent from the physical world explain the physical world? Plato's language of "participation" and "imitation" names the relationship but does not explain it.

3. **The explanatory problem.** The Forms do not add explanatory power. Saying "this horse is a horse because it participates in the Form of Horse" tells us nothing we did not already know. It merely gives the question a name.

4. **The scientific problem.** If the Forms are what we should study, then empirical investigation of the natural world is secondary or irrelevant. But Aristotle's biological work had shown him that enormous amounts of real knowledge are available through careful observation of actual organisms. A metaphysics that devalues observation is a metaphysics that blocks science.

**Aristotle's alternative:** The form of a thing is not in a separate realm. It is **in the thing itself**. The form of this horse — its structure, its functional organization, its "horseness" — is in this horse, making it what it is. You learn about forms by studying actual things, not by trying to glimpse a separate world.

This position — which philosophers call *hylomorphism* (from *hylē*, matter, and *morphē*, form) — says that every physical object is a compound of matter and form. The marble is the matter; the statue's shape is the form. The body is the matter; the soul is the form. You cannot have form without matter (there is no shapeless statue), and you cannot have organized matter without form (there is no soul without a body).

The implications are immense:

- **For science:** Study the physical world directly. The forms are in the things. Biology, physics, and observation become central to philosophy.
- **For psychology:** The soul is the form of the body — not a separate substance trapped in a material prison (Plato's image), but the organizing principle that makes a living body alive. This is a radically different view of human nature.
- **For ethics:** The good life is achievable *in this world*, through the development of virtues in actual human communities. You do not need to escape the physical world to find the good.

### The *Nicomachean Ethics*: Virtue, Happiness, and the Good Life

The *Nicomachean Ethics* is probably the most important single work of moral philosophy in the Western tradition. Its central question: **what is the good life for a human being?**

**The argument, in outline:**

1. **Every action aims at some good.** When you eat, you aim at nourishment. When you study, you aim at knowledge. When you work, you aim at money (which aims at security, which aims at...). There is a chain of goods, each serving the next.

2. **There must be a highest good** — a final end that is desired for its own sake, not as a means to something else. Aristotle calls this *eudaimonia* — usually translated "happiness," but better rendered as "flourishing" or "living well and doing well."

3. **Eudaimonia is not pleasure, not wealth, not honor.** These are goods, but none is self-sufficient. Pleasure without virtue is empty. Wealth without good use is pointless. Honor depends on others' opinions.

4. **Eudaimonia is "activity of the soul in accordance with virtue."** The flourishing human life is one in which you *act well* — exercising the virtues (courage, justice, temperance, practical wisdom, generosity, etc.) in the context of a complete life, in a community, with sufficient external goods.

5. **Virtue is a *hexis* — a stable disposition or habit.** You are not born virtuous. You become virtuous through practice. Virtue is the mean between excess and deficiency, found through practical wisdom (*phronesis*).

6. **Practical wisdom (*phronesis*) is the master virtue.** It is the ability to perceive the specific features of a situation and respond appropriately. It cannot be reduced to rules. It requires experience, reflection, and good judgment.

7. **The virtuous life requires a community.** "Man is by nature a political animal." Virtue cannot be exercised in isolation. The just person needs a community in which to be just. The generous person needs recipients. The courageous person needs dangers to face alongside companions.

**The key insight:** Aristotle's ethics is not a system of *rules* (like Kant's later ethics) or a system of *calculations* (like utilitarianism). It is a system of *character*. The question is not "what should I do in this situation?" but "what kind of person should I be?" If you have the right character — formed through habit, guided by practical wisdom — you will do the right thing in most situations, because doing right will be your nature.

This is why Aristotle spends so much time on *education* and *habituation*. The ethical question is not primarily "what is the right action?" but "how do we form people who reliably choose the right action?" The answer: through practice, community, and the right kind of formation — exactly the concerns that drive Nova Schola.

### The *Politics*: Man as Political Animal

The *Politics* opens with one of the most consequential claims in Western thought:

> *"Man is by nature a political animal."*

What Aristotle means: human beings are not designed for solitary life. A human who lives outside of a political community is "either a beast or a god." The *polis* — the city-state — is not a human invention. It is a natural development of human sociality: from the household, to the village, to the city.

The *Politics* then analyzes:

- **Types of government** — monarchy, aristocracy, polity (and their corruptions: tyranny, oligarchy, democracy)
- **The conditions for political stability** — a large middle class is essential; extreme wealth inequality is politically destabilizing
- **The role of education** — the city must educate its citizens in virtue, because the stability of the regime depends on the character of the citizens
- **Slavery** — the most problematic section, in which Aristotle argues that some people are "natural slaves"

The slavery discussion requires honest engagement. Aristotle's argument: some human beings lack the deliberative capacity for self-governance and are better off (and happier) under the direction of a master who possesses that capacity. The natural slave has the physical strength for labor but not the rational capacity for self-direction.

This argument is wrong. It is demonstrably, historically, morally wrong. And yet it is not a random error. It emerges directly from Aristotle's method: he begins from observed practice (*endoxa*), and every society he observed practiced slavery. Starting from contaminated data, he reached a contaminated conclusion.

Here is the philosophically productive part: **Aristotle's own framework undermines his own conclusion**. If the purpose of the city is *eudaimonia* — the flourishing of its members — and if slaves are members of the household (which Aristotle acknowledges), then denying them the conditions for flourishing contradicts the city's purpose. Later thinkers (the Stoics, the Christian philosophers, the Enlightenment abolitionists) would use exactly this Aristotelian logic to argue against slavery.

The lesson is not "Aristotle was uniquely bad." The lesson is: **even the most powerful philosophical method can be blinded by the assumptions of its era. The best defense is not to abandon the method but to apply it more honestly than its inventor did.**

### The *Poetics*: Literature as Knowledge

The *Poetics* is the first work of literary theory in the Western tradition. Its central claims:

1. **Poetry is *mimesis* — imitation or representation.** Art does not copy reality slavishly. It represents reality *selectively*, choosing what to show and how to show it.

2. **Tragedy represents human beings as "better than they are."** The tragic hero is noble, serious, and consequential. Tragedy elevates human action to reveal its deepest patterns.

3. **The structure of tragedy is the unity of plot.** A good tragedy has a beginning, middle, and end. Every event follows necessarily or probably from what precedes it. Nothing is included that does not contribute to the whole.

4. **Catharsis.** The purpose of tragedy is to arouse pity and fear in the audience and, through the experience of the drama, to *purge* (or clarify) those emotions. You leave the theater having experienced extreme emotion in a structured, meaningful way. This is emotionally and intellectually productive.

5. **Poetry is "more philosophical than history."** History tells what *happened*. Poetry tells what *could* happen, or what *would* happen given certain kinds of people in certain situations. Poetry reveals the universal through the particular.

The *Poetics* matters because it provides a framework for understanding *why stories work* and *what makes a good story*. Every subsequent Western theory of literature, film, and drama engages Aristotle's categories. If you want to understand why certain narratives move you and others don't, the *Poetics* is the place to start.

### Logic: The Invention of Thinking About Thinking

Aristotle did not merely think well. He codified *how* to think well into a formal discipline.

The *Organon* — Aristotle's six logical works — invented:

- **The syllogism** — the basic structure of deductive argument. "All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore Socrates is mortal." Aristotle identified all valid forms of this reasoning.
- **The categories of being** — ten fundamental ways of saying what something is (substance, quantity, quality, relation, place, time, etc.)
- **The distinction between demonstration and dialectic** — proof from certain premises vs. argument from probable premises
- **The theory of definition** — how to define a thing by genus and differentia (a triangle is a figure [genus] with three sides [differentia])

Logic as a formal discipline **did not exist before Aristotle**. After him, it was the foundation of all subsequent Western thought for over two thousand years. Kant, writing in the 18th century, claimed that logic had not needed to take a single step forward since Aristotle. (This was slightly too generous — but only slightly. It was not until Frege's *Begriffsschrift* in 1879 that logic genuinely advanced beyond the Aristotelian framework.)

## The Hard Questions

### The Limits of Formation: Aristotle and Alexander

Aristotle's most famous student was also his most problematic.

The standard narrative: Aristotle taught Alexander ethics, and Alexander became a world-conquering monster who ignored the teaching. The anti-standard narrative: Aristotle's formation was part of what made Alexander great, and his excesses were his own.

The truth is more complicated.

**What Aristotle's formation gave Alexander:**
- An intellectual framework for understanding his conquests as a civilizing mission (the spread of Greek culture, the founding of cities, the encouragement of learning)
- A genuine respect for knowledge — Alexander sent biological specimens back to Aristotle from the field, founded libraries, employed scholars
- The capacity for strategic thinking and long-term planning that military genius alone does not explain
- A vocabulary for self-reflection that Alexander occasionally used and occasionally ignored

**What Aristotle's formation did not give Alexander:**
- The habitual moderation that the *Nicomachean Ethics* describes as the core of virtue
- The practical wisdom to resist the corrupting effects of absolute power
- The emotional stability to prevent outbursts like the killing of Cleitus
- The humility to resist demanding divine honors

**Why?** Several factors:

1. **Time.** Aristotle had Alexander for three years (ages 13–16). The Academy had Aristotle for twenty. Three years is not enough to form a character against the pressures Alexander would face.

2. **Power.** Alexander became king at twenty and supreme commander of the largest military force in the world shortly after. No formation, however good, can fully prepare a twenty-year-old for unlimited power. Aristotle's own ethics acknowledges this: virtue requires *practice* in a *community* with *appropriate constraints*. Alexander had none of these after Gaugamela.

3. **Culture.** As Alexander moved east, he absorbed Persian court culture — which encouraged divine kingship, absolute authority, and spectacle. This pulled against everything Aristotle had taught about moderation and political equality.

4. **Nature.** Alexander was extraordinary — extraordinary talent, extraordinary ambition, extraordinary emotional intensity. Extraordinary natures are harder to moderate than ordinary ones. The golden mean for Alexander was, objectively, a harder target to hit.

**The lesson:** Formation is not fate. Even the best teacher, working with the most talented student, produces a person who must still *choose* to use what they learned. The teacher's responsibility is to provide the best possible intellectual and moral equipment. The student's responsibility is to use it. When the student fails, the teacher has not necessarily failed — but the teacher must also ask: *what could I have done differently?*

### The Slavery Blindness: Method and Morality

Aristotle's defense of natural slavery is the clearest example in the Western canon of a great philosopher being catastrophically wrong on a moral question.

The philosophical interest is not in rehearsing why he was wrong (he was). It is in understanding *how* he went wrong — because the mechanism is repeatable, and you may be subject to the same failure mode.

**Aristotle's method:** Begin from received opinion (*endoxa*) — what most people believe, what the wise believe, what seems obviously true. Then analyze, refine, and systematize.

**The problem:** If received opinion is contaminated by systemic injustice, beginning from received opinion will systematize the injustice rather than critique it. Aristotle observed that every society he knew practiced slavery. He observed that slaves performed certain roles. He concluded that these roles reflected natural capacities. The method worked flawlessly. The data was poisoned.

**The deeper problem:** Aristotle had the philosophical tools to avoid this error. His *own* principle — that the purpose of the city is the *eudaimonia* of its members — logically entails that denying flourishing to a class of members undermines the city's purpose. But he did not apply this principle to slaves. He exempted them from the category of "full members" by defining them as naturally lacking the relevant capacity.

This is the pattern: **when a philosophical system encounters a practice the philosopher benefits from, the system tends to generate an exemption rather than a critique.** Aristotle benefited from slave labor (as all Athenian citizens did). His system, predictably, exempted slavery from its general principles.

**The question for you:** What practices in *your* world are so universal that you take them for granted? What received opinions (*endoxa*) have you absorbed without examination? Aristotle's method is powerful — but its power depends on the quality of the starting data. If you begin from unexamined assumptions, you will systematize those assumptions rather than questioning them.

The students in this course who engage this question honestly — who actually name specific assumptions they might be wrong about — will have done something that Aristotle himself, on this topic, could not.

### Socrates vs. Aristotle: Stay or Flee?

When charged with impiety, Socrates stayed, stood trial, and died. When charged with impiety, Aristotle fled.

Both decisions had logic behind them:

**Socrates' logic (in the *Crito*):** The laws of Athens have formed me; I have benefited from them for seventy years; I have implicitly consented to their authority by remaining in Athens; to flee now, when the laws have reached an unjust verdict in my case, would be to betray the entire relationship. Better to die unjustly than to live as a lawbreaker.

**Aristotle's logic:** Socrates' death produced a martyr and a political crisis. Another philosophical execution would produce... another philosophical execution. Martyrdom serves philosophy only the first time. Better to preserve oneself (and one's work) than to repeat a gesture whose productive force has been spent. "Lest Athens sin twice against philosophy."

**The question:** Who was right?

Arguments for Socrates' choice:
- Principled acceptance of unjust punishment demonstrates the seriousness of the philosophical life
- Socrates' death produced Plato's response (the Academy), which produced Aristotle. The martyrdom was *generative*.
- Fleeing arguably confirms the charge — the fugitive looks guilty

Arguments for Aristotle's choice:
- Dying for a principle that has already been demonstrated is redundant
- Aristotle's survival preserved his ability to continue writing and teaching
- Pragmatic self-preservation allows you to fight another day

There may not be a single right answer. The question is worth holding open, because you will face versions of it: *when do I accept unjust consequences to make a point, and when do I withdraw to preserve my capacity to work?*

## Practices for Students (Ages 15-18)

### 1. The *Nicomachean Ethics* Reading

Read *Nicomachean Ethics* Books I, II, and X. (Available in any major translation — Irwin's Hackett edition is recommended.)

Write a 1,500-word essay on ONE of the following:

1. **"Eudaimonia is 'activity of the soul in accordance with virtue.'" What does this mean in practice? Is Aristotle's account of happiness superior to the accounts you encounter in contemporary culture (pleasure, success, self-expression)? Defend your answer.**

2. **"We become just by doing just acts." If virtue is a habit, does this mean there is no role for moral reasoning? Or is practical wisdom (*phronesis*) itself a kind of habit? How do habit and reflection interact in Aristotle's account?**

3. **In Book X, Aristotle argues that the highest form of happiness is *theōria* — contemplation or philosophical reflection. Is this convincing? Does it contradict the earlier books' emphasis on active virtue in the community? How do you reconcile the two claims?**

---

### 2. The *Politics* and the Slavery Question

Read *Politics* Book I (especially chapters 3-7, on slavery).

Write a 1,000-word paper answering:

> **Where exactly does Aristotle's argument for natural slavery go wrong? Identify the specific logical or empirical error. Then show how Aristotle's own philosophical framework — the principle that the city exists for the flourishing of its members — provides the resources to correct his error.**

This exercise is not about condemning Aristotle. It is about learning to use a philosopher's best principles to correct their worst conclusions. This is how intellectual traditions grow.

---

### 3. The Poetics Application

Read the *Poetics* (it is short — about 50 pages in most editions).

Choose a film, novel, or play you know well. Write a 1,500-word analysis applying Aristotle's framework:

- Does it achieve unity of plot?
- Is the protagonist "better than we are" (as Aristotle requires for tragedy)?
- Does the plot move through reversal (*peripeteia*) and recognition (*anagnorisis*)?
- Does it produce catharsis? What emotions does it arouse and purge?
- Would Aristotle consider this a good work of art? Do you agree with his judgment?

---

### 4. The System-Building Project

Aristotle's distinctive achievement was not any single insight but the *system* — the organized architecture of knowledge across multiple fields.

Choose a domain you know well — a sport, an art form, a hobby, a subject area, a social phenomenon. Build a systematic account of it, modeled on Aristotle's method:

1. **Define the domain.** What is it? What is its purpose (*telos*)?
2. **Classify its parts.** What are the major divisions? What categories organize the domain?
3. **Identify the excellences.** What does it look like when this domain is practiced well? What are the virtues specific to it?
4. **Identify the failure modes.** What goes wrong, and why?
5. **Propose a formation program.** How would you train someone from novice to expert in this domain?

Write this as a 2,000-word paper. You are doing what Aristotle did: taking a mass of experience and organizing it into transmissible knowledge.

---

### 5. The Formation Audit

Aristotle's life raises the question of formation's limits. His twenty years at the Academy formed him profoundly. His three years with Alexander formed Alexander partially. His own cultural formation left him blind to the injustice of slavery.

Conduct an audit of your own formation:

1. **Who and what formed you?** List the five most formative influences on your intellectual and moral development (people, institutions, books, experiences).
2. **What did each give you?** What intellectual habits, moral dispositions, or character traits did each influence contribute?
3. **What might each have gotten wrong?** Every formation has blind spots. What assumptions did you absorb uncritically? Where might you be the equivalent of Aristotle on slavery — applying your best method to contaminated data?
4. **What gaps remain?** What do you need that your formation did not provide? Who or what could provide it?

Write this as a 2,000-word self-assessment. Be honest. The quality of this paper depends entirely on your willingness to examine your own formation seriously.

---

### 6. The Observation Discipline

For one month, spend 20 minutes per day in systematic observation of something in the natural or human world. Choose one focus:

- A natural ecosystem (garden, park, pond)
- A social environment (school hallway, family dinner, public transit)
- Your own behavior patterns (emotional responses, decision-making, habits)

Keep a daily journal. Record what you observe — not what you think or feel, but what you *see*.

At the end of the month, write a 1,000-word synthesis: What patterns emerged? What surprised you? What would Aristotle have said about what you found?

This is the Aristotelian empirical discipline applied to your own life. The students who do this exercise well often report that it permanently changes how they see the world.

## Discussion Questions (Philosophical & Historical)

### On Plato and Aristotle
1. Aristotle said he was "a greater friend of truth" than of Plato. Is it possible to honor a teacher while rejecting their central philosophical claims? How do you know when disagreement is respectful and when it is betrayal?
2. Plato wrote dialogues; Aristotle wrote treatises. What is gained and lost in each form? Which form is better suited to which kind of truth?
3. The Academy lasted 900 years; the Lyceum lasted a few centuries. Does institutional longevity measure philosophical success? What else might measure it?

### On Ethics
4. Is Aristotle's virtue ethics superior to rule-based ethics (Kant) or consequentialist ethics (utilitarianism)? What can virtue ethics handle that the others cannot — and vice versa?
5. Aristotle says practical wisdom (*phronesis*) cannot be taught through rules — it must be developed through experience. If this is true, what are the implications for moral education?
6. The *Nicomachean Ethics* assumes that virtue requires a community and sufficient external goods (health, wealth, friends). Is a virtuous life possible for someone who lacks these? Is Aristotle too optimistic about the conditions for human flourishing?

### On Science and Method
7. Aristotle's biology was brilliantly observational. His physics was largely wrong (objects fall because they "seek their natural place"). What explains the difference? Is observation more reliable in some domains than others?
8. "Begin from received opinion" (*endoxa*) is Aristotle's philosophical starting point. When does this method work well, and when does it systematize prejudice?
9. Darwin credited Aristotle as the greatest biologist. Modern physicists often dismiss Aristotle's physics. How should we evaluate a thinker whose work is brilliant in some fields and wrong in others?

### On Formation and Power
10. Aristotle tutored Alexander for three years. Plato tried to tutor Dionysius for decades and failed. What made the difference? Was it the teacher, the student, or the circumstances?
11. Can you form a ruler? History's record is mixed. What conditions make mentor-of-the-powerful projects succeed, and what conditions doom them?
12. Aristotle fled Athens; Socrates stayed and died. Who was right? Does your answer change depending on your age, your circumstances, or the stakes?

## Parent / Teacher Guide

### What to Emphasize at This Age
- **The *Nicomachean Ethics* as a live philosophical resource.** This is not a historical artifact. It is one of the most powerful frameworks for thinking about the good life available today. Treat it as current philosophy, not ancient history.
- **The critique of Plato as a model of intellectual courage.** Disagreeing with your teacher publicly, fairly, and constructively is one of the hardest things an intellectual can do. Aristotle did it at the highest level.
- **The slavery blindness as a warning.** Every great thinker has blind spots produced by their era. The question "what am I wrong about?" is the most important question Aristotle's failure teaches.

### What to Complicate
- **The Alexander relationship.** The honest assessment is "partially succeeded, partially failed." Resist both the hagiographic reading (Aristotle made Alexander great) and the cynical reading (Aristotle's teaching was irrelevant). The truth is in between, and the in-between is where the learning happens.
- **The observation vs. theory tension.** Aristotle was right that observation matters. He was wrong that his physics (based on observation of everyday phenomena) could explain celestial mechanics. The lesson: observation is necessary but not sufficient. Theory and observation must check each other.
- **The relationship between virtue ethics and other ethical frameworks.** Aristotle is not the only game in town. A well-educated student should be able to articulate the strengths and weaknesses of virtue ethics relative to Kantian and utilitarian alternatives.

### Advanced Extensions
- Read Alasdair MacIntyre, *After Virtue* (chapters 12-15) — the most important modern revival of Aristotelian ethics
- Read Martha Nussbaum, *The Fragility of Goodness* (the Aristotle chapters) — a brilliant feminist engagement with Aristotle
- Read Armand Marie Leroi, *The Lagoon* — a modern biologist's account of Aristotle's biological work
- Read the *Poetics* and apply it to a film or play the student has recently seen
- Compare *Nicomachean Ethics* Book I with the opening of Mill's *Utilitarianism* — two competing accounts of happiness

### Writing Assignments (Choose One)
- The *Nicomachean Ethics* essay (Practice #1) — the most philosophically demanding
- The slavery analysis (Practice #2) — the most morally urgent
- The system-building project (Practice #4) — the most practically useful
- The formation audit (Practice #5) — the most personally transformative
- A 2,000-word essay: "Aristotle fled; Socrates stayed. A defense of one choice."
- A 2,000-word essay: "What Aristotle would say about my generation's moral formation"

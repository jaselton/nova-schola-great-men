# Aristotle: Parent Teaching Guide

---

## Overview: What This Profile Teaches

Aristotle is the figure who shows what a truly great education produces — not a copy of the teacher, but a thinker who completes the teacher's work by seeing what was missed and filling the gap. He is also the figure who demonstrates that the *form* of knowledge matters: unorganized brilliance is personal; organized brilliance is transmissible. Aristotle's system — logic, biology, ethics, politics, poetics, metaphysics — is transmissible in a way that Plato's dialogues, for all their beauty, are not.

This profile teaches four things that no other Nova Schola profile teaches as clearly:

1. **Completion over imitation.** The best student does not replicate the teacher's conclusions. They see the teacher's gaps and fill them. Aristotle honored Plato by disagreeing with him, not by defending him.
2. **Virtue as habit.** Aristotle's central ethical claim — that you become virtuous by practicing virtue, the way you become a musician by playing music — is the most practically useful moral insight in the Western tradition for children and families.
3. **The empirical discipline.** Looking at things carefully, classifying what you see, and building knowledge from observation is a practice every child can adopt. Aristotle invented it; your child can inherit it.
4. **The limits of formation.** Aristotle's partial failure with Alexander is the honest counterweight to the formation thesis. Even the best teacher cannot guarantee the student's choices. Formation equips; it does not determine.

## Age-Appropriate Emphases

### Ages 8-10: The Doctor's Son, Observation, Habits

**Core story:** Aristotle's medical-family background, the twenty years at the Academy, the biological research on Lesbos, tutoring Alexander, and the founding of the Lyceum.

**Secondary stories:** The habit of virtue (simplified). The difference between Plato and Aristotle ("Plato looked up, Aristotle looked around").

**What to teach:**
- Observation is a discipline — really looking at things reveals what casual glancing misses
- Virtue is a habit built through practice, not a decision made once
- The best students don't copy their teachers — they add what the teachers missed
- Organizing knowledge into a system makes it useful for others

**What to leave for later:**
- The theory of Forms vs. hylomorphism (too abstract)
- The *Nicomachean Ethics* in any detail beyond "virtue is a habit"
- The slavery question
- The *Politics* and *Poetics*
- The flight-from-Athens ethical dilemma

**Success at this age looks like:** your child can tell the story of Aristotle's life in their own words, can explain that "we are what we repeatedly do," has done at least one sustained observation exercise, and understands the basic difference between Plato's approach and Aristotle's approach.

---

### Ages 11-14: The Golden Mean, Classification, Honest Disagreement

**Core story:** The Plato-Aristotle disagreement, the golden mean, the biological work, the Alexander experiment, the Lyceum as system.

**Secondary stories:** The 158 constitutions. The invention of logic. The *Poetics*' analysis of storytelling.

**What to teach:**
- The golden mean is not compromise — it is precision (the right response to the specific situation)
- Virtue is built through practice over time (the 30-day virtue habit)
- Disagreeing with your teacher respectfully and honestly is one of the hardest intellectual acts
- Classification and systematic thinking are skills anyone can develop
- The Alexander experiment partially succeeded and partially failed — formation is not fate

**What to complicate:**
- The slavery question (introduce honestly; show how Aristotle's own framework undermines his conclusion)
- The Alexander experiment (engage the question of formation's limits)
- Theory vs. observation (don't present Aristotle as simply "better" than Plato)
- The Socrates-Aristotle comparison (stay vs. flee)

**Success at this age looks like:** your child can explain the golden mean with real examples, has completed a 30-day virtue habit exercise, can articulate the Plato-Aristotle disagreement fairly, and has done at least one substantial classification or observation project.

---

### Ages 15-18: The Full System, the Ethics, the Hard Questions

**Core story:** The *Nicomachean Ethics* in depth. The critique of Forms. The *Politics* (including slavery). The *Poetics*. Logic as invention. The Alexander question. The flight from Athens.

**What to teach:**
- The *Nicomachean Ethics* as a live philosophical resource, not a historical artifact
- The critique of Plato as a model of constructive intellectual disagreement
- The slavery blindness as a warning about the limits of even the best method
- System-building as an intellectual discipline
- The relationship between formation and freedom (you can equip a person; you cannot determine their choices)

**What to engage head-on:**
- The slavery question in full philosophical depth
- The Alexander question (partial success, partial failure — what does this mean for the formation thesis?)
- The Socrates-vs.-Aristotle choice (stay and die, or flee and live?)
- The relationship between Aristotelian virtue ethics and modern ethical frameworks (Kantian, utilitarian)
- The question "what am I blind to?" — Aristotle's most important lesson for the self-examining student

**Success at this age looks like:** your student has read substantial portions of the *Nicomachean Ethics*, can make a philosophical argument about virtue ethics, has engaged the slavery question honestly (using Aristotle's own framework to correct Aristotle), and has conducted a serious formation audit of their own intellectual and moral development.

## Common Misconceptions to Address

### Misconception 1: "Aristotle was just wrong about everything — his science has been replaced."

**Reality:** Aristotle's *physics* was eventually superseded. His *biology* was brilliantly accurate and unmatched for nearly 2,000 years. His *logic* dominated for 2,200 years. His *ethics* and *political philosophy* remain among the most important works in their fields. His *poetics* is still the foundation of literary criticism. Judging Aristotle only by his physics is like judging a great chef only by one failed dish.

### Misconception 2: "The golden mean means always being moderate."

**Reality:** The golden mean is not a preference for the middle of the road. It is the *right response to the specific situation* — and sometimes the right response is extreme. If your house is on fire, the golden mean of courage is not "moderately leaving the building." It is "getting everyone out as fast as possible." The mean requires *judgment*, not a formula. That is why practical wisdom (*phronesis*) is the hardest virtue.

### Misconception 3: "Aristotle betrayed Plato by disagreeing with him."

**Reality:** Aristotle spent twenty years at Plato's Academy — the longest philosophical apprenticeship in history. He learned Plato's method thoroughly before developing his alternative. His critiques of Plato are among the most respectful in the history of philosophy: he always states Plato's position fairly before arguing against it. Intellectual loyalty to a teacher does not mean intellectual agreement. The highest honor a student can give a teacher is serious, honest engagement.

### Misconception 4: "Aristotle failed with Alexander."

**Reality:** The assessment is mixed. Alexander was brilliant, cultured, strategically sophisticated, and genuinely interested in knowledge — all qualities that Aristotle's formation contributed to. Alexander was also wrathful, grandiose, and increasingly autocratic — qualities that Aristotle's formation could not fully prevent. The honest answer is "partially succeeded, partially failed." The important question is not "did Aristotle fail?" but "what are the conditions under which formation succeeds and fails?"

### Misconception 5: "Aristotle's philosophy is dry and boring."

**Reality:** The Aristotle we read is lecture notes — the equivalent of a professor's class outlines, not their published books. His published works (all lost) were reportedly beautiful. Cicero called his prose "a river of gold." The ideas within the lecture notes — the golden mean, virtue as habit, the analysis of tragedy, the classification of the natural world — are among the most powerful and practically useful in the history of thought.

## Discussion Facilitation Tips

### For Younger Students (8-10)

- **Start with the doctor's son.** The medical-family background is the key to Aristotle's character. Ask: *"What do you think dinner-table conversations were like in a doctor's house?"* This makes the abstract concrete.
- **Do the Observation Journal.** This is the single most important activity. Actually go outside together. Actually spend five minutes looking at one thing. Draw it. Label it. This is not homework — it is a formative experience.
- **Use the virtue-habit language immediately.** When your child does something kind, brave, or honest, say: *"You're building the habit of [virtue]. Aristotle would say you just got a little bit more [virtuous] by practicing."* This embeds the concept in daily life.

### For Middle Students (11-14)

- **Teach the golden mean through real scenarios.** Use situations the student actually faces: peer pressure, conflict with friends, study habits, physical training. For each one, identify the two extremes and the mean. Make them do the work of finding the mean themselves.
- **Do the 30-Day Virtue Habit.** This is the most transformative exercise in the profile. A month of deliberate virtue practice — with daily journaling — produces a *felt understanding* of Aristotle's central claim. The student who has done this exercise will never forget what "virtue as habit" means.
- **Introduce the slavery question honestly.** Say something like: *"Aristotle was one of the greatest thinkers in history, and he was catastrophically wrong about slavery. Let's look at how he went wrong — and then let's use his own best ideas to correct his worst mistake."* This teaches intellectual honesty and the practice of internal critique.

### For Older Students (15-18)

- **Assign primary source reading.** The *Nicomachean Ethics* (Books I, II, X) is non-negotiable. At this age, the student needs to read Aristotle's own words, not a summary. The experience of working through dense philosophical prose is itself formative.
- **Run the formation audit.** The exercise where students examine their own intellectual and moral formation — who formed them, what they absorbed, where they might be blind — is the most personally important exercise in this profile. Push for honesty.
- **Engage the Socrates-vs.-Aristotle comparison.** The "stay and die" vs. "flee and live" question has no clean answer. That is the point. Help the student hold the question open, weigh the arguments, and articulate their own position without premature closure.

## Sensitive Topics & How to Handle Them

### The Slavery Argument

Aristotle's defense of natural slavery is morally wrong and must be addressed honestly at all ages where it is introduced (11+).

**How to handle it:**

1. **State clearly that he was wrong.** Do not hedge. Natural slavery is indefensible. Aristotle was wrong.
2. **Explain how he went wrong.** His method — beginning from received opinion — worked against him when every received opinion was contaminated by ubiquitous slavery.
3. **Show how his own framework corrects the error.** The principle that the city exists for human flourishing logically undermines slavery. Later thinkers used Aristotelian logic to make anti-slavery arguments.
4. **Apply the lesson to the student.** Ask: *"What practices in our world are so universal that we assume they are natural? What might we be wrong about?"*

This approach models intellectual honesty without either dismissing Aristotle or excusing him.

### The Alexander Failure

The partial failure of Aristotle's formation of Alexander is the honest counterweight to the formation thesis. Handle it by:

1. **Acknowledging both success and failure.** Alexander was brilliant, cultured, and intellectually curious (success). He was also wrathful, grandiose, and increasingly autocratic (failure).
2. **Analyzing the factors.** Time (only three years), power (unlimited from age twenty), culture (Persian court norms), nature (extraordinary temperament).
3. **Drawing the lesson.** Formation equips; it does not determine. The teacher gives the tools; the student chooses whether to use them.
4. **Asking the student.** "If you were Aristotle, what would you have done differently? Is there anything that could have changed the outcome?"

### The Women Question

Aristotle's views on women's subordination are as problematic as his views on slavery, though less central to his system. In the *Politics* and *Generation of Animals*, he argues that women possess rational capacity but in an inferior or incomplete form.

Handle this the same way as the slavery argument: state that he was wrong, explain the methodological error (generalizing from contaminated cultural data), and show how his own framework (human flourishing as the purpose of the city) undermines the conclusion.

At ages 15+, note that the Academy — Plato's school — had women students (Axiothea and Lastheneia), while the Lyceum has no record of female members. Plato was more progressive on this point than his student.

### The Flight from Athens

Aristotle's decision to flee Athens rather than face trial (as Socrates had done) is a genuine moral question, not a settled case. Both choices had reasons. Facilitate this as a discussion, not a lesson with a predetermined answer.

Key point: help the student see that both Socrates' and Aristotle's choices were *reasoned*. Socrates argued from principle (obligation to the laws). Aristotle argued from consequence (preventing a repetition of philosophical martyrdom). The student should articulate their own position and defend it.

## Extension Activities (Beyond Core Lessons)

### Field Trips & Experiences
- Visit a natural history museum. Focus on the classification systems used for animals and plants. Ask: *"How does this connect to what Aristotle was doing 2,300 years ago?"*
- Visit a botanical garden. Practice Aristotelian observation: draw specimens, note characteristics, try to classify plants by visible features before reading the labels.
- Attend a play or film together. Apply the *Poetics*: does the plot have unity? Is there reversal and recognition? Does it produce catharsis?
- Visit a university campus and notice the departmental structure. Ask: *"Is this how Aristotle would have organized knowledge? What would he change?"*

### Additional Reading

**For 8-10:**
- Nature documentaries (particularly David Attenborough) with discussion of classification
- A picture-book biography of Aristotle (several exist — preview for quality)

**For 11-14:**
- Armand Marie Leroi, *The Lagoon* (selected chapters — the biological fieldwork on Lesbos)
- Bryan Magee, *The Story of Philosophy* (Aristotle chapter)
- Selections from the *Historia Animalium* — descriptions of marine life

**For 15-18:**
- Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* (Irwin translation, Hackett) — required
- Aristotle, *Poetics* (Sachs translation, Focus) — short and rewarding
- Aristotle, *Politics* Book I — including the slavery argument
- Armand Marie Leroi, *The Lagoon* (full) — a modern biologist's account
- Alasdair MacIntyre, *After Virtue* (chapters 12-15) — the modern Aristotelian revival
- Martha Nussbaum, *The Fragility of Goodness* (Aristotle chapters)

### Project Ideas

1. **The Family Observation Project.** Choose a natural phenomenon together — a garden through the seasons, a bird feeder's visitors, the weather over a month. Track it systematically. At the end, classify and analyze what you found. You are doing Aristotelian science as a family.

2. **The Golden Mean Workshop.** Once a week at dinner, name a virtue and identify its two extremes together. Then discuss: where is the mean *for each family member* — because the mean is relative to the person and situation, not a fixed point.

3. **The Classification Game.** Each family member picks a domain (music, movies, sports, books, foods) and builds a classification system for it. Share systems. Critique them. Revise them. Notice how systematic thinking clarifies.

4. **The *Poetics* Film Night.** Watch a film together. Before discussing anything, each person answers five questions from the *Poetics* framework: What is the plot? Is there unity? What is the reversal? What is the recognition? Did it produce catharsis? Compare answers.

5. **The Virtue Habit Challenge.** The whole family picks one virtue and practices it for thirty days together. Daily check-ins at dinner. End-of-month reflection. This is the most important Aristotelian practice available to a household.

## Integration with Other Subjects

### How Aristotle Connects to Your Curriculum

**Science / Biology**
- The founding of empirical biology
- Classification systems (Linnaean taxonomy descends from Aristotelian categories)
- The scientific method — observation, classification, generalization
- Darwin's acknowledgment of Aristotle as the greatest biologist

**Philosophy / Ethics**
- Virtue ethics (the dominant ethical framework in the ancient world, revived in modern philosophy)
- The golden mean
- Practical wisdom (*phronesis*)
- The relationship between happiness and virtue
- The critique of Plato's Forms

**Literature / Language Arts**
- The *Poetics* — the first work of literary criticism
- Plot structure, catharsis, mimesis
- The analysis of tragedy
- Aristotle's influence on all subsequent literary theory

**Mathematics / Logic**
- The invention of formal logic
- The syllogism
- The structure of valid argument
- The relationship between logic and mathematics

**History / Social Studies**
- The 158 constitutions project
- The political philosophy of the *Politics*
- Aristotle's relationship to Alexander and the Hellenistic world
- The transmission of Aristotle through Islamic and Christian philosophy

**Government / Civics**
- "Man is by nature a political animal"
- Types of government (monarchy, aristocracy, polity and their corruptions)
- The role of education in forming citizens
- The slavery argument and its refutation

## Assessment: How Do You Know They've Learned?

### Younger Students (8-10)

1. Can tell the story of Aristotle's life — from Stagira to the Academy to Lesbos to Macedon to the Lyceum
2. Can explain that "we are what we repeatedly do" in their own words
3. Has completed at least one observation journal
4. Can explain the basic difference between Plato and Aristotle ("Plato looked up, Aristotle looked around")
5. Can name the mentor chain: Socrates → Plato → Aristotle → Alexander

### Middle Students (11-14)

1. Can explain the golden mean with at least three real-life examples
2. Has completed the 30-day virtue habit exercise
3. Can articulate the Plato-Aristotle disagreement fairly (stating both positions before evaluating)
4. Has completed a substantial classification or observation project
5. Can explain why Aristotle's formation of Alexander was "mixed" — neither a complete success nor a complete failure
6. Can identify at least one assumption in their own thinking that might be the equivalent of Aristotle's slavery blindness

### Older Students (15-18)

1. Has read substantial portions of the *Nicomachean Ethics* and can articulate its central argument
2. Can make a philosophical argument about virtue ethics' strengths and weaknesses
3. Has engaged the slavery question honestly — using Aristotle's own framework to correct his error
4. Has conducted a formation audit and can articulate their own intellectual and moral blind spots
5. Can articulate the difference between Socrates' and Aristotle's responses to prosecution and defend their own position
6. Has produced at least one substantial piece of systematic work (classification, system-building, or analysis)

## What Success Looks Like

### Short-term (During the lesson/week)
- Your child asks "what's the golden mean here?" when facing a dilemma
- They observe something carefully that they would previously have glanced at
- They start tracking a virtue habit
- They mention Aristotle's disagreement with Plato as an example of how disagreement should work

### Medium-term (Month after)
- The virtue habit has become... a habit
- They spontaneously classify or systematize things in their environment
- They reference the golden mean in moral reasoning
- They ask "what am I blind to?" — not as a performance, but as a genuine question
- They have read at least one primary source (even a short one)

### Long-term (Year+ after)
- They practice virtue as habit — daily, unremarkable, steady
- They approach knowledge systematically — organizing what they learn, not just accumulating it
- They disagree with authority figures (including you) respectfully and substantively
- They recognize when observation, not theory, is the right starting point — and vice versa
- They hold the "formation audit" question open permanently: *what did I absorb uncritically, and where am I blind?*

## Final Thoughts for Parents

### What Aristotle Really Teaches

Aristotle teaches the most practical lesson in the entire Great Man curriculum: **character is built through practice, not through decision**. This is not a philosophical abstraction. It is the most actionable claim in the Western ethical tradition, and it is available to a six-year-old and a sixty-year-old alike.

If your child absorbs nothing else from this profile, let them absorb this: *you are building your character right now, with every choice you make. Every small act of courage makes you braver. Every small act of honesty makes you more truthful. Every small act of patience makes you calmer. You are not waiting to become the person you will be. You are becoming that person today.*

Aristotle also teaches the harder lesson: **even the best method has blind spots**. He looked at the world more carefully than anyone before him and still defended slavery, because the world he looked at practiced slavery universally. The cure is not to distrust observation (Aristotle's method was sound) but to observe *more widely* and to question *more honestly*.

The student who can do both — build character through practice *and* question their own assumptions honestly — is the student who has truly learned from Aristotle. That student has completed Aristotle's work in the same way Aristotle completed Plato's.

### The Mentor Chain

Place the mentor chain on your wall:

**Socrates → Plato → Aristotle → Alexander**

Four figures. Four profiles. Each one formed by the one before, each one building something the predecessor could not. Socrates taught the method. Plato built the institution. Aristotle systematized the knowledge. Alexander carried the culture to the edge of the world.

Your child is now ready to see the chain whole. They have studied each link. They have seen how formation works — and how it sometimes fails. They have absorbed the Socratic question (examine your life), the Platonic response (build an institution), the Aristotelian method (look at things, practice virtue, organize knowledge), and the Alexandrian ambition (carry what you've learned as far as you can).

The chain does not end with Alexander. It continues through Marcus Aurelius, Cicero, Augustine, Aquinas, and every subsequent figure in this curriculum. But the foundation is laid. Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Alexander — the rest is elaboration on what these four established.

Teach it well. The chain extends to your child.

# Aristotle: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Aristotle to one or more children. It is designed to be used by a parent or teacher at home, with no specialized materials. Total time commitment: roughly **45–75 minutes per day**, divisible into morning and afternoon sessions.

The plan is calibrated for **ages 8-14** as its default. Extensions and substitutions for ages 15-18 are marked throughout.

The week moves through: **The Doctor's Son → The Art of Looking → The Student Who Disagreed → The Habit of Virtue → The System and the Legacy**. This follows the developmental logic of Aristotle's life: observation first, formation second, independence third, practice fourth, system fifth.

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for the child (the "Aristotle notebook" for the week)
- Pencils, pens, colored pencils or markers
- A magnifying glass (optional but excellent)
- Access to an outdoor space — garden, park, yard, or even a windowsill
- A small collection of natural objects (leaves, shells, stones, insects) or household items for classification exercises
- Optional: a copy of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (any readable translation — Irwin's Hackett edition for 15+)
- Optional: a nature field guide for your region

### Choose Your Age Band

Pick the student lesson appropriate to your child(ren):
- [Ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md)
- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

If you have multiple children across bands, each can read their own lesson; the week's activities below work across ages with minor adjustment.

---

## Monday: The Doctor's Son — Observation as a Way of Life

### Morning: Read Together (20–30 min)

Read the age-appropriate student lesson aloud, or have the student read it independently. Then discuss.

**Key moments to pause on:**
- Aristotle's father Nicomachus as a physician — *"What do doctors do that other people don't? They look carefully."*
- The orphaning — both parents dead by age ten, raised by a guardian
- The arrival at the Academy — a provincial boy entering the best school in the world at seventeen

**For 15-18:** Additionally read *Nicomachean Ethics* Book I, Chapter 1 (just the first two pages) — Aristotle's argument that every action aims at some good, and that there must be a highest good. Notice how different this opening is from a Platonic dialogue.

### Afternoon: The First Observation (20–30 min)

Go outside together. Each person picks **one natural object** — an insect, a plant, a stone, a flower, a piece of bark, anything.

Sit with it for **ten full minutes**. Draw it in the Aristotle notebook. Label every part you can identify. Write three things you notice that you would have missed in a five-second glance.

After the observation, share drawings and notes. Ask each person:
- *"What surprised you?"*
- *"What did you notice at minute 8 that you missed at minute 1?"*

This is the Aristotelian discipline. You are training the doctor's eye.

### Homework

In the Aristotle notebook:
- Write today's date, then: "Day 1: The Doctor's Son"
- Answer: *"What is one thing in my daily life I have never really looked at carefully? Tomorrow, I will look at it for five minutes."*

## Tuesday: The Art of Looking — Classification and the Birth of Science

### Morning: Memory Work (5 min)

Learn and recite together:

> **"We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit."**

Repeat three times, slowly. At the end of the day, ask the child to recite from memory.

**For 11-14, add:** *"Man is by nature a political animal."* — Politics I.2

**For 15-18, add:** *"For the things we have to learn before we can do them, we learn by doing them."* — Nicomachean Ethics II.1

### Morning: The Lesbos Story (15 min)

Tell (or read from the student lesson) the story of Aristotle's biological work on Lesbos: opening sea creatures, drawing what he found inside, counting parts, tracking development, classifying over 500 species.

Emphasize: **No philosopher had ever done this before.** Before Aristotle, philosophers *thought* about the world. Aristotle went out and *looked* at the world. He invented science.

**Ask:** *"Why do you think no one had done this before? What is hard about really looking at things instead of just thinking about them?"*

### Afternoon: The Classification Challenge (30–45 min)

Gather a collection of 15-20 objects. These can be:
- Natural objects (leaves, stones, shells, seeds)
- Household items (utensils, buttons, coins, small toys)
- A mix of both

**Task:** Classify them into groups.

**Round 1:** The child creates their own classification system. Any system is fine. Write it down.

**Round 2:** Now classify the *same* objects using a *different* system. A new principle of organization.

**Round 3:** Compare the two systems. Which is better? Why? What do you mean by "better"?

**Discussion:** *"Aristotle classified over 500 animal species. He had to decide what counted as a 'difference.' Is a color difference as important as a shape difference? Is a behavior difference more important than a size difference? There are no automatic answers — you have to decide, and defend your decision."*

**For 15-18:** After the exercise, read a passage from Aristotle's *Historia Animalium* (a description of an octopus or dolphin). Notice the quality of observation. Ask: *"How does Aristotle's classification compare to yours? What makes his method scientific?"*

### Homework

In the notebook, draw and classify five things you observed today. For each one, note: **genus** (the broad category) and **differentia** (what makes it different from other members of the category). This is Aristotle's method of definition: define by genus and differentia.

---

## Wednesday: The Student Who Disagreed — Plato, Aristotle, and Intellectual Courage

### Morning: The Great Disagreement (20–30 min)

Tell (or read) the story of Aristotle's relationship with Plato:
- Twenty years at the Academy
- Plato calling Aristotle "the Mind" and "the Reader"
- The philosophical disagreement: Forms vs. things-in-themselves
- The famous line: "I am a friend of Plato, but a greater friend of truth"
- The departure after Plato's death

**Core question (all ages):** *"What is harder: agreeing with someone you love, or telling them honestly that you think they're wrong?"*

**For 8-10:** Keep the disagreement simple: "Plato thought the really real world was invisible — made of perfect ideas. Aristotle thought the really real world is right here — the animals, the rocks, the people. They loved each other and disagreed about this for twenty years."

**For 11-14:** Introduce the Forms vs. hylomorphism distinction in more detail. Use the horse example: Plato says there's a perfect, invisible Horse that all real horses copy. Aristotle says the "horseness" is *in* the actual horse — study the horse to understand horseness.

**For 15-18:** Read together *Nicomachean Ethics* I.6, where Aristotle critiques the theory of Forms — respectfully, carefully, and devastatingly. Notice the tone: this is how intellectual disagreement should work.

### Afternoon: The Disagreement Exercise (20–30 min)

**For 8-10:** Think of something a parent, teacher, or friend believes that you think is wrong. (It can be small — a favorite food, a way of doing things, an opinion about a movie.) Write three sentences:
1. What they believe, stated as fairly as you can
2. Why you disagree
3. How you could tell them respectfully

**For 11-14:** Write a one-page dialogue between Plato and Aristotle, arguing about whether the real world is the world of Forms or the world of physical things. **Rule:** Each character must make their best argument. No strawmen. Each side must sound convincing.

**For 15-18:** Write a one-page response to this question: *"When is it right to disagree with a teacher publicly, and when is it better to disagree privately or not at all? What principles should govern the decision?"*

### Homework

In the notebook:
- Write: "Aristotle honored Plato by disagreeing with him — not by copying him."
- Answer: *"Is there something I've been taught that I'm not sure is right? What would it take to investigate honestly?"*

## Thursday: The Habit of Virtue — The Golden Mean and Character as Practice

### Morning: The Golden Mean (20–30 min)

Teach the golden mean. Use the table:

| Too Little | Just Right (Virtue) | Too Much |
|---|---|---|
| Cowardice | **Courage** | Rashness |
| Stinginess | **Generosity** | Wastefulness |
| Self-deprecation | **Proper pride** | Vanity |
| Dullness | **Wit** | Clowning |
| Passivity | **Righteous anger** | Rage |

**Key point:** The golden mean is NOT "always be moderate." It is "find the *right* response for *this* situation." Sometimes the right response is extreme. If someone is drowning, the courageous response is to jump in — not to "moderately consider helping."

**Practice together:** Name five situations the child actually faces (at school, at home, with friends). For each, identify:
1. The excess (too much)
2. The deficiency (too little)
3. The mean (the right response for this situation)

**For 15-18:** Read *Nicomachean Ethics* II.6-7 (the formal account of virtue as the mean). Notice Aristotle's careful qualifications: the mean is relative to the person and situation, not an arithmetic midpoint.

### Afternoon: The Virtue Habit Launch (20 min)

This is the most important practical exercise in the Aristotle profile.

**Together, choose ONE virtue to practice for the rest of the week (and ideally for 30 days beyond):**
- Courage
- Patience
- Kindness
- Honesty
- Diligence

Make a simple tracking chart in the notebook. Each day has one row:
- Date
- What I did to practice this virtue today
- Was it easier than yesterday? (Yes/No)

**Start today.** Before bed, the child writes down one act of the chosen virtue they performed today.

**Explain:** *"Aristotle says you don't become brave by deciding to be brave. You become brave by doing brave things until bravery is your habit. This chart is how you build the habit. By the end of the month, if you do this honestly, you will have changed your character — not by magic, but by practice."*

### Homework

Perform one deliberate act of the chosen virtue before bed. Record it.

## Friday: The System and the Legacy — Organizing Knowledge and the Mentor Chain

### Morning: The Lyceum and the System (20–30 min)

Tell (or read) the story of the Lyceum: Aristotle's return to Athens at forty-nine, the founding of his own school, the twelve years of systematic teaching and writing.

**Emphasize the scope:**
- Invented logic (the rules of clear thinking)
- Wrote the *Nicomachean Ethics* (how to live a good life)
- Collected 158 Greek constitutions (political science)
- Wrote the *Poetics* (the rules of good storytelling)
- Continued the biological work from Lesbos
- Organized *all of it* into a system

**Key point:** *"Aristotle didn't just know a lot of things. He organized what he knew into a system — so that other people could use it after he was gone. That is the difference between being smart and being useful. Smart is personal. A system is transmissible."*

**For 15-18:** Discuss the difference between Plato's Academy and Aristotle's Lyceum. The Academy emphasized dialectic and mathematics; the Lyceum emphasized empirical research and encyclopedic scope. Both were great. What did each do well that the other did not?

### Morning: The Mentor Chain (10 min)

Draw the chain on a large piece of paper:

**Socrates → Plato → Aristotle → Alexander**

For each link, write one sentence about what was transmitted:

- Socrates → Plato: "The method of questioning — and the conviction that it was worth dying for"
- Plato → Aristotle: "Twenty years of philosophical training — and the institutional model of a school"
- Aristotle → Alexander: "Homer, ethics, the habit of looking at the world — and the idea that a ruler should think"

Put this on the wall. You now have the complete chain visible for the rest of the curriculum.

### Afternoon: Narration and Reflection (30 min)

**Narration (10–15 min):**
The child retells Aristotle's life story, from memory, aloud. Start at Stagira. Move through the Academy, Lesbos, Macedon, the Lyceum, the flight from Athens.

Listen without interrupting except for gentle prompts. After they finish, ask: *"What part of his life will you remember most clearly ten years from now?"*

**Reflection (15 min):**
In the notebook:

**For 8-10:**
- *"The most important thing I learned from Aristotle is ______."*
- *"One habit I am building right now is ______."*
- *"Something I want to look at more carefully is ______."*

**For 11-14:**
- *"The golden mean applied to my biggest current challenge means ______."*
- *"The difference between Plato and Aristotle, in my own words, is ______."*
- *"Something I disagree with a teacher or parent about, and how I could say it respectfully, is ______."*
- *"My virtue habit after four days is ______. Is it getting easier?"*

**For 15-18:**
- A single-page reflection essay: *"What Aristotle teaches me about my own formation — what I've absorbed, what I might be blind to, and what I need to build."*

### The Commitment: The One Thing

At the end of the week, the child picks **one thing from Aristotle's life they will carry forward**.

Examples:
- *"I will observe one thing carefully for five minutes every day for a month."*
- *"I will continue my virtue habit for thirty days."*
- *"I will practice disagreeing respectfully — stating the other person's position fairly before I argue."*
- *"I will classify one domain of my life into a system this month."*
- *"I will read the Nicomachean Ethics this semester."*
- *"I will ask 'what am I blind to?' at least once a week."*

They write it at the top of a new page in the notebook, dated.

Refer back to this page once a month for the next year.

## Extension Ideas (If You Want More)

### Art & Images
- Study Raphael's *School of Athens*. Find Plato (pointing up) and Aristotle (pointing out). Identify other figures. This painting is the visual summary of the Plato-Aristotle relationship.
- Find images of Lesbos and the lagoon of Pyrrha — where Aristotle did his biological work.
- Look at illustrations from medieval copies of Aristotle's works — notice how his ideas were transmitted visually across centuries.

### Books
- **For 8-10:** Nature field guides; David Attenborough books; children's biographies of Aristotle
- **For 11-14:** Armand Marie Leroi, *The Lagoon* (selected chapters); Bryan Magee, *The Story of Philosophy* (Aristotle chapter)
- **For 15-18:** Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* (Irwin, Hackett); *Poetics* (Sachs, Focus); Alasdair MacIntyre, *After Virtue*; Martha Nussbaum, *The Fragility of Goodness*

### Field Trip Ideas
- Natural history museum — observe the classification systems. Ask: "How is this Aristotelian?"
- Botanical garden — practice Aristotelian observation and classification
- A play or film — apply the *Poetics* framework
- A university campus — notice how knowledge is organized into departments (Aristotle's legacy)

### Deep Dive
- Listen to the BBC *In Our Time* episodes on Aristotle (free podcast)
- For 15-18: Watch Yale's Open Course on ancient Greek philosophy (free online)
- Read selected passages from the *Historia Animalium* — Aristotle's descriptions of octopus, dolphin, or bee behavior

## Assessment: How Do You Know They Learned?

By the end of the week, the child should be able to (at their age band):

**All ages:**
- Tell the story of Aristotle's life in their own words
- Recite "We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence is not an act, but a habit" and explain it
- Explain the difference between Plato's approach and Aristotle's approach
- Describe at least one observation exercise they completed
- Name the mentor chain: Socrates → Plato → Aristotle → Alexander

**Ages 11+:**
- Explain the golden mean with real-life examples
- Have begun a virtue habit and tracked it for several days
- Articulate why Aristotle's disagreement with Plato was respectful, not a betrayal
- Complete at least one classification exercise

**Ages 15-18:**
- Articulate the central argument of the *Nicomachean Ethics* (eudaimonia as activity of the soul in accordance with virtue)
- Engage the golden mean as precision, not compromise
- Begin examining their own formation and its blind spots
- Take a position on the Socrates-vs.-Aristotle flight question

---

## What's Next?

Aristotle completes the core mentor chain: Socrates → Plato → Aristotle → Alexander. The next figures extend the tradition into new domains:

- **Marcus Aurelius** — the Stoic emperor, the other great test of the philosopher-ruler thesis
- **Socrates** — now that students have seen his legacy through Plato and Aristotle, they can study the man himself with full context
- **Isocrates** — Plato's rival educator, offering a contrasting model of formation
- **Cicero** — the Roman inheritor of the Greek philosophical tradition
- **Quintilian** — the Roman theorist of education who systematized formation itself

The foundation is complete. Socrates, Plato, Aristotle, Alexander — the four pillars. Everything that follows is elaboration, extension, and the long transmission of what these four established into the Roman, medieval, Renaissance, and modern worlds.

Your child now has the foundation. Build on it.

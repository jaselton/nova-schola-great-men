# Isocrates: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Isocrates to one or more children. It is designed for a parent or teacher at home, with no specialized materials beyond a notebook — though for older students, access to free online translations of Isocrates's *Against the Sophists* and *Antidosis* is strongly recommended. Total time commitment: roughly **45–75 minutes per day**, divisible into morning and afternoon sessions.

The plan is calibrated for **ages 8-14** as its default, with extensions and adjustments for ages 15-18 marked throughout.

The week moves through: **The Man Who Couldn't Speak → What His School Actually Taught → The Debate with Plato → The Panhellenic Vision → The Legacy and the Question It Leaves**. This follows the logical development of Isocrates's contribution: first the biographical formation story, then the educational philosophy, then the intellectual context, then the political vision, then the question of what it all means.

The most important structural feature of this week: **Isocrates is the figure who most directly challenges this curriculum's method.** He insists that education is formation, not information. Every day includes at least one exercise designed to produce genuine formation — actual change in thinking or expression — not just comprehension of content. Take these exercises seriously. They are the week's substance.

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for each student — this week's notebook is the **"Formation Journal"**. Unlike Marcus's daily review or Socrates's examined-life entries, this journal is about *output*: what did I write, say, or argue today that was genuinely clear and genuinely mine?
- For ages 11-18: access to translations of Isocrates (available free at [ellopos.net](https://www.ellopos.net/elpenor/greek-texts/ancient-Greece/isocrates/default.asp) and at [Perseus Digital Library](https://www.perseus.tufts.edu)). Specifically: *Against the Sophists* (short, all ages) and *Antidosis* (sections 180–275 for 11-18).
- A map of the Greek world in the fourth century BCE — locate Athens, Sparta, Thebes, Macedon, Delphi, and the extent of the Persian Empire.
- For ages 15-18: Cicero's *De Oratore* Book I (available free at [thelatinlibrary.com](http://www.thelatinlibrary.com) or in the Loeb Classical Library) — to trace the Isocratean tradition forward.

### Choose Your Age Band

Select the student lesson appropriate to your child(ren):

- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

### The Formation Journal Setup

On Day One, establish the journal with a specific structure different from previous weeks:

Each day has two entries:
1. **Morning: The Explanation Test** — Pick one idea from the lesson. Write a paragraph explaining it to someone who knows nothing about it. No technical terms without definitions. No assumed knowledge. Clear, organized, accurate.
2. **Evening: The Revision** — Come back to the morning entry. Read it as if you are the imagined reader who knows nothing. Revise it. What was unclear? What did you discover you didn't understand as well as you thought?

This two-part practice is the Isocratean method applied to itself: articulation as examination and development of thought.

## Monday: The Limitation — What Do You Do When You Can't Do the Thing?

### Morning: The Story and Discussion (25–35 min)

Read the age-appropriate student lesson aloud, or have the student read it independently. Focus today on the biographical story: Isocrates's family, his education, the war that destroyed his wealth, the weak voice that prevented public speaking, the decade as a speechwriter, and the decision to open a school.

**Key moments to pause on:**

- The limitation: *"Isocrates had exactly the limitation that should have made his career impossible. He was trained for rhetoric in a city where rhetoric ran on live performance — and he couldn't perform publicly. What would you have done? Would you have given up, or found a different way in?"*

- The speechwriting decade: *"He spent ten years writing speeches for other people to read. He was good at it and found it insufficient. What's the difference between being good at something and finding it sufficient? Is it possible to be very skilled at something and still know it's not your real work?"*

- The school: *"He charged a thousand drachmas — a year's wages for a craftsman. He took nine students at a time. He turned away students he thought weren't capable. What does this tell you about what he thought he was doing?"*

For 15-18: Add the following discussion. The *Antidosis* (354 BCE) was written at eighty-two. Isocrates lived to ninety-eight and revised it repeatedly. What does it mean to write your life's defense forty-four years before you die, then keep revising it? What is he defending? What is he afraid will be misunderstood?

### Afternoon: Formation Journal Day 1 — The Limitation Exercise (25–35 min)

**Morning entry (write before or during the lesson):**
Explain, in one clear paragraph, what the word "rhetoric" means — as if explaining it to someone who has never heard the word. Do not look it up. Use only what you know. (If you're not sure you know enough, that's important information.)

**Afternoon session:**
Discuss the limitation concept. Ask:

*"What is something you currently cannot do — a real limitation, not a trivial one — that is in the way of something you care about? Write it down. Now: is there a way around this limitation instead of through it? Not a workaround that avoids the real thing, but a redirect that reaches the same destination by a different path?"*

Have the student write a short paragraph (four to eight sentences) exploring this. Not to solve the problem — just to see it clearly.

**Evening entry:**
Return to the morning "What is rhetoric?" paragraph. Read it as a critical reader. What did you assume your imaginary reader would know? What is unclear? Revise it so that someone who has never heard the word could follow it. Notice: what did the revision reveal about the gaps in your own understanding?

## Tuesday: What He Actually Taught — The Formation/Information Distinction

### Morning: The Philosophy in Action (30–40 min)

Today's focus is the core Isocratean distinction: education as formation versus education as information.

Open by asking: *"What have you gotten from this curriculum so far? What do you now know that you didn't know before? And: what kind of person are you becoming — is that different from last month?"*

Take the answers seriously. The second question is much harder.

Now introduce Isocrates's three-component model of education: **natural talent + sustained practice + good models**. He claimed that all three were necessary and that none could substitute for the others. Walk through each:

- *Natural talent:* "He turned away students he didn't think could be formed. What does that mean? Is it fair? Does it reflect something true about education, or is it elitism?"
- *Sustained practice:* "He worked with students for three or four years. Not a semester. Not a workshop. What does three years of sustained practice in clear thinking and effective writing actually produce? Have you experienced anything like that?"
- *Good models:* "He had his students read the best available prose — not formulas. What makes something a good model, as opposed to just an impressive example?"

**For ages 8-10:** Simplify to: "He taught by having students read really good writing and then try to write as well themselves. He corrected them. Over and over. What do you think would change in your writing if someone did that with you every day for three years?"

**For 15-18:** Add: Read the relevant section of *Against the Sophists* (sections 14–18) where Isocrates attacks educators who reduce rhetoric to formulas. What specifically is his critique? Is it still relevant? Where do you see formula-based teaching today?

### Afternoon: The Say-It-Clearly Exercise (30–40 min)

Choose a topic the student knows something about — a subject from this curriculum, a hobby, a skill. Have them explain it in writing as if to someone who knows nothing about it.

Requirements:
- No undefined technical terms
- No assumed knowledge
- Clear structure (what is it, why does it matter, what should the reader understand by the end)
- Accurate throughout

Then read it together critically. Ask:
- *"Is this actually clear, or does it assume things the imagined reader doesn't know?"*
- *"Is there any point where you got stuck because you realized you didn't actually understand something as well as you thought?"*
- *"Which sentence was hardest to write? Why?"*

Revise together. Then have the student revise privately.

**Formation Journal:** Write one sentence about what the revision process revealed. Was it different from what you expected when you started writing?

## Wednesday: The Debate — Isocrates vs. Plato

### Morning: The Two Schools (35–45 min)

Today presents the most intellectually demanding content of the week: the Isocrates-Plato debate. It is important to present this as a genuine debate — both sides making real arguments — rather than as a quiz about who said what.

**Set up the debate:**

*"Athens in 390 BCE had two major schools with two very different theories of education. They shared one enemy — the Sophists, who they both thought were selling technique without substance — but they had almost opposite ideas about what the right alternative was."*

Present Plato's position first:
- Education is the pursuit of genuine knowledge (not opinion)
- That requires mathematical training and philosophical dialectic
- Rhetoric is dangerous because it deals in opinion and can manipulate
- The best life is the philosophical life; civic engagement is a lesser duty

Present Isocrates's position:
- Education is the formation of practically wise citizens
- That requires rhetorical training grounded in genuine philosophical understanding
- Philosophy divorced from civic application is intellectual vanity
- The best life is the active life of a citizen who uses full formed capacities in service of community

**For ages 8-10:** Simplify the debate to: Plato says learn to think purely and correctly; Isocrates says learn to think and also communicate what you think, for real people in a real city. Ask: which do you think is more important? Is one of them right, or do you need both?

**For 11-14:** Walk through both sides carefully. Then: *"Which position do you find more persuasive? Why? Is there something each side is missing that the other supplies?"*

**For 15-18:** Add Aristotle as the complicating third figure. His *Rhetoric* is a serious philosophical treatise — which Plato would have found unacceptable — and his concept of *phronesis* (practical wisdom) as a genuine intellectual virtue is a philosophical vindication of what Isocrates's school was doing in practice. *"Is Aristotle taking Isocrates's side? What does that mean for how we evaluate the debate?"*

### Afternoon: Structured Argument Practice (30–40 min)

Today's exercise is applied Isocratean rhetoric: pick a side of the Isocrates-Plato debate and argue for it in writing.

Requirements:
- State your position clearly in the first sentence
- Give at least two specific reasons for your position
- Acknowledge at least one strong point on the other side
- Explain why that point doesn't change your conclusion

**For ages 8-10:** Simplify. Have the student argue for either "thinking clearly is the most important part of education" or "being able to explain your thinking is the most important part." One good paragraph.

**For 11-14:** Three to four paragraphs. Both sides acknowledged.

**For 15-18:** Full five-paragraph argument, incorporating the *Against the Sophists* as a primary source. Reference Aristotle's position.

**Formation Journal:** After writing, answer: Did the process of writing the argument change what you actually believe? Or did it just organize what you already thought?

## Thursday: The Panhellenic Vision — When Your Life's Argument Finally Gets Proved Wrong

### Morning: The Political Argument and Its Failure (30–40 min)

Today is about the Panhellenic argument — and the death.

Present the argument clearly: Isocrates spent fifty years arguing that the Greek cities should stop destroying each other and unite against Persia. He made this argument in every format available to him: school instruction, public pamphlets, open letters to kings. He was largely ignored.

Then Philip of Macedon united the Greeks by conquering them. His son Alexander led the Persian campaign. Isocrates had been right about the goal. He had been replaced as the agent.

*"What does it mean to spend fifty years arguing for something, to finally see it happen — and to have it happen in a way that destroys what you were trying to protect in the first place?"*

The death at ninety-eight. *"When he heard about Chaeronea, he stopped eating. He died four days later. What do you make of this? Was it despair? Was it integrity — the only consistent response to the failure of everything he'd committed to? Or was it simply an old man's body breaking down under shock?"*

**For ages 8-10:** Focus on the emotional reality: he worked for fifty years at something important, and it didn't turn out how he hoped. *"Has anything like that ever happened to you — working hard at something and having it fail? What did you do? What should you do?"*

**For 11-14:** Push harder: *"Is Chaeronea a failure for Isocrates? His school ran for sixty years and produced exceptional people. His tradition shaped Roman education. His ideas are still being discussed. In what sense did he fail? In what sense did he succeed?"*

**For 15-18:** Add: *"His Panhellenic argument was eventually implemented — just not by the Greeks, and not voluntarily. Does this vindicate the argument, fail it, or make the question complicated? Is there such a thing as being right about a goal but wrong about the conditions that make the goal achievable?"*

### Afternoon: The Long Game Exercise (25–35 min)

Today's journal exercise is about commitment across time.

*"Choose something you care about — not a hobby, but something genuinely important to you. Something you want to be true, or to change, or to build. Describe it in a paragraph."*

Then: *"How long would you commit to this before you would consider the project finished or failed? What would 'success' look like? What would 'failure' look like? And: could you imagine keeping at it for ninety-eight years — not literally, but in the sense of committing your whole life to it without certainty of outcome?"*

This is a hard question for any age. Don't require a confident answer. Require genuine engagement with the question.

**Formation Journal:** Write the commitment paragraph. Come back to it at the end of the year and see if it still holds.

## Friday: The Legacy — What Does Isocrates Leave Western Education?

### Morning: The Long Thread (30–40 min)

Today follows the Isocratean tradition forward — through Rome, into the Renaissance, and into contemporary educational debates.

**Step 1: Rome**

Introduce Cicero. He was born 106 BCE — 232 years after Isocrates opened his school. He read Isocrates obsessively, modeled his prose on Isocratean period style, and built his ideal of the perfect orator directly on Isocrates's framework.

The phrase Cato the Elder used — and that Cicero and Quintilian made the foundation of Roman education — is *vir bonus dicendi peritus*: the good man skilled in speaking.

*"This is Isocrates's formula in Latin. The goal of education is not a well-informed man, not a technically skilled man, but a good man who is also a skilled communicator. Why is the 'good' part first?"*

**Step 2: The Renaissance**

The humanists — Erasmus, More, Vives — revived classical rhetorical education in explicit opposition to what they saw as the sterile, technically precise but practically impotent philosophy of the late medieval universities. They called what they were doing *studia humanitatis*: the studies that make one human. Grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, moral philosophy. This is Isocrates's program.

**Step 3: The Contemporary Debate**

*"Is this debate still happening? Where do you see versions of the Isocrates-Plato argument in contemporary education debates?"*

Some possibilities to discuss:
- STEM education vs. humanities — Plato vs. Isocrates in modern dress
- Skills training vs. character formation
- Measurable outcomes vs. "whole person" development
- Specialized expertise vs. broad civic education

**For ages 8-10:** Focus on the simple form of the contemporary debate: *"Should school teach you to know things, or should it teach you to become a certain kind of person? Can it do both? Which is harder?"*

**For 11-14:** Push toward a real position: *"Which side do you actually agree with? And: what would a school look like if it tried to do what Isocrates proposed — genuinely form people, not just teach them?"*

**For 15-18:** Add: *"The Isocratean tradition shaped formal education from Rome through the Renaissance. The Enlightenment shifted the center of gravity toward the Platonic model — science, technical knowledge, universal principles. Is the contemporary university more Platonic or Isocratean? Should it be different?"*

### Afternoon: The Week's Summation (25–35 min)

Return to the Formation Journal from the week. Have the student reread all their entries privately.

Then write a final entry addressing:

1. *"What did I learn about Isocrates this week?"*
2. *"Is there one thing I can now explain more clearly than I could at the start of the week — not about Isocrates, but about anything?"*
3. *"Do I agree with Isocrates's main claim: that education is formation, not information? What would I need to believe to agree? What would I need to believe to disagree?"*
4. *"What question from this week am I going to keep thinking about?"*

For **ages 15-18**, add an essay option (for the weekend or following week):

- *"Isocrates argued that you cannot be a genuinely great orator without also being a genuinely good person. Evaluate this claim carefully. Is it true? What evidence would support or undermine it? Does your conclusion change anything about how you think education should work?"*
- *"The Isocrates-Plato debate is sometimes characterized as 'practical vs. theoretical' education. Is this characterization accurate? What is lost and what is gained by framing it this way?"*
- *"Isocrates died from grief after Chaeronea. Was this a failure or a vindication of his life's work? Construct a serious argument for each side, then take a position."*

## After the Week

### The Sustaining Practice

The practice Isocrates leaves behind is more demanding than Socrates's "How do I know this?" or Marcus's daily review.

**The Isocratean practice:** Before you can say you genuinely understand something, try to write it clearly enough that a thoughtful person who knows nothing about it can follow your argument. If you cannot, you do not yet understand it. Return to the subject, think harder, and try again.

This practice takes longer than the others. It is also more revealing. The act of writing is the act of thinking — not the transcription of pre-formed thought but the process through which thought achieves precision and form. Isocrates knew this. It is why he spent ten years on the *Panegyricus*.

A practical version for everyday use: the **one-paragraph test**. Once a week, choose something you encountered that week — an idea, an argument, an event, an experience — and write a single paragraph explaining it clearly to an imagined reader who knows nothing about it. Revise it once. Notice what the revision reveals.

This practice can last a lifetime. The student who begins it at ten and maintains it at forty is a substantially more capable thinker than the one who did not.

### Further Reading

**For ages 8-10:** No required reading beyond the student lesson. If the child wants more, read them the story of Cicero's education — he modeled himself on Isocrates and used that formation to become Rome's greatest orator.

**For ages 11-14:** *Against the Sophists* in full (fifteen minutes). Then optionally, sections 180–215 of *Antidosis* — accessible with some help. Then Plato's *Phaedrus* 260a–275e for the other side of the debate.

**For ages 15-18:** The full program: *Against the Sophists*, *Antidosis* (180–275), *Nicocles*, a substantial portion of *Panegyricus*. Then Cicero's *De Oratore* Book I. Then Werner Jaeger's *Paideia* Vol. III on Isocrates (scholarly but accessible). Over the following months: Quintilian's *Institutio Oratoria* Book I, and at least one of the Renaissance humanist educational texts (Erasmus's *Ciceronianus* is short and pointed).

### Connections to the Rest of the Sequence

- **Cicero** (Day 8): The Roman Isocrates. Everything you understand about Isocrates's educational ideal illuminates what Cicero was trying to build — and makes his eventual failure more poignant in the same way.
- **Quintilian** (Day 9): The systematic completion of the Isocratean program. *Institutio Oratoria* is the most comprehensive educational treatise of antiquity; its philosophical foundations are Isocratean.
- **Erasmus** (Day 16): The Renaissance revival of Isocratean humanist education. The *studia humanitatis* is Isocrates's program adapted for the Christian European context.
- **Every subsequent figure**: For each, ask the Isocratean question — not just "What did this person know?" but "What kind of person was this person? What had they become? And how did their formation show up in the actual choices they made?"

The Isocratean question is different from the Socratic question. Socrates asks: *Have you examined this?* Isocrates asks: *What kind of person have you become?* Both questions are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.

The student who carries both questions into the rest of the sequence — and into the rest of their life — is the student the curriculum is designed to produce.

---

## A Final Word to the Parent

Isocrates believed that the formation of one student, done well, over three or four years, produces something the transmission of information to a hundred students can never match.

You are working with your child for not three years but their entire childhood. You have a proximity and a continuity that Isocrates's paid school could never replicate.

The question his life's work poses to you — and it is an uncomfortable question — is: *Are you actually forming your child, or are you covering material?* The difference is real and important. Covering material produces a student who knows things. Forming a person produces someone who is becoming something.

The good news is that these are not mutually exclusive. The curriculum's content is sound. But Isocrates would insist that the content is the occasion, not the substance. The substance is what happens when you sit with your child and work through a difficult idea together — when they try to explain something and realize they don't understand it, when they revise a paragraph and discover that revision is actually thinking, when they encounter a genuine argument and have to engage with it rather than just absorb it.

That is the Isocratean education. You are already delivering it, every time the work gets genuinely hard and you both stay with it.

He would say: keep doing that. For as many years as you can. Then let them go.

The formation that happens in those years is the most lasting thing you can give them.

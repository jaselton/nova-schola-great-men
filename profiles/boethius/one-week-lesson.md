# Boethius: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

**Target:** All ages (adapt depth and discussion to student maturity)  
**Time commitment:** 45-60 minutes/day  
**Structure:** 5 days, Monday-Friday  
**Goal:** Introduce Boethius as scholar, statesman, prisoner, and civilizational bridge while helping students practice the distinction between changing fortune and lasting virtue

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed
- Student notebook or journal
- Pencil/pen
- Two sheets of plain paper or index cards
- Printed quote cards (optional)
- Simple timeline page or blank paper for one
- Optional: map of the late Roman world / Italy

### Choose Your Age Band
- **Ages 8-10:** Story, narration, concrete examples, steady-heart practice
- **Ages 11-14:** Add historical context, preservation project, journal reflection
- **Ages 15-18:** Add primary source analysis, providence/free will discussion, comparative writing

---

## Monday: The Story and the World

### Morning: Read Together (20-30 min)

**Ages 8-10:** Read the [student lesson ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md) together  
**Ages 11-14:** Read the [student lesson ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md) together or independently  
**Ages 15-18:** Assign the [student lesson ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md) and discuss afterward

**Pause to discuss:**
- Who was Boethius before prison?
- What did he try to preserve?
- What happened when fortune turned against him?
- What did he discover that prison could not take away?

---

### Afternoon: Build the Timeline (15-20 min)

Create a simple timeline with these points:
- c. 475-477: Boethius born
- Western Roman Empire already fallen in the West
- Studies Greek philosophy and Roman learning
- Writes translations and commentaries
- Serves under Theoderic
- Imprisoned for alleged treason
- c. 524-526: executed
- *The Consolation of Philosophy* survives and shapes later centuries

**Ages 8-10:** Keep it simple and visual  
**Ages 11-14:** Add one-sentence explanations  
**Ages 15-18:** Add note about historical uncertainty around the execution date

**Reflection question:**
Why might one book outlast a kingdom?

---

## Tuesday: Fortune and Stability

### Morning: Memory Work (5-10 min)

Choose one line:

**Ages 8-10:**
> "Nothing is miserable unless you think it so."

**Ages 11-14 and 15-18:**
> "Nothing is miserable unless you think it so."

Repeat together several times. Then ask: what does this line mean, and what does it not mean?

---

### Afternoon: Two-Column Exercise (25-35 min)

On one side write: **What Fortune Can Change**  
On the other side write: **What I Can Still Choose**

Possible entries:

**Fortune can change:**
- comfort
- plans
- reputation
- money
- winning or losing
- position
- health

**I can still choose:**
- honesty
- courage
- patience
- gratitude
- self-command
- prayer
- truthfulness

**Ages 8-10:** Use everyday examples (cancelled playdate, broken toy, losing a game)  
**Ages 11-14:** Use school and friendship examples  
**Ages 15-18:** Include status, recognition, achievement, and future plans

**Discussion:**
- Which side do people spend more time worrying about?
- Which side actually shapes character more?

---

## Wednesday: Boethius the Preserver

### Morning: Why Books Matter (15-20 min)

Explain that Boethius did not only write his own ideas. He also translated and explained older books so later generations could still think clearly.

**Discussion prompts:**
- What is lost when a culture loses access to its best books?
- Is preserving wisdom as important as creating something new?
- Who in our world preserves important things for the future?

---

### Afternoon: Preservation Project (25-40 min)

**Activity:** Each student picks one worthy thing to preserve and pass on.

Ideas:
- a poem
- a Bible verse
- a family story
- a proverb
- a historical quotation
- a practical skill or recipe

**Ages 8-10:** Draw it and explain why it matters  
**Ages 11-14:** Write it down and teach it to someone else  
**Ages 15-18:** Add a paragraph on why preserving this matters for a healthy culture

**Goal:** Help students feel that transmission is honorable work.

---

## Thursday: Philosophy in the Prison Cell

### Morning: Read a Short Excerpt (15-20 min)

Choose a brief adapted or original excerpt from *The Consolation of Philosophy*.

Suggested theme:
- fortune changes
- false goods cannot satisfy
- evil harms the doer most deeply
- providence sees more than we do

Read slowly. Then ask:
- What is Lady Philosophy trying to cure in Boethius?
- Why is she firm instead of soft?
- Does she help by challenging him?

---

### Afternoon: Guided Discussion or Writing (20-30 min)

**Ages 8-10:**
Ask: "If something hard happened to you today, what good thing could you still choose?"

**Ages 11-14:**
Write 1 paragraph: "What is one thing I depend on too much that could be taken away?"

**Ages 15-18:**
Write 2-3 paragraphs: "Can a person be free while imprisoned? Distinguish inward freedom from outward freedom."

---

## Friday: Narration, Comparison, and Application

### Morning: Narration (15-20 min)

**Ages 8-10 (oral):**
Tell me Boethius's story from beginning to end.

**Ages 11-14 (written):**
Write one page on Boethius's life and main lesson.

**Ages 15-18 (analytical):**
Write one page answering: What was Boethius really trying to defend in prison—his innocence, his intellect, or his soul?

---

### Afternoon: Compare and Apply (20-30 min)

Compare Boethius with one earlier figure:
- **Socrates** — both unjustly condemned, both use reason under pressure
- **Marcus Aurelius** — both stress inner order, but one rules while the other suffers under rulers
- **Augustine** — both late antique Christians wrestling with the soul, order, and providence

**Questions:**
- Who had more external power?
- Who shows more interior struggle?
- What kind of freedom does each model?

End with this application prompt:

> What is one thing you can do next week to become less dependent on changing fortune and more rooted in lasting goods?

Have the student choose one concrete practice.

---

## Optional Weekend Extension

### Family Table Conversation
Ask everyone at the table:
- What is one unstable thing people trust too much today?
- What is one stable thing they should trust more?

### For Older Students
Read a second short selection from *The Consolation* and compare it with Marcus Aurelius or Augustine.

---

## Teaching Tips

### Keep the Main Contrast Clear
Boethius is easiest to lose in abstraction. Keep bringing students back to the contrast:

**changing circumstances** vs. **lasting character**

### Do Not Oversoften the Story
He was not merely inconvenienced. He was brought low by political injustice. Let students feel the seriousness.

### But Do Not Make It Only Dark
The point is not tragedy for tragedy's sake. The point is that wisdom, rightly possessed, can remain active under pressure.

---

## End-of-Week Outcomes

By the end of the week, students should be able to say:
- who Boethius was
- why his writings mattered historically
- what fortune can and cannot take away
- why interior freedom matters
- how this lesson applies to their own lives

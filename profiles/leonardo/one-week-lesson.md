# Leonardo da Vinci: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

**Target:** All ages (select activities appropriate to your student's level)  
**Time commitment:** 45-60 minutes/day  
**Structure:** 5 days, Monday-Friday  
**Goal:** Introduce Leonardo's life, method, and lessons through story, practice, and discussion

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed
- Notebook or sketchbook for student
- Pencils, pens, colored pencils (optional)
- Access to nature (yard, park, or even houseplants)
- One object from nature (leaf, flower, shell, pinecone)
- Timer
- Optional: Images of Leonardo's artwork and notebook pages (easily found online)

### Choose Your Age Band
- **Ages 8-10:** Focus on story, observation exercises, and simple discussion
- **Ages 11-14:** Add deeper questions, compare Leonardo to modern school, design project
- **Ages 15-18:** Add primary source reading, philosophical questions, and analytical writing

---

## Monday: The Story

### Morning: Read Together (20-30 min)

**Ages 8-10:** Read the [student lesson ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md) together  
**Ages 11-14:** Read the [student lesson ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md) — or have them read independently  
**Ages 15-18:** Assign the [student lesson ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md) as independent reading

**As you read, pause to discuss:**
- "Why couldn't Leonardo go to the fancy schools?"
- "What did he do instead?"
- "What was special about how Leonardo learned?"

---

### Afternoon: Start the Observation Notebook (15-20 min)

**Activity:** Set up Leonardo's method

**What to do:**
1. Give your student a notebook (doesn't have to be fancy—Leonardo's were small and simple)
2. Write on the first page: "[Student's name]'s Observation Notebook — Following Leonardo's Method"
3. Add the date
4. Copy Leonardo's quote on the second page:

> "The eye is the window of the soul."  
> — Leonardo da Vinci

**First entry:**
Go outside together. Spend 5-10 minutes just looking. Then have your student draw ONE thing they notice. It doesn't have to be perfect—Leonardo's early sketches weren't either.

**Younger students:** You might draw alongside them  
**Older students:** Let them work independently, but stay nearby

**Reflection question (write or discuss):**
"What did you notice today that you've never noticed before?"

---

## Tuesday: Observation Practice

### Morning: Memory Work (5 min)

**All ages:** Memorize Leonardo's quote

> "The eye is the window of the soul."

**How to practice:**
- Say it together 5 times
- Student says it alone 3 times
- Ask: "What does this quote mean?"
- Discuss: "Why did Leonardo think the eye was so important?"

---

### Afternoon: The Five Drawings Exercise (30-40 min)

**Activity:** Leonardo's training method—draw the same thing multiple times, seeing more each time

**What to do:**

1. **Pick one object from nature:** Leaf, flower, shell, pinecone, stick, rock
2. **Draw it five times:**

**Drawing 1 (5 min):** Just draw what you see
- No instructions, no judgment
- Set timer, draw until it beeps

**Drawing 2 (5 min):** Look more closely—what details did you miss?
- Before starting, spend 1 minute just looking
- Notice: texture, edges, patterns, small marks

**Drawing 3 (5 min):** Notice light and shadow
- Where is the light coming from?
- Which parts are bright? Which are dark?
- Add shading

**Drawing 4 (5 min):** Study the structure
- How is it shaped?
- What patterns repeat?
- How do the parts connect?

**Drawing 5 (10 min):** Put everything together
- Use all you've learned in drawings 1-4
- Take your time
- This is your "Leonardo drawing"

**After:** Compare drawing 1 and drawing 5

**Discussion questions:**
- "What did you notice in drawing 5 that you didn't see in drawing 1?"
- "Which was harder—drawing quickly or drawing carefully?"
- "Did looking carefully make you see more?"

---

## Wednesday: Discussion & Application

### Morning: Socratic Discussion (20-30 min)

**Pick 3-5 questions appropriate to your student's age:**

**Ages 8-10:**
1. Why did Leonardo watch birds for hours? Was he wasting time or learning?
2. If you couldn't go to school, how would you learn about the world?
3. What's harder: starting something new or finishing it?
4. What's one thing you'd like to watch really carefully (like Leonardo did)?

**Ages 11-14:**
1. Was Leonardo's exclusion from formal education a good thing or a bad thing? Why?
2. Leonardo said experience is better than books. Is that always true? When might books be better?
3. Compare the workshop (where Leonardo learned) to modern school. What's similar? What's different?
4. Why couldn't Leonardo finish things? What could have helped him?

**Ages 15-18:**
1. Can observation alone produce knowledge, or do you also need theoretical frameworks from books?
2. Leonardo worked mostly alone without deadlines. Would structure have helped or hurt his creativity?
3. Is specialization (modern education) better or worse than integration (Leonardo's approach)?
4. Leonardo's genius didn't save him from incompletion. What does this teach us about talent vs. discipline?

**How to facilitate:**
- Ask the question
- Wait (10-15 seconds of silence is okay—they're thinking!)
- Don't jump to your answer—pull out theirs
- When they answer, ask "Why do you think that?" or "Can you give an example?"
- If they're stuck, offer two contrasting views and ask which they lean toward

---

### Afternoon: Copywork (15 min)

**Activity:** Practice careful writing (Leonardo's method for learning Latin)

**What to copy:**

**Ages 8-10:**
> "The eye is the window of the soul."  
> — Leonardo da Vinci

Copy 3 times, as neatly as possible.

**Ages 11-14:**
> "Those who are inventors between Nature and Man are to be considered simply as is an object in front of a mirror in comparison with its image when seen in the mirror, the one being something in itself, the other nothing."  
> — Leonardo da Vinci

Copy once, slowly and carefully.

**Ages 15-18:**
> "I am fully conscious that, not being a literary man, certain presumptuous persons will think that they may reasonably blame me... They will say that I, having no literary skill, cannot properly express that which I desire to treat of, but they do not know that my subjects are to be dealt with by experience rather than by words."  
> — Leonardo da Vinci, Codex Atlanticus

Copy once, then write 3-4 sentences: "What is Leonardo defending here? Do you agree with him?"

---

## Thursday: Hands-On Project

### All Day: Design-Build-Test (60-90 min total, can be split)

**Activity:** Leonardo's maker method—design, build, test, redesign

**Choose a project appropriate to age/skill:**

**Ages 8-10: Simple Machines**
- Build a lever (stick + rock as fulcrum)
- Build a ramp (board + books)
- Build a pulley (string + spool)

**Process:**
1. **Design (10 min):** Draw what you want to build
2. **Build (20 min):** Make it with household items
3. **Test (10 min):** Does it work? How well?
4. **Observe (5 min):** Draw what actually happened
5. **Discuss:** Why did it work (or not work)?

---

**Ages 11-14: Design Challenge**
- Design a bridge to hold weight (cardboard, popsicle sticks, or straws)
- Design a boat that floats and holds cargo (aluminum foil, paper)
- Design a paper airplane optimized for distance OR time aloft

**Process:**
1. **Research (10 min):** Look at 2-3 examples online
2. **Design (15 min):** Sketch your design, label parts
3. **Build (30 min):** Build version 1
4. **Test (10 min):** Measure results
5. **Redesign (20 min):** What needs to change? Build version 2
6. **Test again (10 min):** Did it improve?
7. **Document:** Draw both versions, note what you learned

---

**Ages 15-18: Empirical Investigation**
- Study a specific phenomenon (water vortex, pendulum period, light refraction)
- Design an experiment to understand it better
- Document process Leonardo-style

**Process:**
1. **Question (10 min):** What do you want to understand?
2. **Hypothesis (10 min):** What do you think causes this?
3. **Design experiment (20 min):** How can you test it?
4. **Run experiment (30 min):** Observe, measure, document
5. **Analysis (20 min):** What did you learn? What surprised you?
6. **Write-up:** 2-3 pages with sketches, data, and conclusions

---

## Friday: Reflection & Narration

### Morning: Narration (15-20 min)

**Activity:** Tell back what you learned (classical method)

**Ages 8-10 (oral narration):**
"Tell me the story of Leonardo da Vinci. Who was he? What did he do? What did you learn from him?"

Let them talk for 3-5 minutes without interrupting. Then ask 1-2 follow-up questions.

---

**Ages 11-14 (written narration):**
"Write a one-page summary of Leonardo's life and what you learned from him. Include:
- Who he was
- How he learned
- What made him special
- What you'll remember most"

15 minutes, then read aloud and discuss.

---

**Ages 15-18 (analytical reflection):**
"Write 2-3 pages answering this question: 'What does Leonardo's life teach about the relationship between education and genius?'"

Required elements:
- Reference specific details from Leonardo's life
- Take a position (don't just summarize)
- Connect to your own educational experience

---

### Afternoon: Review & Application (20-30 min)

**Activity:** What will you take forward?

**Discuss together:**

1. **What's the most important thing you learned about Leonardo this week?**

2. **What's one thing Leonardo did that you want to imitate?**
   - Being more curious?
   - Observing more carefully?
   - Keeping a notebook?
   - Asking more questions?
   - Finishing what you start?

3. **What's one thing Leonardo did that you want to avoid?**
   - Starting too many things?
   - Working without deadlines?
   - Being too much of a perfectionist?

4. **This week, you practiced:**
   - Observing carefully (five drawings exercise)
   - Keeping a notebook
   - Building and testing
   - Discussion and thinking

   **Which practice will you continue next week?**

---

### Commitment: The One Thing

**End the week with a concrete commitment:**

**Choose ONE practice to continue for the next 30 days:**

- [ ] Keep an observation notebook (1 drawing/day)
- [ ] Ask one "How does this work?" question per week
- [ ] Complete one project from start to finish
- [ ] Practice looking closely at __________ (fill in)
- [ ] Other: _______________________

**Write the commitment on the last page of the notebook.**

**Parent:** Check in weekly to see how it's going.

## Extension Ideas (If You Want More)

### Art & Images
- Look at Leonardo's actual paintings online (*Last Supper*, *Mona Lisa*, *Vitruvian Man*)
- Find Leonardo's notebook pages (easily searchable)—look at his mirror writing, his sketches
- Watch a video about Leonardo's life (many good ones on YouTube)

### Books
- Read a picture book biography together (Diane Stanley's is excellent)
- Check out a Leonardo book from the library
- For older students: Walter Isaacson's biography is comprehensive and readable

### Field Trip
- Art museum (if nearby)—look for Renaissance paintings
- Science museum—connect Leonardo's investigations to modern science
- Maker space or workshop—experience collaborative hands-on learning

### Deep Dive
- Pick one Leonardo topic to investigate more deeply:
  - His anatomical drawings
  - His flying machine designs
  - His studies of water
  - His mathematics and geometry
- Spend a week becoming a "mini-expert" on that one aspect

---

## Assessment: How Do You Know They Learned?

**Don't quiz them on dates and facts. Instead, watch for:**

✓ Increased attention to detail when observing  
✓ Willingness to keep looking even when it's "hard"  
✓ Questions like "How does this work?" or "Why is that?"  
✓ References to Leonardo when facing challenges  
✓ Pride in finishing at least one project this week  
✓ Continued use of observation notebook after the week ends  

**The real goal:** Not that they memorize facts about Leonardo, but that they **become a little more like Leonardo**—more curious, more observant, more willing to look closely at the world.

---

## What's Next?

After Leonardo, consider studying:

- **Michelangelo** (contrast: more focused, completed more projects)
- **Galileo** (extends Leonardo's empiricism into formal scientific method)
- **Benjamin Franklin** (similar autodidact + maker pattern)
- **Alexander the Great** (shows importance of mentorship—what Leonardo lacked)

Each great man illuminates different virtues, different methods, different strengths and weaknesses.

The goal of the whole series: **Formation for greatness through proximity to greatness.**

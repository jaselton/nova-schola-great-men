# Alexander the Great: Student Lesson (Ages 15-18)
## *Pothos* and the Limits of Formation

---

## The Central Problem

Alexander is the most famous test case in the history of education. The claim under examination is this:

> *Greatness can be formed.*

Not discovered. Not drawn out. *Formed.* Built, deliberately, by parents who know what they are doing and teachers who know what they are doing, over about two decades.

Philip II and Olympias believed this. They committed to it. They hired the most accomplished philosopher alive — Aristotle — to serve as their son's tutor for three years. They began the project before Alexander could walk and they continued it through his campaign apprenticeship at eighteen.

The experiment produced a man who, at thirty-two, had:
- Destroyed the largest empire in the world
- Founded more than twenty cities
- Created the Hellenistic cultural zone that would persist for a thousand years
- Never lost a battle

The experiment also produced a man who, at thirty-two, had:
- Murdered his oldest friend at a dinner table in a drunken rage
- Executed his tutor's own nephew for refusing to worship him as a god
- Marched his army through a desert where most of them died
- Died without a successor, condemning his empire to civil war

Your question is not "was Alexander good or bad?" That is the wrong question. Your question is this:

> *What does Alexander's life tell us about what formation can and cannot do? And what does that imply about the formation you should be undertaking on yourself right now?*

## The Philosophical Setup

### Aristotle's Ethical Theory, in Brief

To read Alexander rightly, you need to know what Aristotle believed he was doing.

Aristotle's ethics, as laid out in the *Nicomachean Ethics*, rests on several claims:

1. **Every action aims at some good.** The highest good — the one pursued for its own sake — is *eudaimonia*, which is usually translated as "flourishing" or "happiness" but is better understood as "a life well lived, taken as a whole."

2. **Virtue is what produces flourishing.** Virtue (*aretē*) is a settled disposition to act well. It is not a feeling or a moment. It is a character trait made durable by repetition.

3. **Virtue is habituated.** This is the crucial claim: *you become virtuous by practicing virtuous actions until they are automatic*. A brave person is someone who has practiced facing fear until facing fear is simply what they do. A temperate person has practiced moderation until moderation is the path of least resistance.

4. **Virtue is a mean between extremes.** Courage is the mean between cowardice (too little confidence) and rashness (too much). Magnanimity is the mean between vanity and pusillanimity. Temperance is the mean between insensibility and self-indulgence.

5. **Practical wisdom (*phronesis*) is required.** Because virtues are context-dependent, you need practical wisdom — the ability to perceive what a situation calls for — to apply them rightly.

When Aristotle came to Mieza, *this* is what he was trying to cultivate in Alexander: a set of habituated dispositions, governed by practical wisdom, toward the mean in each virtue.

He did some of it. He did not do all of it.

### The Macedonian Background

It is easy to forget, reading Plutarch, that Macedon was not Aristotle's Athens. It was a culture with:

- Polygamous royal marriages and endemic succession violence
- A drinking culture of heroic proportions, even by Greek standards
- A kingship modeled partly on Homeric warrior-kings, partly on Persian divine monarchs
- A court where assassination was a routine political instrument

Aristotle had three years at Mieza. He did not have the thirty-year immersion in Athenian civic culture that Plato had before him. The philosophical content arrived into a cultural container that it could shape — but not replace.

Keep this in mind when you are tempted to say "Aristotle failed." A better formulation: *Aristotle's formation was doing battle with a Macedonian background, and it held up as long as Alexander was in Europe. The further he traveled from Athens and Mieza, the more the background asserted itself.*

## The Method in Practice: What Aristotle Actually Gave Him

### 1. A Vocabulary of the Soul

Aristotle provided Alexander with technical terms for his own interior life. *Thymos* (spirited courage). *Eros* (desire). *Logos* (reason). *Phronesis* (practical wisdom). *Megalopsychia* (greatness of soul, or magnanimity). *Hybris* (overstepping the human measure).

This matters. To *name* a motion of the soul is to gain a certain kind of power over it. A young man who notices his own *thymos* rising can, at least sometimes, step away from it. A young man who has no word for *thymos* is simply angry, and he acts.

### 2. The Homeric Framework, Upgraded

Leonidas had drilled Homer into Alexander. Aristotle did something more sophisticated: he gave Alexander an annotated *Iliad* that he personally edited. Alexander carried this copy across Asia. It is not coincidence that Alexander visited Troy before crossing into Persia, or that his mourning for Hephaestion was explicitly modeled on Achilles's mourning for Patroclus.

The *Iliad* became Alexander's operational mythology. Not decoration — *operational*. He made campaign decisions partly on the basis of being the new Achilles.

### 3. A Conception of Kingship

Aristotle wrote (works now lost) called *On Kingship* and *On Colonies* specifically for Alexander. We have hints of their content in later sources: the king as guardian of law, the king's duty to flourishing subjects, the distinction between the king and the tyrant. Some of this Alexander absorbed. The magnanimity toward Darius's family, the preservation of Porus's kingdom, the founding of cities as institutions (not garrisons) — these are Aristotelian-king moves.

### 4. A Model of Inquiry

Aristotle was, in addition to being an ethicist, one of the great natural philosophers of antiquity. He collected specimens. He dissected animals. He wrote on botany, meteorology, astronomy, and the soul. Alexander absorbed the *investigative posture*: when he reached the Indus, he sent specimens of Indian flora and fauna back to Aristotle. His campaign was accompanied by engineers, surveyors, ethnographers, and naturalists. The expedition was *also* a research trip.

### 5. What He Did Not Give Him

Aristotle did not — could not — give Alexander a reliable brake on his own *thymos*. He gave him the vocabulary. He gave him the ethical theory. He even gave him the habit of self-examination. What he could not give was a lifelong accountability structure capable of standing up to a man who, at twenty-five, had no superior on earth.

This is the structural limit. Habituated virtue works best in a community of equals who correct one another. Once Alexander had no equals — once he was Great King of Persia — the correction stopped. The habits stayed. The *corrections* could no longer be applied. And habits, absent correction, drift.

## Case Studies: Formation Working and Failing

### Case 1: The Treatment of Darius's Family (Working)

After Issus (333 BCE), Alexander captured Darius's mother Sisygambis, his wife Stateira, and his children. The Persian women were terrified — Near Eastern conquest norms expected rape, enslavement, and murder.

Alexander:
- Sent word immediately that they would be treated as royalty
- Refused to look upon Stateira (considered the most beautiful woman in Asia), out of respect
- Permitted Sisygambis to retain her household and rank
- Later, when Stateira died in childbirth, he buried her with full honors and wept publicly

Why does this matter philosophically? Because it is the *Aristotelian-king move* executed perfectly. Alexander is presenting himself as the rightful king of Asia — not a conqueror taking women as loot, but the legitimate successor presiding over the royal household. Magnanimity here is not a feeling; it is *political philosophy applied*.

Sisygambis, who survived Darius by years, famously mourned Alexander's death as if he were her own son. The formation worked.

### Case 2: Porus at the Hydaspes (Working)

In 326 BCE, after defeating the Indian king Porus in a grueling battle that cost Alexander his beloved horse Bucephalus, Alexander asked the captured king how he wished to be treated.

*"As a king,"* Porus said.

Alexander returned Porus his kingdom and enlarged it.

Read this carefully. Alexander had spent months, at great cost, defeating this man. He now *gives him back more than he took*. Why?

Because Aristotelian kingship views the defeated king as a *potential ally* rather than a *reduced rival*. Porus, given his kingdom, became a loyal satrap. Porus, enslaved, would have been a rebellion waiting to happen. The strategic logic tracks the ethical logic exactly — which is itself Aristotle's point: virtue and flourishing are not opposed to practical success. Done well, they *are* practical success.

The formation worked here too.

### Case 3: The Burning of Persepolis (Failing)

In 330 BCE, after the Battle of Gaugamela and the flight of Darius, Alexander entered Persepolis — the Persian ceremonial capital. He looted it. And one night, in a heavy drinking party that included the Athenian courtesan Thais, Persepolis burned.

Ancient sources disagree on intent. Some say it was deliberate revenge for Xerxes's burning of Athens in 480 BCE. Some say it was drunken vandalism. Most modern historians suspect a mixture.

What is certain: Alexander had just been proclaiming himself the legitimate successor of Darius. He had been courting the Persian elite. He was about to pursue Darius as a usurper, not a rival. The burning of Persepolis *contradicted his own strategy*.

Plutarch tells us he regretted it the next day. He put the fires out.

This is the first major crack in the formation. The *thymos* — spirited rage at Persia's historical crimes against Greece — overrode the *phronesis* (practical wisdom) that Aristotle had tried to cultivate. The philosopher's student made a drunken decision a philosopher would not have endorsed.

### Case 4: The Murder of Cleitus (Failing Catastrophically)

Samarkand, 328 BCE. Alexander is now Great King. He has taken up Persian dress, Persian court ceremony, and attempts to introduce *proskynesis* — the prostration before the king that, in Macedonian and Greek eyes, is an act of worship.

His old friend Cleitus the Black, who had saved Alexander's life at the Granicus by cutting off the sword-arm of a Persian noble about to strike Alexander from behind — Cleitus is drunk, Alexander is drunk, and Cleitus begins to mock.

He says Philip won Alexander's battles. He says the Macedonians built Alexander, and Alexander is now betraying them for Persian fashions. He sneers at the divine-descent claim.

Alexander reaches for a weapon. Friends hold him back. Cleitus is hustled out. He comes back. He keeps shouting.

Alexander takes a spear from a palace guard and drives it through his friend's chest.

What happens next is philosophically important. Alexander does not celebrate. He does not justify. He is *shattered*. He locks himself in his tent for three days. He refuses food. He tries to kill himself. Callisthenes — Aristotle's nephew — and the philosopher Anaxarchus must physically intervene.

What does this tell us?

It tells us that **Alexander's conscience was fully functional** — the formation was real. He *knew* what he had done. He knew it violated everything Aristotle had taught him.

It also tells us that **knowing the right thing is not enough**. Aristotle's ethical system assumes that habituated virtue produces reliable action under pressure. Cleitus's murder is the empirical data that suggests otherwise: that a sufficiently trained, sufficiently drunk, sufficiently provoked man can, in one instant, destroy years of formation.

This is why the 15-18 lesson matters. You are old enough now to understand that your formation is being built to hold under pressure — but that pressure can, in extremity, exceed any formation. You must plan for both: to build the formation as strong as possible, *and* to structure your life so you are less often at the extremity.

### Case 5: The Gedrosian Desert March (Failing Strategically)

325 BCE. Alexander, returning from India, decides to march a large portion of his army through the Gedrosian Desert — modern Makran, in Pakistan and Iran. Water is almost nonexistent. The heat is extreme.

Ancient sources give conflicting reasons. Plutarch suggests punishment for the Hyphasis mutiny. Arrian suggests the desire to match the legendary march of Cyrus. Some moderns point to genuine strategic need (supplying the fleet).

Whatever the reason, as many as three-quarters of the marchers died. It is arguably the worst operational decision of Alexander's career.

Here is the *Pothos* reading — the one you should take seriously.

*Pothos*, for Alexander, was a word that meant "longing" or "yearning for what is beyond." It is the word ancient sources use to describe his visit to the oracle at Siwa, his push to the Indus, and his plans (unfulfilled) to circumnavigate Arabia. *Pothos* was his signature motivation.

Under the right circumstances, *pothos* produces Alexandria. It produces the founding of cities. It produces the willingness to do what has never been done.

Under the wrong circumstances, *pothos* produces Gedrosia. It produces the willingness to kill your own soldiers testing a limit you did not need to test.

*Pothos* is the virtue that has no natural brake. Unlike temperance or magnanimity, which have social cues that signal when enough is enough, *pothos* is *defined* by its refusal of "enough." It is the drive toward more.

This is why we say: the same thing that made Alexander great also destroyed him.

**For your own life, this is the hardest question raised by Alexander's:** if your signature virtue is the refusal of limits, what is your brake?

## The Integration Thesis

One of the most interesting things about Alexander is the scope of his vision. He was not simply conquering. He was attempting, at a cultural level, to integrate.

Evidence:
- **Mass weddings at Susa (324 BCE).** Alexander himself married Stateira (Darius's daughter). Hephaestion married another daughter. Eighty officers married Persian noblewomen. The goal: fuse Macedonian and Persian ruling classes.
- **The Epigonoi.** He ordered 30,000 young Persians trained in Macedonian tactics and equipped in Macedonian arms — a future integrated army.
- **Persian custom at court.** The diadem, the robe, the attempted *proskynesis* — these were not vanity. They were an attempt to be a legitimate king to both Macedonians and Persians simultaneously.
- **The founded cities.** Alexandrias from Egypt to the Hindu Kush — Greek civic institutions planted in Asian contexts, designed as hybrid cultural centers.

This project largely failed in the short term. The Macedonian wedding subjects mostly divorced their Persian wives after Alexander's death. The integrated army was never deployed at scale. The resentment between Macedonian veterans and Persian courtiers exploded at the Opis mutiny.

But in the long term, it succeeded spectacularly. The Hellenistic world that emerged from Alexander's successors was genuinely integrated: Greek language as the lingua franca of the Near East for a thousand years; Greek-educated Jewish philosophers (Philo of Alexandria); Greek-speaking Buddhist kings in Bactria; the Greek cities of Egypt, Syria, and Asia Minor that carried Roman and later Christian civilization.

Alexander saw something his generation did not. The question for you:

> *Where is the cultural integration your generation does not yet see?*

Nova Schola's own bet — that classical formation can reach every American family, not just an educated elite — is one possible answer. Think about what yours might be.

## The Succession Failure

Alexander died at thirty-two with no functioning succession.

Roxana was pregnant with his only son, but the child (Alexander IV) was not yet born. Alexander's half-brother Arrhidaeus was mentally unfit. The answer "to the strongest" guaranteed civil war.

Within forty years:
- Alexander's son was murdered (309 BCE)
- Roxana was murdered
- Olympias was murdered
- Alexander's generals — Ptolemy, Seleucus, Antigonus, Cassander, Lysimachus — had carved up the empire into rival kingdoms
- Macedon itself would eventually fall to Rome (148 BCE)

The empire, as empire, was gone within a generation.

**Why does this matter for your formation?**

Because the greatest failure of the most deliberately formed human of antiquity was not a battle. It was the failure to build an institution that outlasted him. He had a son on the way, a half-brother, a wife, a mother, a cohort of eight generals who had loved him since they were boys at Mieza. He could have named a successor. He could have built a structure. He did not.

The reason is almost certainly what ancient sources hint at: he believed, at some level, that he would live forever. The Olympias-built self-mythology — son of Zeus, new Achilles — had worked too well. The man who believes his own myth cannot plan for a world without him.

**The lesson for you:** the final test of formation is *building something that survives the formation*. A family. A school. A company. A body of work. A cohort of friends who will carry the work forward. This is the test that Alexander failed. Most people fail it. *Most people do not even try it.* Try.

## Comparative Analysis: Three Endings for the Same Virtue

### Alexander (d. 323 BCE, age 32)

The drive wins. He dies in motion, at the peak, with his empire unstructured and his succession unplanned. The cultural zone he created outlasts him by a thousand years; the political entity dies with him. *He was too drive, too early.*

### George Washington (d. 1799, age 67)

A man of comparable ambition who *gave power back*. Refused the crown offered by his officers. Stepped down after two terms. Returned to his farm at Mount Vernon. The nation he founded is still operating under the institutions he established.

The difference is not less *pothos*. Washington was intensely ambitious. The difference is that Washington chose *the institution* over *the man*. He could have been Alexander. He chose to be Cincinnatus.

### Napoleon Bonaparte (d. 1821, age 51)

A man self-consciously modeled on Alexander, who read Arrian on campaign, who explicitly imitated Alexander's strategy. Napoleon had the drive. He had the operational genius. He founded institutions — the Napoleonic Code survives to this day.

But Napoleon could not stop either. He died exiled on Saint Helena, defeated, having lost everything he had built. *He was too drive, too long.*

### The Question

All three men had enormous *pothos*. All three were the deliberate products of some kind of formation. One died in motion at the apex. One stepped down and built an institution that lasted. One pushed past his apex into ruin.

**Which ending do you want?**

## Practices for Students (Ages 15-18)

### 1. The Primary Source Project (4 weeks)

Read, in this order:

**Week 1:** Plutarch, *Life of Alexander* (Penguin translation, *The Age of Alexander*)  
**Week 2:** Arrian, *Anabasis of Alexander*, Book 1 (Penguin as *The Campaigns of Alexander*)  
**Week 3:** Arrian, Books 4–5 (Cleitus, Callisthenes, Porus, Hyphasis)  
**Week 4:** Selections from Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* Books I–II (on virtue as habituation) and *Politics* Book III (on kingship)

After each week, write 300–500 words on a single question:
- Week 1: What picture of Alexander's *character* does Plutarch construct, and how does he build it?
- Week 2: How does Arrian, as a military man, evaluate Alexander's command?
- Week 3: Does Arrian condemn or defend Alexander in the Cleitus episode? How do you know?
- Week 4: Where do you see Aristotle's ethics in Alexander's actions — and where do you see Aristotle's ethics violated?

---

### 2. The *Pothos* Self-Audit

Name your own *pothos* — the drive that pushes you toward more-than-enough. It may not be glory. It may be intellectual completeness, athletic excellence, aesthetic perfection, social influence, or something else.

In a notebook, over two weeks, answer:

1. **What specifically is the drive?** (Name it. Don't be modest.)
2. **When did it first show up?** (Age, circumstance, story.)
3. **What has it produced that is good?** (Be specific.)
4. **What has it cost you, or people around you, that is bad?** (Be honest.)
5. **Does it have a natural brake?** (Be honest again.)
6. **If not — what external brake are you installing?** (Coach, spouse, mentor, sabbath, deadline, accountability partner.)

This is not a journaling exercise. This is a *system design* exercise. You are engineering a governor on the engine of your drive.

---

### 3. The Mieza Thesis

Write a 5–8 page essay on this question:

> **Is Nova Schola's wager — that greatness can be formed — proven by Alexander's life, refuted by it, or something more complicated?**

Requirements:
- Cite at least one primary source (Plutarch, Arrian, or Aristotle) with specific passages
- Make a clear thesis in the first paragraph
- Consider at least one counter-argument
- Connect to your own life in the final page — what does your answer imply for how you should be forming yourself right now?

This is the thesis that the next four years of your education should in some sense be trying to answer.

---

### 4. The Succession Exercise

Pick a project, role, or responsibility you currently hold that *matters* — something you would not want to vanish if you vanished.

It could be:
- A club or team you lead
- A family responsibility (a younger sibling, a grandparent's routine)
- A creative project (a novel, a game, a piece of music)
- A friendship circle you organize
- A small business or online presence

Now, write a succession plan. Two pages. Answer:

1. If I disappeared for six months, what would break first?
2. Who else could do this, and what would I need to teach them?
3. What documentation or structure would I need to create?
4. What is the minimum viable version of this thing without me?
5. When is my target date to have the succession in place?

Then *execute it*. In the next 90 days, make the thing capable of surviving your absence.

This is the practice Alexander did not do. Practicing it once — even at small scale — permanently changes how you relate to your own work.

---

### 5. The Cleitus Journal

For two weeks, every night, spend five minutes answering one question:

> *Where today did my temper, my impatience, or my drive come closer to hurting someone I care about than it should have?*

You are not confessing sins. You are *mapping your thymos* — noticing where it rises, what triggers it, how it felt, how near you came to saying something you would have regretted.

At the end of two weeks, look for patterns. You are looking for the *shape* of your wrath. Everyone has one. Knowing yours is the difference between Alexander killing Cleitus and Alexander walking out of the room.

## Discussion Questions (Philosophical & Historical)

### On Formation
1. Aristotle believed virtue is habituated — that you become good by practicing good actions. Alexander, at twenty-eight, murdered his best friend in a drunken rage. Does Cleitus's murder refute Aristotle's ethics? Or does it confirm them (by showing what happens when the habituation is disrupted)?
2. How much of Alexander's greatness is attributable to each of: Philip, Olympias, Leonidas, Lysimachus, Aristotle, the cohort at Mieza, his own nature? Rank them and defend your ranking.
3. Could the same formation, applied to a different child, have produced the same outcome? What does your answer imply about the relationship between formation and nature?

### On Virtue and Vice
4. Is *pothos* a virtue, a vice, or something that can be either depending on context? If it can be either, what determines which it becomes?
5. Alexander's magnanimity toward Darius's family and Porus coexists with his cruelty to Thebes and Tyre. Is this inconsistency? Strategy? Something else? How does it illuminate what magnanimity actually is?
6. Aristotle wrote of *megalopsychia* (greatness of soul) as a virtue. Did Alexander possess *megalopsychia*, or merely its counterfeit, *hybris*? How can you tell the difference?

### On Leadership and Institutions
7. Washington stepped down after two terms. Alexander could not stop. Napoleon could not stop. What virtue did Washington have that the others lacked?
8. The Hellenistic world that Alexander's successors built lasted a thousand years. The political empire fractured within a generation. In what sense was Alexander's project successful, and in what sense was it a failure?
9. If you had been Alexander at the Hyphasis River, with your army refusing to go further — what would you have done?

### On Your Own Formation
10. Who is playing Aristotle in your life right now? Who should be?
11. Who is in your Mieza cohort? If nobody, how will you build one?
12. What is your *pothos*, and what is its brake?
13. What are you building that must survive you?

## Parent / Teacher Guide

### What to Emphasize at This Age
- **The experimental nature of the formation.** Alexander is a case study, not a hero story. Teach it as evidence to be evaluated.
- **The philosophical framework.** Students this age can handle Aristotle in small doses. Don't skip the ethics.
- **The political imagination.** The Hellenistic synthesis — Greek cities across Asia, *koine* Greek as a world language — is one of the great cultural projects in history. Teach it.

### What to Complicate
- **The violence.** Be honest. Alexander killed a lot of people who did not need to die. This is not balanced by Alexandria.
- **The religion.** The divine-descent claim is interesting as formation technique *and* as a case study in what happens when a parent's narrative is taken literally by a child with unlimited power.
- **The sexuality.** Alexander's relationship with Hephaestion is almost certainly to be understood as a loving, likely sexual, bond modeled on Achilles-Patroclus. Parents and teachers will navigate this according to their own values; the important thing is not to *erase* the grief over Hephaestion, which is a major part of Alexander's final year.

### Advanced Extensions
- Read Arrian's Book 7 on the Opis mutiny and Alexander's death — the most operationally rich ancient narrative we have
- Compare Alexander and Julius Caesar: two charismatic field commanders who fused personal and political authority. Why does Caesar's project survive him and Alexander's doesn't?
- Engage with Paul Cartledge's *Alexander the Great: The Hunt for a New Past* — a sophisticated modern reading that emphasizes the sources' limits
- Read Quintus Curtius Rufus on the darker episodes; compare his account with Arrian's on the same events

### Writing Assignments
- The Mieza Thesis (see Practice #3)
- A 5-page paper: "Why was Alexander so often magnanimous to strangers and so often cruel to friends?"
- A 3-page paper: "Alexander, Washington, Napoleon: which of them got the relationship between drive and institution right?"
- A creative piece: imagine you are Aristotle in 323 BCE, receiving the news of Alexander's death. Write a letter to a friend.

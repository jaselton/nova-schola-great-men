# Alexander the Great: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Alexander the Great to one or more children. It is designed to be used by a parent or teacher at home, with no specialized materials. Total time commitment: roughly **45–75 minutes per day**, divisible into morning and afternoon sessions.

The plan is calibrated for **ages 8-14** as its default. Extensions and substitutions for ages 15-18 are marked throughout.

The week moves through: **Story → Observation → Discussion → Hands-on Project → Reflection**. This is the classical rhythm: encounter, practice, examine, make, internalize.

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for the child (the "Alexander notebook" for the week)
- Pencils, pens
- A printed map of the Mediterranean and Near East (or a wall map / globe)
- String, tape, and scissors (for the Knot Day project)
- Optional: modeling clay or small toy figures for battle-reconstruction
- Access to pour water on sand (a sandbox, a planter, a beach if nearby)
- A copy of Plutarch's *Life of Alexander* (any translation, or online)
- A picture-book biography of Alexander for younger children (see parent guide)

### Choose Your Age Band

Pick the student lesson appropriate to your child(ren):
- [Ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md)
- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

If you have multiple children across bands, have each read their own lesson; the week's activities below work across ages with minor adjustment.

---

## Monday: The Story — Formation, Bucephalus, the Front Line

### Morning: Read Together (20–30 min)

Read the age-appropriate student lesson aloud, or have the student read it independently and then discuss.

**Key moments to pause on:**
- The list of tutors (Leonidas, Lysimachus, Aristotle) and what each contributed
- The Bucephalus story — stop and ask: *"What did Alexander see that the adults missed?"*
- Philip's reaction: *"Seek a kingdom worthy of yourself. Macedon is too small for you."* — ask: *"How would that sentence feel, said to you?"*

**For 15-18:** Additionally read the opening of Plutarch's *Life of Alexander* (chapters 1–7) — this covers birth, upbringing, and Bucephalus in Plutarch's own words.

### Afternoon: The Pouring Activity (15 min)

Take your child outside. Fill a cup, a bottle, or a helmet (if you have something helmet-shaped) with water.

Tell them: *"Alexander's soldiers brought him this much water in the Gedrosian Desert. They were all dying of thirst. This was enough for one person."*

Ask them to imagine being the soldier who brought it.

Then pour the water into sand, slowly.

*"He said: 'I will not drink while my men are thirsty.' And then he kept marching."*

Say nothing else. Walk back inside. Do not explain the lesson. Let it work.

**For 15-18:** Do this activity. Then ask: *"Was this leadership or theater? How would you know the difference?"* Discuss.

### Homework

In the Alexander notebook:
- Write today's date, then: "Day 1: The Shadow-Horse and the Helmet of Water."
- Draw Bucephalus, or draw the shadow.
- Write one sentence: "The thing I want to learn to see that I'm not seeing is ______."

---

## Tuesday: Observation — The Bucephalus Exercise

### Morning: Memory Work (5 min)

Learn and recite together:

> **"There is nothing impossible to him who will try."**

Repeat three times. At the end of the day, ask the child to recite from memory.

**For 11-14, add:** *"We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit."* (Aristotelian paraphrase)

**For 15-18, add:** *"I will not steal a victory."* (Alexander to Parmenion before Gaugamela — the line that captures Aristotelian virtue under command pressure)

### Afternoon: The Bucephalus Exercise (30–40 min)

This is the core of the week. Here is how it works:

**Step 1: Pick a "stuck" thing.**
The child names one small thing in their life that feels impossible or that grown-ups have said is too hard. Examples: a specific skill (jumping rope double-unders, a handstand), a homework area (long division, a spelling pattern), a habit (making the bed without being reminded), a puzzle or project.

**Step 2: The ten-minute watch.**
Before trying the thing, the child must watch it for ten minutes. If it's a skill, watch other people do it (a video, a sibling). If it's a task, study what makes it hard. No attempting yet. Just *watching*.

**Step 3: Name what the adults missed.**
Alexander noticed Bucephalus was shying at his shadow. What is your child noticing? They write one sentence in the notebook: *"What I noticed is ______."*

**Step 4: One attempt.**
Now try it, using what they noticed.

**Step 5: Record.**
Win or fail, write in the notebook:
- What did I try?
- What happened?
- If I try again tomorrow, what will I do differently?

**This exercise runs all week.** Tuesday is the setup. Wednesday, Thursday, and Friday the child should be returning to their Bucephalus problem and tracking progress.

**For 15-18:** In addition to or instead of a physical skill, pick a *cognitive* Bucephalus — a piece of schoolwork that has defeated you. Apply the same method: watch (read the material without attempting), name the frame (what do teachers assume about this subject that might be wrong for me?), attempt one reframing.

## Wednesday: Discussion — Aristotle, Formation, the Gordian Knot

### Morning: Socratic Discussion (20–30 min)

Sit down with the child. Talk, don't lecture. Use these questions in order, letting each answer lead the next.

**For 8-10:**
1. Who were Alexander's teachers? What did each one give him?
2. If your parents could pick any teacher in the world to teach you one thing — who, and what?
3. Why do you think Alexander slept with his book of the *Iliad* under his pillow?
4. What's one thing you want to be so good at that you'd sleep with the book about it?

**For 11-14:**
1. Philip could have hired a general. He hired a philosopher. Why?
2. What is the difference between being *taught* and being *formed*?
3. Alexander learned alongside a cohort at Mieza. Who is in your cohort? Who should be?
4. The Gordian Knot: cheating, cleverness, or reframing? How can you tell the difference?

**For 15-18:**
1. Aristotle believed virtue is habituated. Test this against Alexander's Cleitus episode — did the habituation fail, or was Cleitus the exception that proves the rule?
2. If you had three years with one of the greatest philosophers alive, what would you want them to teach you?
3. The Hellenistic world Alexander founded outlasted his political empire by a thousand years. Was his life a success or a failure? By what measure?

### Afternoon: The Knot Project (20–30 min)

**For 8-14:** Get string or a soft cord. Tie one complicated knot — a real one, or just a messy tangle.

Take turns trying to untie it without cutting. Time each attempt.

When everyone is frustrated, pull out scissors.

Ask: *"If a famous oracle said whoever 'loosed' this knot would rule Asia — would you cut it?"*

Discuss:
- Is cutting cheating? Or is the oracle's word "loose" itself ambiguous?
- If you cut it, have you earned the kingdom, or not?
- What's the difference between *cheating* (breaking a rule that was meant seriously) and *reframing* (finding a method the rule didn't anticipate)?

There isn't one right answer. The goal is that the child has *thought* about it.

**For 15-18:** Skip the physical knot. Read Arrian's account of the Gordian Knot (Book 2, Chapter 3) directly. Then write a one-page response: *"When Alexander cut the Gordian Knot, did he deserve the kingdom?"*

### Homework: Copywork

Have the child copy, by hand, slowly, in their best penmanship:

> *"There is nothing impossible to him who will try."*
> *— Alexander*

Below it, copy this:

> *"I owe my father my life. I owe Aristotle the life worth living."*
> *— Alexander, on his teachers*

## Thursday: Hands-On Project — The Siege of Tyre

### All Day: Build a Mole (60–90 min total)

This is the day the child *makes* something.

The story:
- Tyre was an island half a mile offshore, walled, impregnable
- Everyone said Alexander could not take it
- He built a road through the sea

**The Project:**

You need a large basin of water (a bathtub, a wading pool, a shallow plastic tub on the porch). You need small stones, pebbles, sticks, and heavy objects.

Place a small "island" in the middle of the water — a plate, a stone, a small plastic container. This is Tyre.

Give the child this challenge: *build a causeway from the shore to the island using only the materials on hand.*

They will:
- Try stones first. The stones sink.
- Need more stones. The pile grows slowly.
- Get frustrated as the water current undoes their work.
- Need a wider base than they expected.
- Eventually succeed — or not.

**During the build, ask:**
- How many stones would it take to build a real mole across half a mile of ocean?
- How many men would be needed to carry and place them?
- How would you protect the mole from storms?
- How would you defend it from people on the island throwing rocks at you?

**After the build:**

Alexander's siege lasted *seven months*. His engineers built siege towers *on the mole*. The Tyrians used fire-ships to burn the mole multiple times. Alexander rebuilt every time.

Write in the notebook:
- What did I build today?
- How long did it take?
- Alexander did this for seven months. What does seven months of *this* feel like?

**For 15-18:** Do the physical build, then read Arrian's account of the siege (Book 2, Chapters 17–24). Write a one-page analysis: *"What is the difference between persistence and obsession? How do you tell — at the time — which one you are in?"*

### Evening Reflection

Before bed:
- Return to the Bucephalus problem from Tuesday.
- Have you tried it again today?
- What did you notice?

## Friday: Reflection — Narration, Map, Commitment

### Morning: Narration (15–20 min)

**What is narration?**  
In classical education, "narration" is the practice of telling back what you've learned, in your own words, without notes. It is the single most powerful tool for consolidating knowledge.

**How to do it:**
1. Close the notebook and student lesson.
2. The child tells the story of Alexander's life, or the part of it they remember, in their own words, aloud, for 3–10 minutes.
3. You listen without interrupting except to gently prompt ("What happened next?" / "Who was that, again?").
4. After, ask: *"What part do you want to remember most?"*

**For 15-18:** Instead of telling the story, write it. A 500-word essay: *"The most important thing I learned from Alexander this week, and why."*

### Afternoon: The Map and the Scale

Lay out the Mediterranean-and-Near-East map. Together, trace Alexander's route:

1. **Pella** (Macedon) → **Troy** (Achilles's tomb) → **Granicus** → **Gordium** → **Issus** → **Tyre** → **Egypt / Alexandria / Siwa** → **Gaugamela** → **Babylon** → **Persepolis** → **Samarkand (Cleitus)** → **Hydaspes River (Porus)** → **Hyphasis River (army refuses)** → **Gedrosian Desert** → **Babylon (dies)**

**Together, measure approximate distance.** (It comes out to more than 10,000 miles.)

**Ask:**
- Most of this was *on foot*. How long do you think it took?
- (It took 11 years.)
- How old was Alexander when he started? (22.) When he died? (32.)
- What had he done by 32 that most people don't do by 80?

### The Commitment: The One Thing

At the end of the week, the child picks **one thing from Alexander's life that they will carry forward**.

This is not "I will work harder." It is specific. Examples:

- *"I will finish my Bucephalus problem, even if it takes weeks."*
- *"I will find three friends to read one book with."* (Mieza cohort)
- *"I will look at one thing every day for ten minutes without doing anything else."* (observation)
- *"I will memorize the first fifty lines of Homer's *Iliad*."* (like Alexander)
- *"I will lead from the front on one chore a day for a month."* (front-line)
- *"I will name my *pothos* and install a brake."* (15-18)

They write it at the top of a new page in the notebook. They date it.

Refer back to this page at least once a month for the next year.

## Extension Ideas (If You Want More)

### Art & Images
- Look at ancient coins bearing Alexander's portrait (easy to find online)
- Look at Hellenistic sculpture — the sudden rise of realistic portraiture after Alexander
- Find images of the Alexander Sarcophagus (in Istanbul Archaeological Museum)
- Renaissance paintings of Alexander: Veronese, Altdorfer's *The Battle of Alexander at Issus*

### Books
- **Ages 8-10:** Demi, *Alexander the Great* (picture book)
- **Ages 11-14:** Anthony Everitt, *Alexander the Great* — accessible narrative
- **Ages 15-18:** Plutarch, *Life of Alexander*; Arrian's *Anabasis*; Mary Renault's *Fire from Heaven* (novel — preview for teen appropriateness)

### Field Trip Ideas
- Any Greek or Roman archaeology in a nearby museum
- A Hellenistic-era coin or inscription (surprisingly common in US collections)
- A planetarium — Aristotle, Alexander's tutor, made astronomical observations that shaped Western cosmology for centuries

### Deep Dive
- Watch the *In Our Time* (BBC) episode on Alexander the Great (free podcast)
- Watch the *Crash Course* World History episode on Alexander (free on YouTube, good for 11-14)
- For 15-18: Read Book 7 of Arrian — the end of Alexander's life, the Opis mutiny, the death of Hephaestion, the deathbed
- For 15-18: Read selections of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* Book II (on habituated virtue) and return to the Cleitus question

## Assessment: How Do You Know They Learned?

By the end of the week, the child should be able to (at their age band):

**All ages:**
- Name Alexander's main tutors and say something specific about each
- Tell the Bucephalus story in their own words
- Identify at least one example of Alexander reframing an "impossible" problem
- Recite *"There is nothing impossible to him who will try."*
- Find at least six of Alexander's key locations on a map

**Ages 11+:**
- Explain why Philip hired a philosopher (Aristotle) to teach a future king
- Articulate what the Gordian Knot teaches about reframing vs. cheating
- Identify one cost of Alexander's *pothos* — something the drive damaged

**Ages 15-18:**
- Make a substantive argument about whether Alexander's life proves or refutes the Nova Schola formation thesis
- Name their own *pothos* and the brake they are installing
- Compare Alexander with at least one other figure (Washington, Napoleon, Socrates) on the handling of drive

If they can do this, the week has worked. If they cannot yet, do not panic — give the material time. You are stocking a soul. The return on this investment will come years from now.

---

## What's Next?

Alexander opens the Nova Schola curriculum's engagement with the ancients. The next profiles in sequence are:

- **Plato** — the philosopher who shaped Aristotle, who shaped Alexander
- **Aristotle** — not only Alexander's teacher but the organizer of all Greek knowledge
- **Socrates** — the teacher of Plato, and the alternative to the Alexandrian life: excellence without empire
- **Marcus Aurelius** — the philosopher-king Alexander was trained to be and did not quite become

A family that walks this arc deliberately — Alexander, then Plato, then Aristotle, then Socrates, then Marcus Aurelius — will have traced, in five weeks, the full circuit of the Great Man framework: mentor, student, philosopher, action, reflection.

Alexander is the hook. The ones who follow are the deeper water.

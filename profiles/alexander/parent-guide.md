# Alexander the Great: Parent Teaching Guide

---

## Overview: What This Profile Teaches

Alexander the Great is not optional for a classical education. He is the single most important test case for the entire Nova Schola thesis, which says:

> *Greatness can be formed. The method is mentorship and expectation.*

Aristotle tutoring Alexander is the paradigm case — the moment in history that the entire Great Man framework points to. If formation works, Alexander is the proof. If formation has limits, Alexander is also the proof of those limits. Your child needs both halves.

This guide helps you teach the whole Alexander — the engineered king *and* the warning. It is calibrated by age so you can pitch the material appropriately without watering it down.

---

## Age-Appropriate Emphases

### Ages 8-10: Observation, Courage, Shared Hardship

**Core story:** Bucephalus. The twelve-year-old who saw what adults missed.

**Secondary stories:** Aristotle as tutor. The charge at Chaeronea. The helmet of water poured in the sand. The seven-month siege of Tyre.

**What to teach:**
- Observation as a skill (not just "looking around")
- That strict teachers can be loving teachers
- That leaders go first — they share the thirst, the cold, the danger
- That "impossible" often means "nobody has been stubborn enough yet"

**What to leave for later:**
- The murder of Cleitus
- The burning of Persepolis
- The casualties of the Gedrosian Desert
- The divine-descent claims (mention lightly; don't theologize)
- The death of Hephaestion and its aftermath
- The assassination of Philip

**Success at this age looks like:** your child can tell the Bucephalus story in their own words and can identify one small "impossible" thing in their own life that might be reframed. They memorize *"There is nothing impossible to him who will try."*

---

### Ages 11-14: Formation, Reframing, the First Crack

**Core story:** Aristotle at Mieza and the school that formed a generation of rulers.

**Secondary stories:** Bucephalus (as formation evidence, not just cleverness). The Gordian Knot. The Siege of Tyre. The first hint of the Cleitus episode (without lingering).

**What to teach:**
- Formation as a *deliberate project* — not accident, not destiny
- The cohort principle — Alexander learned alongside friends, not alone
- The Gordian reframing move as a learnable skill
- The beginning of the warning: even the best education has limits

**What to complicate:**
- The divine-descent narrative — is this good or bad parenting? Let your child think about it.
- The violence at Thebes and Tyre — it's not all heroism.
- The relationship between capability and character — they are not the same thing.

**What to still protect from (mostly):**
- The full Cleitus story — mention, but don't dwell
- The detail of Gedrosian casualties
- Philip's polygamy and the assassination politics (keep this abstract)

**Success at this age looks like:** your child can explain why Philip hired Aristotle (and why it was expensive), can identify the Gordian reframing move in their own life, and can name one thing about Alexander's life that worries them.

---

### Ages 15-18: *Pothos*, Succession, the Limits of Education

**Core story:** The whole arc. Formation → capability → the drive exceeding the formation → the failure to plan beyond oneself.

**Secondary stories:** The Cleitus murder in full. The Gedrosian Desert. The failure of succession. The comparative analysis with Washington and Napoleon.

**What to teach:**
- Aristotle's ethical framework (virtue as habituation)
- *Pothos* as a virtue that has no natural brake
- The difference between cultivating a drive and being consumed by it
- That the final test of formation is building something that survives the formation

**What to engage head-on:**
- The limits of education. Even Aristotle teaching for three years could not eliminate Alexander's wrath.
- The relationship between glory and institution. Washington built an institution; Alexander did not.
- The comparative ethics of conquest. Don't romanticize. Don't flinch.

**What is now age-appropriate:**
- Polygamy and succession politics at the Macedonian court
- Alexander's relationship with Hephaestion (handle per your family's values, but don't erase it)
- The theological claims and the Siwa oracle (as intellectual history)
- Drunkenness as a persistent operational failure

**Success at this age looks like:** your child can make a clear argument about whether Alexander's life proves or refutes the Nova Schola thesis, can name their own *pothos* and its brake, and can describe one thing they are building that must survive them.

## Common Misconceptions to Address

### Misconception 1: "Alexander was a natural prodigy."

**Reality:** Alexander was the most deliberately formed human in antiquity. His capability was manufactured — deliberately, expensively, over twenty years — by a father who was a military genius, a mother who was a master of identity construction, a severe tutor (Leonidas), an imaginative tutor (Lysimachus), and the greatest philosopher then living (Aristotle). Formation *worked*. That is the point. If your child comes away thinking Alexander was "born great," they have missed the Nova Schola lesson entirely.

### Misconception 2: "Aristotle failed, because Alexander ended up killing Cleitus."

**Reality:** Aristotle did not fail. He achieved something extraordinary: a student who, at twenty-eight, founded cities as institutions, showed magnanimity to defeated kings, sent specimens of Indian flora back to his tutor, and could articulate the difference between a king and a tyrant in his own actions. What Aristotle did not do was *guarantee* that Alexander would always act on the virtue he had been taught. Nothing could guarantee that. The lesson is that formation produces capability reliably; it does not produce perfection. Your child should leave admiring what Aristotle accomplished *and* sober about what no education can accomplish.

### Misconception 3: "Alexander's conquests were good because they spread Greek culture."

**Reality:** This is a partial truth told as a whole truth. Alexander's conquests spread Greek language, civic institutions, and cultural synthesis across Asia — that is real, and its importance should not be minimized. But those conquests also killed enormous numbers of people, destroyed Thebes, enslaved Tyre, and pressed Alexander's own soldiers past their limits. A serious Great Man education does not present conquest as unambiguously good. It presents it as the occasion where we can observe virtue and vice in action, *not* as the thing we are teaching our children to imitate.

### Misconception 4: "So the lesson is Alexander was bad."

**Reality:** No. The lesson is that Alexander was the *test case*. He demonstrates both what formation can achieve (extraordinary capability, genuine virtues of courage, magnanimity, intellectual curiosity, strategic reframing) and what formation cannot eliminate (wrath, the inability to stop, the failure to plan beyond oneself). Your child is not being asked to decide whether Alexander was a good man. Your child is being asked to learn *from* Alexander — both from his virtues and from his warnings.

### Misconception 5: "If I want my child to be formed this way, we need Aristotle."

**Reality:** You don't need Aristotle. You need *to take formation seriously*. The deliberate choices Philip and Olympias made — severe tutors, role-play tutors, a philosophical framework, a cohort of peers, progressive responsibility, explicit expectations of greatness — are available to any committed family. Nova Schola's bet is that the method Philip used is not the property of kings. It's the property of parents who commit to it. You are the Philip in your child's life. The Aristotle role you can fill through curriculum, books, online tutors, and the compounding power of time.

## Discussion Facilitation Tips

### For Younger Students (8-10)

- **Slow down on Bucephalus.** Let them sit with what Alexander noticed. Ask: *"What was the grown-ups' mistake? Why did they miss it?"* Most will say "they didn't look." Correct gently: they *looked*. They didn't *watch*.
- **Make the helmet-of-water story physical.** If you can, pour water into sand together. Talk about what the soldiers must have felt watching their king do that. Don't explain the lesson. Let it land.
- **Use the Chaeronea story to counter the lowered-expectations culture.** A phrase I recommend: *"Alexander was eighteen at Chaeronea. I think kids today are treated as though they are much less capable than they are."*
- **Do not moralize the conquest.** At this age, Alexander is primarily about *character.* Save the moral evaluation for 15-18.

### For Middle Students (11-14)

- **Run the formation project as a thought experiment.** Ask: "If you were Philip, and you had the money to build *any* education for your child — what would you build?" Then show them what Philip actually built. Compare.
- **Take the Gordian Knot seriously.** Don't let them dismiss it as "cheating." Ask: "What is the difference between cheating and reframing a problem?" Push them to articulate it. There is a real answer, and it matters.
- **Introduce the warning at this age, in small doses.** Mention that Alexander "could not stop" and that this caused problems. Don't do the full Cleitus story yet, but plant the seed that even formed greatness has dangers.
- **Use the cohort principle on them.** Help them identify 3–5 peers they could form alongside. If they can't, that is the problem you should work on with them.

### For Older Students (15-18)

- **Make it philosophically rigorous.** Aristotle's ethics is the hidden layer under the biography. Older students can handle Books I–II of the *Nicomachean Ethics*. Don't skip it.
- **Do not protect them from the Cleitus episode.** This is the centerpiece of the 15-18 lesson. The full story — the drunkenness, the murder, the three days in the tent — is what they need to grapple with.
- **Run the comparative analysis with Washington and Napoleon.** Three endings for the same virtue: dying at the peak (Alexander), stepping down (Washington), pushing past the peak into ruin (Napoleon). This is the most useful ethical exercise in the profile.
- **Require the *pothos* self-audit.** It is the exercise most likely to actually change the trajectory of a teenager's life.

## Sensitive Topics & How to Handle Them

### Violence

Alexander's career is violent. Thebes was destroyed, Tyre enslaved, Gaza massacred. Be honest about this with students old enough to understand it (roughly 11 and up). *Do not sanitize.* Classical education fails when it presents conquerors as uncomplicated heroes. It also fails when it presents them as uncomplicated monsters. The honest account is harder and produces better thinkers.

### Drunkenness

Alexander drank heavily, as did the Macedonian court. Drunkenness is the proximate cause of the burning of Persepolis *and* the murder of Cleitus. Don't hide this. For teenagers especially, the lesson is directly applicable: if the most trained man of antiquity could not reliably override his drunk self, neither can you. Your formation does not apply when you are impaired. *Stay sober during moments of power.*

### The Hephaestion Relationship

Alexander and Hephaestion were almost certainly lovers, modeled on Achilles and Patroclus. Different families will navigate this differently. I recommend:

- **For 8-10:** Say "his best friend" and leave it at that.
- **For 11-14:** Say "his closest friend — probably his dearest person in the world — modeled on Achilles and Patroclus in the *Iliad*." Most will not ask further. If asked, you can note that the Greek world understood close male friendships differently than modern cultures.
- **For 15-18:** Engage directly. The relationship is part of the formation. The grief over Hephaestion's death is part of Alexander's final year. Whatever your values, *do not erase the bond* — the erasure is a historical falsification.

### The Divine-Descent Claims

Olympias told Alexander he was descended from Zeus. Alexander, at Siwa, was told he was Son of Ammon. He reportedly began to style himself as semi-divine.

Handle this at three levels:

- **Formation technique:** Olympias was building an identity. "You are descended from heroes" is a specific kind of parenting choice. Discuss whether it's good or bad parenting. There isn't one answer.
- **Historical practice:** Near Eastern kingship routinely involved divine claims. Alexander's behavior made political sense in its context.
- **Ethical warning:** By the end of his life, Alexander appears to have believed parts of his own mythology. This is the limit of identity-building. The story you tell a child can become the story they cannot escape.

### The Assassination of Philip

Philip was assassinated in 336 BCE. Some ancient sources (and modern historians) raise the possibility that Olympias and Alexander had some knowledge. This is never proven.

For younger children: "His father was killed, and Alexander became king at twenty." That's enough.

For older students: discuss the ambiguity. The fact that we cannot know is itself an important historical lesson — court politics at that level leaves no clean record.

## Extension Activities (Beyond Core Lessons)

### Field Trips & Experiences
- Visit a natural history museum — Aristotle was a naturalist, and the expedition he trained also collected specimens
- Attend a performance of a Greek tragedy (Sophocles, Aeschylus, Euripides) — these were part of the Mieza curriculum
- Visit any Greek or Roman archaeology collection in your area — coinage, armor, inscriptions
- Take a multi-day hike that pushes the child's current endurance — physical experience of the campaign life is irreplaceable

### Additional Reading

**For 8-10:**
- Demi, *Alexander the Great* (picture book)
- Jessie Hartland, *Alexander the Great*

**For 11-14:**
- Anthony Everitt, *Alexander the Great* (accessible narrative biography)
- Mary Renault, *Fire from Heaven* (Alexander's youth, historical novel; preview)
- Mary Renault, *The Persian Boy* (Alexander's later life; preview for teen appropriateness)

**For 15-18:**
- Plutarch, *Life of Alexander* (in *The Age of Alexander*)
- Arrian, *The Campaigns of Alexander* (Penguin Classics)
- Paul Cartledge, *Alexander the Great: The Hunt for a New Past*
- Philip Freeman, *Alexander the Great*
- Relevant books of Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (I, II, VI)

### Project Ideas

1. **The Map Project** — Draw Alexander's route on a large wall map. Mark battles, cities founded, key episodes. Keep it visible through the week.
2. **The Coin Project** — Research Alexander's coinage (Hellenistic tetradrachms are stunning). Discuss how coinage propagates a ruler's image.
3. **The Founding Exercise** — Have the child design their own "Alexandria." Where would they found a city today? What would be in it? Why?
4. **The *Iliad* Reading** — Read Book 22 (Achilles kills Hector) aloud. This is the story Alexander carried in his pillow. It's unmissable.
5. **The Family Debate** — Stage a formal debate: *"Resolved: Alexander's life is a model worth imitating."* Assign sides by coin-flip. Make both sides argue seriously.

## Integration with Other Subjects

### How Alexander Connects to Your Curriculum

**History / Social Studies**
- The transition from Classical Greek to Hellenistic world
- The Persian Empire (Achaemenid) — previous lesson context
- Roman reception of Alexander (Caesar, Pompey) — later lesson
- The Silk Road and the cultural zones Alexander helped establish

**Philosophy / Ethics**
- Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* (direct connection)
- Virtue ethics as a framework
- The philosophical problem of great men (Carlyle, Hegel, modern debates)

**Literature**
- Homer's *Iliad* (Alexander's operational text)
- Plutarch's *Parallel Lives* as a literary genre
- The "Alexander Romance" tradition across Persian, Arabic, and medieval literatures
- Mary Renault's historical novels (for 15-18)

**Geography**
- The Mediterranean and Near East
- The Hindu Kush and Central Asian geography
- The Indus Valley
- Campaign logistics — why terrain matters

**Mathematics / Logistics**
- Army sizes and supply lines (a serious logistical curriculum can be built around the Macedonian army)
- Siege engineering (Tyre, Gaza, Sogdian Rock)
- Distance and time (how long does it take to march 10,000 miles?)

**Natural Science**
- Aristotle's biology and Alexander's specimen-collecting
- Mediterranean ecology vs. Near Eastern ecology
- The horse (Bucephalus) as a biological and cultural object

**Music / Art**
- Hellenistic sculpture (the sudden rise of realistic portraiture)
- Depictions of Alexander through history (Roman, medieval, Renaissance, modern)
- Folk traditions about Alexander across cultures (Iskandar in Persian, Dhul-Qarnayn in Arabic)

## Assessment: How Do You Know They've Learned?

### Younger Students (8-10)

Your child has learned Alexander at this age if they can:

1. Tell the Bucephalus story in their own words, including *why* Alexander saw what adults missed
2. Name Aristotle as Alexander's teacher and say one thing about him
3. Explain why Alexander poured the water into the sand
4. Identify one "impossible" thing in their own life and try to reframe it
5. Recite *"There is nothing impossible to him who will try."*

Bonus: they begin spontaneously using Alexander as a reference ("I'm going to Bucephalus this problem").

### Middle Students (11-14)

Your child has learned Alexander at this age if they can:

1. Explain what made Philip's choice of Aristotle as tutor so significant
2. Identify three examples of Alexander reframing an "impossible" problem
3. Name the two sides of Alexander's character — what the formation achieved and what it couldn't prevent
4. Articulate why having a cohort (Mieza) mattered, and identify their own emerging cohort
5. Point on a map to at least six of Alexander's key locations

Bonus: they can explain the Gordian Knot distinction between cheating and reframing in their own words.

### Older Students (15-18)

Your student has learned Alexander at this age if they can:

1. Make a substantive argument about whether Alexander's life proves or refutes the Nova Schola formation thesis
2. Explain Aristotle's concept of virtue as habituation and identify where Alexander's actions conform to or violate it
3. Name their own *pothos* and describe the brake they are installing
4. Analyze the Cleitus episode philosophically — what it tells us about the limits of education
5. Compare Alexander, Washington, and Napoleon as three endings for the same ambition
6. Describe one thing they are building that must survive them and articulate a succession plan for it

Bonus: they begin making their educational choices differently because of Alexander. They seek out formation. They ask who their Aristotle is. They build their Mieza cohort deliberately.

## What Success Looks Like

### Short-term (During the lesson/week)

- Your child brings up Bucephalus or Aristotle or the Gordian Knot unprompted in conversation
- They attempt the Gordian reframing move on a small problem
- They ask questions that weren't in the lesson ("Wait, so what happened to Bucephalus?")
- They argue with a point in the material (especially the 15-18 age) — this is the goal, not a problem
- They want to read more (biographies, historical fiction, primary sources)

### Medium-term (Month after)

- They spontaneously reference Alexander in discussions of current events, school conflicts, sports, or their own goals
- They identify reframing opportunities without prompting
- They begin to take their own formation more seriously (asking better questions, seeking better books, noticing their own habits)
- They start to think about leadership differently — specifically about what makes a leader worth following

### Long-term (Year+ after)

- They understand themselves as being *formed*, deliberately, and participate actively in their formation
- They hold *pothos* and its brake as simultaneously present concepts in their lives
- They begin building things that outlast moments — relationships, projects, habits
- They can identify whose formation they are in turn responsible for (younger siblings, future children, students, colleagues)
- They return to Plutarch and Arrian on their own, without being assigned

## Troubleshooting Common Challenges

### "My child thinks Alexander is just cool because he conquered the world."

This is normal, and at the 8-10 age, it is basically the right first reaction. Don't fight it. Build on it: *"Yes, he was an amazing commander. Let's look at why — what made him the one who could do it?"* Then slide from "conquering" to "observation, courage, reframing, leading from the front." The moral complications come later.

If they're older and still stuck on conquest-worship, introduce Washington deliberately. Washington *also* had enormous ambition. But he chose to step down. Is that less great, or is it more? The comparison is the cure.

### "My child is scared of the violence."

Respect this. Fear of violence is not immaturity — it is often moral perception. For 8-10, just lower the dose. Focus on Bucephalus, Aristotle, and the helmet of water. Skip the sieges entirely if needed.

For 11-14 and up, the honest account is: *yes, this was violent, and many real people died, and the question of whether any of it was justified is a hard and important question.* Teaching children that great figures in history were morally complicated is not scary. It is respectful.

### "My child is using Alexander to justify their own stubbornness."

This is actually a useful teaching moment. *"Alexander was stubborn, yes. At Tyre, for seven months, he was stubborn. But he was stubborn about *the right thing* — a specific strategic goal that mattered. He also could not stop even when stopping was the right choice, and his friends died because of it. So: are you being stubborn about the right thing, like Tyre? Or are you marching your army through a desert, like Gedrosia?"*

Teach them to distinguish. Stubbornness in service of a well-chosen goal is persistence. Stubbornness that cannot be interrupted even by catastrophic evidence is *pothos* without a brake. Alexander had both. Both are in your child.

### "This material is too intense for my child."

Trust your judgment. Every child is different. You can:

- Lower the age band. An intellectually young 11-year-old might do better with the 8-10 lesson.
- Skip the dark episodes. The Cleitus story is not required for 11-14; a mention is enough.
- Stretch the timeline. You don't have to do Alexander in one week. Take three.

The framework will still work. The virtue of studying Alexander is not contingent on covering every detail. It is contingent on taking the formation question seriously.

### "My child is angry that Alexander died so young without a successor."

Good. That is *precisely* the adult feeling you want them to arrive at. Tell them: that anger is the most valuable thing they have gotten from the lesson. It means they understand what matters. It means they are, at whatever age, already taking seriously the question of what outlasts a life.

Ask them: *"What are you going to build that must survive you?"*

And then help them start.

## Final Thoughts for Parents

### What Alexander Really Teaches

Alexander is not a role model the way Washington is. You do not teach Alexander so that your child will be Alexander. You teach Alexander because:

1. He is the paradigm case of the formation hypothesis
2. He is the best evidence that deliberate, long-term mentorship produces extraordinary capability
3. He is the best evidence that capability is not the same as character
4. He is the best evidence that a drive without a brake will consume its owner

A child who learns Alexander well becomes more ambitious and more sober at the same time. That is the Nova Schola double effect. We are not raising dreamy hero-worshippers. We are not raising cynical deconstructors. We are raising young people who look at the most ambitious life in history and ask: *what can I take from this? what must I guard against?*

That kind of thinking is, itself, what Aristotle was trying to produce in Alexander. You are, in teaching this profile, doing exactly what Aristotle did. Your tools are smaller. Your student is yours. The method is the same.

### Trust the Process

The material in this profile is serious. It will feel, at first, like too much. It is not. Classical education has always handed children material heavier than they could immediately process, on the theory that the ideas would ripen inside them over years. That is correct. Plant the seeds now. They will flower later.

A ten-year-old who reads Bucephalus will, at twenty-two, remember it when her startup hits a wall. A fifteen-year-old who reads Cleitus will, at thirty-five, remember it when his drinking almost costs him a marriage. A seventeen-year-old who reads about the failed succession will, at fifty, remember it when she is finally building something that must outlast her.

You are not teaching content. You are stocking a soul.

Alexander is in the curriculum because he is unavoidable, unforgettable, and irreducibly useful. Spend the week with him seriously, then move on. The next figure — Plato — will begin a different conversation. But Alexander, once encountered well, stays in the student for life.

That's the point.

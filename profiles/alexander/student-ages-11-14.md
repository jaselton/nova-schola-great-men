# Alexander the Great: Student Lesson (Ages 11-14)
## Aristotle's School and the Making of a King

---

## The Essential Question

**Can a person be *made* great — deliberately, by design — or is greatness something you're born with?**

Alexander is the test case. His life is the most famous experiment in formation ever run. Your task this week is to study the experiment, decide what it proves, and figure out what it means for *your* own formation.

---

## The Deeper Story

### The Macedon You Need to Picture

When Alexander was born in 356 BCE, Macedon was *not* the great power everyone thinks of. It was a half-Greek backwater on the northern edge of the Greek world. Athenians called Macedonians "barbarians." Their kings were considered semi-legitimate. Macedonian court life was violent, polygamous, and short on stable succession.

Philip II, Alexander's father, changed all this in about twenty years. He reformed the army (inventing the Macedonian phalanx with its longer spear, the *sarissa*), he married strategically (Olympias was one of at least seven wives), and he fought constantly. He lost an eye. He broke his leg. He became the most feared commander in Greece.

So Alexander grew up in a household that was already training him — without anyone calling it that — for exactly one role: *to succeed Philip.* The question was whether he could.

### The Mother Who Built the Myth

Olympias, Alexander's mother, was a princess from Epirus. She was also a religious ecstatic — a devotee of Dionysus, the god of wine and divine madness — and ancient sources describe her sleeping with serpents as part of her rites. More usefully for Alexander's formation, she was a brilliant political operator and a genius at building identity.

From the time Alexander could understand language, she told him he was descended from **Achilles** (the greatest warrior of the *Iliad*) on her side and **Heracles** (the greatest hero of myth) on Philip's side. She hinted, and then said openly, that Zeus himself might be his real father.

Was this literal? Probably not. Was it *effective*? Enormously.

A child who grows up being told "you are the heir of Achilles" behaves differently than a child told "do your best." Olympias did not raise Alexander to hope. She raised him to *assume*. The belief was the scaffolding on which the rest of the formation was built.

### Two Teachers, Two Purposes

Before Aristotle, Alexander had two tutors, and they did very different work.

**Leonidas** was a relative of Olympias and a severe disciplinarian. He gave Alexander:
- Cold mornings, long walks, plain food
- Inspection of his baggage to remove any luxuries Olympias smuggled in
- Homer by memorization
- The *habitus* — the physical habit — of Spartan toughness

Leonidas was answering the question: *Will this boy be soft?* The answer had to be no.

**Lysimachus** was the opposite — a gentler figure who worked through imagination and story. He called Alexander "Achilles," himself "Phoenix" (Achilles's old tutor), and Philip "Peleus" (Achilles's father). He ran his lessons as a kind of long-form role-play.

This is pedagogically profound. Alexander was not being taught *about* heroism in the abstract. He was being cast *as* a hero, daily, by an adult who took it seriously. Modern psychology has a name for this: **identity priming**. If you tell a child often enough who he is, he will eventually become that person.

Put them together: Leonidas made the body tough, Lysimachus made the soul ambitious. Both were deliberate. Neither was accidental.

### Bucephalus: The Moment the Formation Showed

At about twelve, Alexander had his famous encounter with the horse Bucephalus.

A horse-dealer brought a magnificent but unmanageable stallion and asked a staggering price. Philip's grooms couldn't mount him. Philip was about to send the horse away.

Alexander said the grown men were losing a wonderful horse through *lack of skill and boldness*. Philip challenged him: if you fail, what will you pay? "The price of the horse," said Alexander.

Here is what actually happened, pedagogically:

1. **Alexander had been watching.** He noticed that Bucephalus shied every time he looked down. The horse was afraid of his own shadow.
2. **Alexander had internalized the Achilles identity.** A normal twelve-year-old does not stake his father's money on his own perception against adult horsemen. Alexander did, because his formation had made him believe his perception was reliable.
3. **He acted.** He turned the horse to the sun, eliminating the shadow, and mounted.

Philip's reaction is the key moment: he wept, and said, "My son, seek a kingdom worthy of yourself. Macedon is too small for you."

From that day, Philip's formation project was running *openly*. Everyone at court now knew what was being built. The boy knew too.

### Aristotle at Mieza (343–340 BCE, Alexander ages 13–16)

When Alexander turned thirteen, Philip hired the most expensive tutor in history. Aristotle was forty-one, already considered the greatest philosopher then living, and a former student of Plato himself. The fee Aristotle negotiated tells you everything: Philip had destroyed Aristotle's hometown of Stagira years earlier. Aristotle asked that the town be rebuilt and its former inhabitants freed from slavery. Philip agreed.

Think about that negotiation. Aristotle did not ask for silver. He asked for *polis* — for a restored city. The philosopher priced his services in the currency philosophers actually care about.

Philip built Aristotle a school at the **Nymphaeum of Mieza**, a rural sanctuary about thirty miles from the palace. Alexander was joined by a carefully chosen cohort of noble youths:

- **Hephaestion** — who would become his lifelong closest friend
- **Ptolemy** — who would later rule Egypt and found the dynasty that ended with Cleopatra
- **Cassander** — who would later become king of Macedon
- **Craterus, Nearchus, Leonnatus** — who would become his greatest generals

This detail matters. Aristotle was not tutoring a boy. He was forming a **generation of rulers** together. The school at Mieza produced, in three years, almost every major figure of the next century of world history.

The curriculum covered:

- **Homer** — annotated personally by Aristotle; Alexander carried this copy into Asia
- **Ethics** — what we now call the *Nicomachean Ethics*, the doctrine of virtue as habituated excellence
- **Politics** — what a king is, what he owes his people, what the *polis* is for
- **Rhetoric** — the art of persuasion among free men
- **Natural philosophy** — biology, botany, medicine, zoology
- **Geography** — what was known of the Persian Empire and beyond
- **The tragedians** — Sophocles, Aeschylus, Euripides

Notice what is *not* on this list. Aristotle did not teach Alexander how to fight. That came from Philip and from campaign experience. What Aristotle provided was the **interior architecture** — the philosophical framework through which Alexander would later interpret his own actions.

When Alexander later said *"I owe my father my life; I owe Aristotle the life worth living,"* he was naming this distinction exactly. Philip gave him the body and the army. Aristotle gave him the inside of the skull.

### The Test: Chaeronea, Age 18

The school at Mieza ended when Alexander was sixteen, because Philip needed him. At eighteen, he was given command of the left wing of Philip's army at the **Battle of Chaeronea** — the battle that would decide whether Athens and Thebes remained free or came under Macedonian hegemony.

Opposite Alexander stood the **Theban Sacred Band** — three hundred elite warriors, paired as lovers, undefeated for forty years. They were the single most feared unit in the Greek world.

Alexander charged them with the Companion Cavalry. He broke them. When the dust cleared, the Sacred Band was dead to the last man — in formation, where they had fallen. Archaeology has since confirmed this: the mass grave at Chaeronea held 254 skeletons in rows.

The formation was working. The eighteen-year-old had done what adult commanders had spent their careers *avoiding*.

### The Gordian Knot: The Signature Move

Three years later, as king, Alexander crossed into Asia. At the city of Gordium in Phrygia, he was shown the famous Gordian Knot — an intricate knot of cord binding the yoke to an ancient wagon. An oracle had promised that whoever "loosed" the knot would rule Asia.

Alexander tried to untie it. He couldn't. The knot had defeated generations of claimants.

Then he drew his sword and cut it.

This story is often told as if it were impatience or showing off. It is neither. It is **the signature Alexandrian move**: when a problem as defined has defeated everyone before you, *redefine the problem*.

The oracle had promised the kingdom to whoever *loosed* the knot. The oracle had not specified the method. Alexander took the goal as binding and the means as negotiable.

This same move shows up across his career:
- **Bucephalus:** the horse is unmanageable. Reframe: the horse is spooked by shadow. Turn to the sun.
- **Tyre:** the city is impregnable as an island. Reframe: make it a peninsula. Build a road through the sea.
- **The Sogdian Rock:** the fortress is uncapturable. Reframe: offer extraordinary reward. Get 300 volunteers to climb at night.
- **The Hydaspes:** Porus has blocked the direct crossing. Reframe: cross miles upstream at night, in a storm, and surprise him.

Students should *learn this move*. When everyone is stuck, the question is rarely *how can I solve this problem?* It is usually *why has no one questioned the frame in which this problem is stated?*

### Tyre: The Seven-Month Lesson in Persistence

After Issus, on his way to Egypt, Alexander needed Tyre to surrender so that the Persian navy would have no Phoenician base in his rear. Tyre refused. Tyre was an island city half a mile offshore, walled on all sides, supplied by sea. No one had taken it in centuries.

Alexander besieged it for **seven months**.

He ordered a mole built through the ocean — a causeway of stone and timber and rubble. The Tyrians sent fire-ships that burned the first mole. He built it wider. They threw rocks from the walls. He built siege towers on the mole. A storm destroyed much of it. He rebuilt. When Phoenician cities further south defected to him, he finally had a fleet and could blockade the harbor.

When Tyre fell, Alexander was merciless. Most of the population was enslaved or crucified. The message to the Persian world was permanent: *do not waste my time*.

The lesson is harder than it looks. *Tyre is impregnable* is a *statement about current technology*, not a statement about reality. The moment someone is willing to spend seven months building a road through the sea, impregnable is no longer true.

Most people give up because the *standard* version of the problem is unsolved. Alexander's life says: if the standard version is unsolved, find the *non-standard* version. Build what has never been built.

### The Crack: The Murder of Cleitus (328 BCE)

By twenty-eight, Alexander had done what no one had ever done: destroyed the Persian Empire, taken Egypt, captured Babylon, Susa, and Persepolis, and was now the Great King of Persia himself. He had also started adopting Persian customs — the diadem, the royal robe, requests that his court perform *proskynesis* (prostration) before him.

The Macedonians, who had made him king and bled for him for a decade, hated this. To a Greek, prostration was an act of worship reserved for gods.

At a drunken banquet at Samarkand, one of his oldest friends — **Cleitus the Black**, who had personally saved Alexander's life at the Battle of the Granicus — began mocking Alexander. He said Philip had won Alexander's victories for him. He sneered at the divine-descent claims. He said Alexander was becoming a Persian tyrant.

Alexander, drunk, reached for a weapon. His friends held him back. Cleitus was hustled out of the room. Then Cleitus — also drunk — came back in, still shouting mockery.

Alexander grabbed a spear from a guard and drove it through his friend's chest.

He was inconsolable. He locked himself in his tent for three days, refused food, had to be physically prevented from suicide. He had murdered one of his best friends, at his own table, over drunken insults.

**Here is what this moment means.**

Aristotle's entire ethical system rested on the idea that virtue is habituated — that you become good by practicing good actions until they are automatic. The man who has practiced self-control, Aristotle taught, will have self-control available to him when it is needed.

The most famous student of Aristotle in history, at twenty-eight, killed his best friend in a drunken rage at a feast.

That is not a small failure. That is the formation itself cracking.

Two things can both be true:

1. Aristotle's pedagogy *worked spectacularly* in cultivating Alexander's capabilities — his rhetoric, his curiosity, his magnanimity to Darius's family and Porus, his founding of cities.
2. Aristotle's pedagogy *did not eliminate* the underlying nature — the wrath, the drunkenness, the hybris.

This is the most important thing you will learn from Alexander. Formation is real. Formation also has limits. You can be *made capable* of greatness and *still need* lifelong discipline to prevent what formation cannot prevent.

## The Lesson

### 1. Formation Is Real — Take Yours Seriously
Alexander was not an accident. He was *built* by his parents, his teachers, and his environment — deliberately, over twenty years. You are being built too. The question is not *whether* but *how*, and *by whom*. Pick your teachers carefully. Pick the books you sleep with.

### 2. Identity Precedes Capability
Lysimachus did not teach Alexander to *become like* Achilles. He taught Alexander that he *was* Achilles. The identity came first. The capability followed. What you believe you are does a lot of work before your skills get tested. Choose the identity carefully.

### 3. The Gordian Move — Reframe When the Frame Has Defeated Everyone
Most problems in adult life are *not* solved by working harder inside a frame where the problem is unsolvable. They are solved by noticing that the frame was never sacred. Alexander did this at Bucephalus, at the Knot, at Tyre, at the Sogdian Rock. Every one of his signature moves was a reframing. Practice this move deliberately.

### 4. Lead From the Front — Or Don't Lead
Alexander fought in the front rank in every major battle. He was wounded repeatedly. He poured his water into the sand rather than drink while his men were thirsty. This is not sentiment. It is the *physics* of command. Men will die for leaders who share the burden. They will not die — or at least not willingly — for leaders who do not.

### 5. The Thing That Made Him Great Also Destroyed Him
*Pothos* — longing, drive, the refusal of limits — drove Alexander to every founding and every triumph. It also drove him to kill Cleitus, to march the Gedrosian Desert, to die at thirty-two with no heir. The Nova Schola warning: cultivate your drive, but do not let it become the only thing you respect. A drive that cannot be *stopped* cannot be *led*. It leads you.

## Practices for Students (Ages 11-14)

### 1. The Gordian Exercise

Pick a problem in your life that feels stuck — schoolwork, a sport skill, a friendship conflict, a habit you want to change.

Write one sentence naming **how the problem is currently framed**:
> *"The problem is: ______."*

Now, for one week, carry a notebook. Every day, write down *one alternative framing*. Example:
- Original: "I'm bad at math."
- Day 1: "I don't practice math enough."
- Day 2: "I try to do math in the wrong order — I should start with vocabulary, not problems."
- Day 3: "I learn math better out loud than silently."
- Day 4: "I'm not bad at math, I'm bad at *timed* math."
- …

At the end of the week, pick the most useful reframing and try it for two weeks.

**Goal:** train the Gordian move — the habit of suspecting the frame before accepting the verdict.

---

### 2. The Mieza Cohort

Alexander did not learn alone at Mieza. He learned with Hephaestion, Ptolemy, Craterus, and the other future generals. They became friends who would follow him to the end of the world.

Who is in your Mieza cohort?

**Make a list of 3-5 people** who are:
- Roughly your age
- Serious about growing into something excellent
- People you can learn alongside (not just hang out with)

Pick **one book** that you will all read and discuss over the next four weeks. Meet once a week — in person, on a call, whatever works — to talk about it.

If you can't find a cohort yet, that is itself the problem to solve. Ask a parent to help identify candidates.

**The Nova Schola principle:** formation rarely happens alone. Alexander had Aristotle *and* a cohort. You need both too.

---

### 3. Copywork: Aristotle's Ethics for Children

Each day this week, copy one of these passages by hand into a notebook, slowly.

**Monday:**
> *"We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit."*  
> — (Paraphrase of Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics* II)

**Tuesday:**
> *"At his best, man is the noblest of all animals; separated from law and justice, he is the worst."*  
> — Aristotle, *Politics* 1.2

**Wednesday:**
> *"It is the mark of an educated mind to be able to entertain a thought without accepting it."*  
> — Attributed to Aristotle

**Thursday:**
> *"There is nothing impossible to him who will try."*  
> — Attributed to Alexander

**Friday:**
> *"I will not steal a victory."*  
> — Alexander to Parmenion, refusing a night attack before Gaugamela

Each day, after copying, answer in two sentences:
1. What does this mean in my own words?
2. One place in my life this week where this applies.

---

### 4. The Leader's Front Line

For this entire week, practice the Alexander-at-the-helmet-of-water principle.

Every day, find **one task in your household or school life** that:
- Nobody wants to do
- Is visible to others
- Would normally be someone else's job to chase you on

Do it first. Without being asked. Without mentioning it afterward.

Keep a short journal entry each night:
- What did I do?
- Who noticed?
- How did it change how others behaved toward me?

**What you'll learn:** *Moral authority* — the kind that makes people actually follow you — is earned by the leader who does the unglamorous work first.

---

### 5. Map the Campaign

Get a blank map of the Mediterranean and the Near East (plenty of printable ones online). Over two weeks, mark:

- **Pella** (starting point)
- **Corinth** (where Greek league gave him command)
- **Troy** (where he visited Achilles's tomb)
- **Granicus River** (first battle against Persians, 334 BCE)
- **Gordium** (the Knot, 333 BCE)
- **Issus** (battle against Darius, 333 BCE)
- **Tyre** (seven-month siege, 332 BCE)
- **Alexandria / Siwa** (Egypt, 331 BCE)
- **Gaugamela** (decisive battle, 331 BCE)
- **Babylon, Susa, Persepolis**
- **Samarkand** (Cleitus episode)
- **Hydaspes River** (battle against Porus, 326 BCE)
- **Hyphasis River** (where the army refused, 326 BCE)
- **Gedrosian Desert** (catastrophic march, 325 BCE)
- **Babylon** (death, 323 BCE)

**Now answer in writing (1 page):**
1. How far was this in miles? (Measure roughly.)
2. How long did it take? (From 334 to 323 = 11 years of campaigning.)
3. What is the farthest point any modern person you know has traveled on foot? How does it compare?

## Discussion Questions (For Parents & Students)

### On Formation
1. Alexander was deliberately formed by his parents, his tutors, and his environment over twenty years. Are *you* being formed deliberately, or by default? What would a deliberate formation look like for you?
2. Olympias told Alexander he was descended from Achilles. Do you think that kind of identity-building is good parenting, bad parenting, or something else?
3. If you had one Aristotle — one extraordinary tutor for one subject — who would it be, and what would you ask them to teach?

### On Method
4. The Gordian Knot: cheating? cleverness? rightful reframing? Where's the line?
5. At the Hyphasis River, Alexander's army refused to go further. Was that disobedience or wisdom? Should a leader override his followers' judgment, or listen to it?
6. Alexander never lost a battle. But he also never planned for a world after him. Which is the bigger accomplishment — winning, or building something that outlasts you?

### On Character
7. Why was Alexander so kind to Darius's family and to Porus, but so cruel to Thebes and to the Tyrians?
8. Aristotle taught that virtue is habituated — that practicing self-control makes self-control automatic. Alexander, at twenty-eight, killed his best friend in a drunken rage. What does this tell us about the limits of education?
9. Nova Schola's thesis is that every child can be formed toward greatness. Alexander is the proof. What would the *warning* version of his life teach a future Nova Schola student?

## Parent Teaching Notes

### What to Emphasize
- **Formation as deliberate project.** The whole lesson hinges on this. Alexander was *built*. Your child can be built too. This is not mystical — it is a sequence of choices adults made.
- **The cohort mattered.** Mieza was not a one-to-one tutorial — it was a cohort of future rulers forming each other. Help your child identify or build theirs.
- **Reframing as a learnable move.** The Gordian/Bucephalus/Tyre pattern is a *skill*, not a personality trait. Name it, practice it, reward it.

### What to Complicate
- **Divine-descent claims.** Useful to discuss whether it's good or bad for a parent to tell a child they're "descended from heroes." Different families will land in different places. Honor the disagreement.
- **The violence.** Alexander killed a lot of people. Be honest about this. Glorify the virtues; do not romanticize the conquests.
- **The Cleitus episode.** This age is *ready* for the hard lesson that education has limits. Don't skip it.

### Possible Challenges
- *"But Alexander was a prince — I can't do what he did."* Correct this directly. The formation was not about being royal. It was about adults taking the formation seriously. You can do that.
- *"So conquering is great?"* Redirect: conquering is the *occasion* where we see formation tested. The virtue under the conquering — courage, magnanimity, reframing — is what we're imitating, not the conquering itself.
- *"But he died at 32."* Yes. That's the serious final question. Is a short great life better than a long ordinary one? There's no one right answer; the point is to take the question seriously.

### Extension Activities
- Read aloud one short passage from Plutarch's *Life of Alexander* each evening for a week
- Watch a good documentary on Aristotle (e.g., Oliver Jens Schmitt's scholarship, or a BBC production — previewing first)
- Find a passage of Homer's *Iliad* (the duel of Achilles and Hector, Book 22) and read it together — this is literally the story Alexander carried in his bag
- Take a long hike — ideally one that exceeds your child's current comfort zone — and make it explicit: "This is a Bucephalus exercise. We will find we can do more than we think."

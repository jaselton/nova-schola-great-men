# Socrates: Student Lesson (Ages 11-14)
## The Man Who Made Athens Uncomfortable Enough to Kill Him

---

## Background: The World Socrates Walked Into

Before you understand Socrates, you need to understand Athens. And before you understand Athens, you need to understand what Athens thought was most valuable.

Athens in 470 BCE was the most powerful democratic city in the Greek world, flush with the pride of having defeated the Persian Empire — an empire fifty times its size — barely a decade before Socrates was born. The city that built the Parthenon, that produced Aeschylus and Sophocles, that operated the most radical experiment in self-government the ancient world had seen, was also a city obsessed with **speech**.

In a democracy, power flows to the person who can persuade a crowd. The assembly of Athenian citizens made all major decisions by vote — on war, on taxes, on exile, on law. The ability to stand up before thousands of citizens and move them with words was the most valuable skill a person could have, and wealthy Athenian families paid enormously to develop it in their sons.

This is why there was a class of professional teachers called the **Sophists** — traveling educators who charged high fees to teach rhetoric: how to make arguments, how to win debates, how to persuade audiences of almost anything. Some Sophists went further: they taught that truth was relative, that justice was just whatever the powerful declared, that the goal of argument was not to discover truth but to *win*. The motto of the more cynical among them: *man is the measure of all things* — there is no objective standard; only what seems true to you is true.

Into this world walked Socrates: barefoot, ugly, charging nothing, claiming to know nothing, asking questions that made everyone uncomfortable.

He was not a Sophist. He was the Sophist's opposite. And Athens couldn't quite figure out what to do with him, until it decided to kill him.

## The Life

### Soldier

Socrates served as a hoplite — heavy infantry — in three Peloponnesian War campaigns: Potidaea (432–430 BCE), Delium (424 BCE), and Amphipolis (422 BCE). He was not a young man when he served; he was in his late thirties and forties. He served alongside Alcibiades, a brilliant and beautiful teenager who would become one of his most famous, most troubled students.

At Potidaea, Socrates stood motionless for twenty-four hours working through a philosophical problem — soldiers gathered to watch, eventually sleeping on the ground around him. He also saved Alcibiades's life when Alcibiades was wounded.

At Delium, the Athenians were routed. Alcibiades, now a cavalry officer, watched Socrates retreat. He later described it in Plato's *Symposium*: Socrates walked off the field slowly, calmly, glancing around with complete composure, making it clear to anyone watching that attacking him would be more trouble than it was worth. He was not afraid. He was completely present.

This physical courage matters. The equanimity Socrates showed at his trial — when he refused to beg for his life — was not a performance. It was the same equanimity he had shown in actual battle twenty-five years earlier. Character, when genuinely formed, does not behave differently under different pressures.

### The Oracle and the Mission

The turning point in Socrates's life was a visit his friend Chaerephon made to the Oracle at Delphi. The Oracle — believed to be channeling the god Apollo — declared that no one in Athens was wiser than Socrates.

Socrates found this impossible. He knew perfectly well that he wasn't wise — he was constantly confused about exactly the things he thought most important. So he did something unusual: he treated the Oracle's declaration as a philosophical problem to be investigated.

He went to the people in Athens who were considered wise — politicians, poets, craftsmen — and tested them. He asked each of them probing questions about the subjects in which they claimed expertise. The results were consistent: in every case, the confident knowledge turned out to be superficial. The politicians couldn't define the concepts they governed by. The poets couldn't explain their own work. The craftsmen, who genuinely knew their trades, assumed that skill at pottery meant understanding justice.

His conclusion: the Oracle was right, but for a reason no one had anticipated. Socrates was wiser than everyone else **not because he knew more, but because he knew what he didn't know**. His ignorance was *conscious* and *honest*. Everyone else's ignorance was invisible to them — which made it far more dangerous.

He interpreted this as a divine commission. His job was to help Athens see itself clearly — to examine its assumptions, expose its confident ignorance, and thereby improve it. He did this for the rest of his life, at no charge, in any public space that would have him.

### The Method: Elenchus

The Greek word *elenchus* (ἔλεγχος) means "cross-examination" or "refutation." Socrates's conversations almost always followed a recognizable structure:

1. **Opening claim.** Someone makes a confident assertion: "Courage is standing your ground in battle."
2. **Acceptance and questioning.** Socrates accepts this provisionally and asks an apparently simple follow-up: "What about the wise retreat? Is that cowardly?"
3. **Complication.** The question reveals the original definition was too narrow (or too broad, or self-contradictory).
4. **Revised definition.** The person offers a new one.
5. **Further complication.** Socrates finds the problem with the new definition.
6. **Aporia.** The person arrives at *aporia* — a Greek word meaning "no way through" — the honest admission that they don't know what they thought they knew.

The endpoint of a Socratic conversation is almost never a firm answer. It is almost always a more honest question. This frustrated people. But Socrates believed — and this is central to his whole philosophy — that arriving at genuine uncertainty is *more valuable* than confirming false certainty. The person who knows they're lost can start looking for the way out. The person who thinks they know the way, but is wrong, will keep walking confidently in the wrong direction.

The method made him enormously popular with young men, who loved watching pompous authority figures get intellectually dismantled. It made him deeply unpopular with the authority figures themselves. And it planted a seed that would eventually grow into his trial.

## The Philosophy

### Virtue Is Knowledge

Socrates held a philosophical position that seems strange at first: **virtue is a form of knowledge, and no one does wrong willingly**. If you really, truly understand what is good, you will do it. If you do something harmful or unjust, it is because you are confused about what is actually good — you are pursuing something that seems good but isn't.

This is called the *Socratic paradox*. Aristotle later criticized it on the grounds that it denies weakness of will (*akrasia* in Greek — knowing the right thing but doing the wrong thing). Most people's experience suggests that weakness of will is real and common.

But look more carefully at what Socrates is claiming. He is not saying people never make mistakes. He is saying that at the root of every moral mistake is a cognitive error — a wrong belief about what is actually valuable. The person who lies to gain money genuinely believes money is more valuable than honesty; if they truly understood what honesty was worth, they wouldn't trade it. The person who acts cruelly for approval genuinely believes approval is worth the cruelty; if they genuinely understood the cost, they would recalculate.

This has a powerful implication: the most important kind of education is not training in skills, but training in *understanding what things are actually worth*. If you can get a person to genuinely understand the value of justice, courage, and wisdom — not just memorize the words, but understand what those things *are* — they will act justly, courageously, and wisely. The problem is that genuine understanding is much harder to produce than it looks.

### The Examined Life

"The unexamined life is not worth living" — *Apology* 38a.

This is the most famous thing Socrates ever said, and it was said in his own defense speech at his trial, as the reason he cannot stop doing philosophy even under threat of death. He means exactly what he says.

A life in which you have never asked: *Why do I believe what I believe? What is this thing I am pursuing actually worth? Am I living in accordance with what I genuinely think is good?* — that life may be comfortable, but it is not a fully human life. The capacity for self-examination is part of what makes us distinctly human. Declining to use it is a kind of self-betrayal.

This is not an invitation to crippling self-doubt. Socrates was not paralyzed by his questioning; he acted decisively throughout his life. The examined life is not the *doubted* life; it is the *reflected* life — one in which you periodically stop and check your beliefs against reality rather than just assuming they are correct.

### The Daemon

Socrates claimed to have an inner divine sign — a *daimonion*, or daemon — that he had heard from childhood. It never told him what to do; it only gave a kind of veto, a signal when he was about to do something he shouldn't. On the morning of his trial, the daemon was silent. Socrates interpreted this as meaning his death was not an evil — and proceeded accordingly.

The daemon raises an interesting question about the relationship between reason and conscience. Socrates was the most rational man in Athens; he built his whole practice on argument and evidence. And yet he took seriously a non-rational interior signal that he could not fully explain. He seems to have thought that reason and something like conscience were not in conflict but complementary — that the daemon and the *elenchus* (cross-examination) served the same purpose of keeping him oriented toward what is genuinely good.

## The Trial, 399 BCE

### The Political Context

The charges against Socrates cannot be understood without the immediately preceding history. In 415 BCE, Athens sent a huge military expedition to conquer Sicily, championed enthusiastically by **Alcibiades** — Socrates's most famous student. The expedition was a catastrophe; it essentially ended Athenian military dominance. Alcibiades, facing prosecution for a separate religious offense, defected to Sparta, then helped Sparta strategically, then defected to Persia. Athens surrendered to Sparta in 404 BCE.

Under the terms of the Spartan peace, Athens was governed for eight months by the Thirty Tyrants — a brutal oligarchic committee that executed or exiled hundreds of leading democrats and stole property on a large scale. The most violent member of the Thirty was **Critias** — who had, in his youth, been part of Socrates's circle.

Democracy was restored in 403 BCE. A general amnesty was declared for actions taken under the Thirty. The city was trying to heal. But the wounds were fresh, and two of the men most associated with Athens's catastrophic decade — Alcibiades and Critias — were associated, in the minds of many Athenians, with Socrates.

The charges: impiety (not honoring the city's gods; introducing new divine beings — the daemon) and corrupting the youth. The second charge almost certainly meant, in the minds of the jury: he produced men like Alcibiades and Critias.

### The Defense

In Plato's *Apology*, Socrates gives a defense that is remarkable primarily for what it refuses to do. He will not grovel. He will not propose exile (which would have been accepted and would have saved his life). He will not stop doing philosophy. He treats the trial as an opportunity to explain, one more time, what he has been doing for forty years — and why Athens needs it, even if Athens doesn't want it.

He tells the Oracle story. He argues that his philosophical mission is a divine service to the city. He argues that the men who think they know what they don't know are the actual danger to Athens, not the man who has spent his life exposing that ignorance. He tells the jury that even if they spare him on the condition that he stop philosophizing, he will not — because to stop would be to disobey Apollo, and he cannot disobey Apollo.

He acknowledges that this defense will not be what the jury wants to hear. He gives it anyway.

The jury votes 280–220 to convict. When Socrates is asked to propose a penalty, he initially suggests the city honor him with free meals for life. Under pressure from friends, he proposes a substantial fine. The jury, more angered than before, votes for death by a larger margin than the conviction.

### The Refusal to Escape

Socrates's friend Crito visits him in prison with a fully arranged escape plan. In Plato's dialogue *Crito*, Socrates dismantles it, argument by argument.

His core argument: he has lived his whole life under the laws of Athens, benefiting from them, implicitly agreeing to abide by them. Even if those laws are being applied unjustly in his particular case, it would be wrong for him to simply ignore them when they become inconvenient. If he flees, he teaches that laws should be obeyed when convenient and broken when not — which destroys the whole basis of civic life.

He also makes a more direct argument: doing wrong in return for wrong is never justified. It would be wrong for him to flee. Crito's love for him is not a good reason for him to do something wrong. The soul's integrity must be maintained even in extremity — *especially* in extremity.

The death scene in Plato's *Phaedo* — written as if observed directly — is one of the most famous passages in ancient literature. Socrates spends his last hours arguing that the soul is immortal and that death is therefore not an evil for the philosopher. His friends weep; he keeps talking; he drinks the hemlock without drama and dies as he lived: in the middle of a philosophical argument, at peace.

## The Students He Made, and Didn't

### Plato

Plato was about twenty to twenty-nine years old during his time with Socrates. The execution of Socrates at age seventy-one was the formative catastrophe of Plato's life. He left Athens for a decade. When he returned, he founded the Academy — the first institution of higher learning in the Western tradition — and spent fifty years writing down his version of the conversations his teacher had with everyone in Athens.

Plato's portrait of Socrates is the primary source we have. It is also inevitably filtered through Plato's own philosophical development. The early dialogues (*Apology*, *Crito*, *Euthyphro*, *Meno*) are believed to be closest to the historical Socrates; the later dialogues use "Socrates" as a mouthpiece for Plato's own mature views. The boundary is uncertain and contested.

What is certain: Socrates made Plato. Plato made the Academy. The Academy made Aristotle. The chain from Socrates to the scientific revolution runs through that sequence.

### Alcibiades

The most philosophically interesting failure. Alcibiades understood Socrates. In Plato's *Symposium*, he gives the most vivid portrait of what it was like to be in Socrates's company — and the most honest admission of what he did with it. He says Socrates is the only person who has ever made him ashamed of himself, who has shown him the distance between who he is and who he could be. He says he has repeatedly tried to run away from this knowledge, because facing it requires changing, and changing requires giving up the applause and admiration he craves more than he craves goodness.

That is the Alcibiades problem stated precisely: he had the knowledge, he lacked the will. Whether this is a failure of Socratic method — which cannot instill the will — or simply the permanent human limitation that even the best teacher cannot substitute for the student's own moral choice, is a question worth sitting with.

### Critias

Less sympathetic, more troubling. Critias was an aristocrat, an intellectual, a poet, and, during the Thirty Tyrants, a mass murderer. He was associated with Socrates in his youth, which is part of why the charge of "corrupting the youth" had traction at the trial. Nothing in what we know of Socrates explains Critias; the most honest conclusion is that Critias used philosophical sophistication in the service of brutal ambition, which is not what philosophy is for, and that exposure to Socratic questioning did not, in his case, produce the honesty about the self that it was supposed to produce. It produced, instead, a more articulate and self-justifying wickedness.

The lesson: method cannot guarantee outcome. The elenchus is a tool for finding truth. If the student is not actually committed to finding truth — if they are only committed to winning — the tool can be turned to other purposes.

## The Relevance

### Socrates and the Question Behind This Curriculum

You are reading a series of profiles of great men. Each profile argues, implicitly, that these men's lives have something to teach you — about virtue, about formation, about how to develop as a person.

Socrates would ask: *How do you know that?*

This is not a hostile question. It is the question he would ask about everything. How do you know these men are great? What do you mean by "great"? How do you know what they did is worth imitating? What exactly is the connection between studying their lives and becoming a better person yourself?

These are real questions. They deserve real answers. And the fact that this curriculum invites you to ask them — rather than just accept them — is itself Socratic. The profiles are meant to be examined, not just absorbed.

### The Practical Skill

The specific habit Socrates teaches is this: before acting on a belief, ask *How do I know this is true?*

Not once, and not performatively. As a genuine practice: before you repeat something you've heard, ask if you actually know it. Before you act on a principle, ask if you've ever really examined that principle. Before you dismiss a person or idea, ask whether you've given it honest scrutiny or only the appearance of scrutiny.

This habit is hard to maintain consistently. But even practicing it occasionally — even three or four times a week — will change the quality of your thinking. It will also, as Athens discovered, make you less manipulable. People who have learned to ask "How do I know this?" are much harder to lead by emotion, fear, or social pressure than people who haven't.

## Things to Do This Week

**1. The Socratic conversation.** Choose a belief you hold confidently — something you're certain is true. Spend ten minutes trying to find a case where it might be false, or a definition that doesn't quite work. Write down what you find. If you find nothing — if your belief survives all the questions — that's actually interesting too. Write that down.

**2. Read the *Apology*.** Read Plato's *Apology* (Socrates's defense speech) in full. It is short — about 25 pages. It is one of the most important documents in Western history. As you read, notice: what is Socrates afraid of? (Nothing.) What is he refusing? (Compromise.) What is he asking Athens to do? (Think.) Does he seem angry? (No.) Does he seem resigned? (No.) What word describes how he seems?

**3. The "confident ignorance" exercise.** This week, when you meet confident statements — in conversation, online, in any medium — practice the mental question: *Does this person actually know this, or do they just feel certain about it?* How can you tell the difference? Write down two or three cases.

**4. The Alcibiades inventory.** Write down one thing you know you should do but keep not doing. Don't rationalize it. Just name it and the reason you're not doing it. This is the kind of honest self-examination Socrates's method is supposed to produce. You don't have to solve it; you have to see it.

**5. Read *Crito*.** Read Plato's *Crito* — the brief dialogue in which Socrates refuses to escape. It is about 12 pages. The argument Socrates makes about law and civic obligation is still relevant. Do you find it convincing? What is the strongest argument against it?

## Essay Questions for Discussion or Writing

- Socrates argued that no one does wrong willingly — that all wrongdoing is really a kind of ignorance. Is this true? Can you think of cases where someone knew what was right and chose wrong anyway?
- At his trial, Socrates refused to propose exile even though it would have saved his life. Was this brave, or was it foolish, or was it something else?
- Socrates spent forty years talking to the people of Athens about justice, virtue, and wisdom — and Athens killed him for it. Was Socrates too confrontational? Should he have found a less provocative way to do his work? Or does the confrontation not matter because the work was right?
- Alcibiades understood Socrates's teaching and could not act on it. Critias understood it and turned it toward cruelty. What does this tell us about the limits of philosophical education? What can philosophy do, and what can it not do?

## One Last Thing

There's a line in Plato's *Symposium* where Alcibiades says something remarkable about what it was like to be around Socrates:

> *"When I listen to him my heart beats faster than if I were in a religious frenzy, and tears run down my face, and I observe that numbers of others have the same experience."*

This is about a bald, ugly, barefoot old man who never wrote anything, charged nothing, and spent his days asking questions in a market square.

He is describing the experience of being genuinely seen — of having someone expose, by pure questioning, how much more you could be than you are, and how much distance there is between your current self and the self you could become.

That is what good teaching feels like. Not the teacher filling you up with information. The teacher helping you see what is actually there inside you — and what is blocking it.

Socrates never taught anyone anything, in the conventional sense. He helped people see what they already, in some sense, knew.

That, it turns out, is the most important kind of teaching there is.

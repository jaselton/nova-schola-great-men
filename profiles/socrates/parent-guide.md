# Socrates: Parent Teaching Guide

---

## Overview: What This Profile Teaches

Socrates is the figure in the Nova Schola sequence who provides the most important internal check on the sequence itself.

You are presenting your child with a curriculum of great men and their achievements — their disciplines, their habits, their formation practices. The risk of any such curriculum is that it becomes a kind of heroic consumption: the child absorbs a sequence of impressive figures, learns the facts about them, imitates their surface practices, and never does the deeper work of asking whether any of it is actually true or actually worth doing.

Socrates is the figure who prevents this. His entire contribution is the insistence that received beliefs — including beliefs about what is worth admiring and imitating — must be genuinely examined before they can be genuinely owned. The child who has absorbed Socrates will not merely accumulate the Great Men sequence; they will interrogate it. They will ask what it means for something to be "great," whether the greatness on display is genuine, what the costs were, and what they themselves actually believe after thinking it through.

This is not destructive. It is the most rigorous kind of formation. Beliefs that survive questioning are more durable than beliefs that were only received. Practices that were consciously chosen are more sustaining than practices that were only imitated. Socrates, properly taught, does not undermine the curriculum — he is the quality-control mechanism that makes the curriculum worth running.

This profile also teaches:

1. **Questioning as a virtue.** Not doubt as a posture, but genuine inquiry — the habit of asking "How do I know this is true?" before acting on a belief. This is a specific, practicable skill that can be developed at any age.

2. **Conscious ignorance as wisdom.** The child who says "I don't know, and I know I don't know" is in a better epistemic position than the child who says "I know" when they don't. Cultivating honest uncertainty about hard questions is harder and more valuable than it sounds.

3. **The gap between knowing and doing.** Alcibiades understood Socrates's teaching completely and could not act on it. This is the permanent cautionary note in any formation project: cognitive understanding of the good is necessary but not sufficient for virtue. The will and the emotions must also be trained. This connects forward to Aristotle's habituation theory and backward to everything the previous profiles have said about practice.

4. **Death as the ultimate consistency test.** Socrates at seventy-one is the same person as Socrates at Potidaea and Delium. His character was formed early and remained consistent under the highest possible pressure. This is the formation ideal — not the performance of virtue when it is comfortable, but the settled disposition that holds even when the cost is mortal.

5. **The limits of what one person can do for another.** You cannot form your child's character for them. You can create conditions, ask good questions, model the practice, and offer the right materials. But at some point — at many points, over many years — your child must choose. Alcibiades knew. He chose wrong. Plato knew. He built something that lasted a thousand years. The teacher's job is to make the choice available and clear. The choice itself belongs to the student.

## Age-Appropriate Emphases

### Ages 8-10: The Question Man, the Oracle, the Death

**Core story:** The barefoot ugly philosopher who asked questions everywhere and was famous for it. The Oracle's declaration, and Socrates's investigation. The method: asking simple questions until people realized they didn't know what they thought they knew. The trial and the death — presented as a choice to be honest rather than safe. Alcibiades as the cautionary student who knew but didn't do.

**What to teach:**
- The Oracle story (it's simple, memorable, and encapsulates the whole project)
- The idea that confidently being wrong is worse than honestly not knowing
- That asking good questions is more important than having all the answers
- That Socrates chose to die rather than stop asking questions — and that this is brave, not strange
- The Alcibiades lesson: knowing what's right and doing what's right are two different things

**What to leave for later:**
- The political context (Alcibiades's treason, Critias's tyranny)
- The philosophical technicalities of the Socratic paradox
- The epistemological problems with the *Apology* as a source
- The *Crito*'s argument about civic obligation (introduce at 11-14)

**One key practice for this age:** The daily "How do I know this?" question. Choose one thing the child encounters each day — something they hear, something they believe, something someone tells them — and ask, gently: *"How do we know that's true?"* Not to be cynical, but to practice the habit of checking. Three minutes. Every day.

**Success at this age:** Your child can tell the Oracle story correctly, knows that Socrates was famous for asking questions rather than giving answers, can name the moment they tried the "How do I know?" practice, and has thought about the Alcibiades problem in some concrete personal form.

---

### Ages 11-14: The Method, the Trial, the Students

**Core story:** Everything at the 8-10 level, plus: the method of elenchus in some detail. The Sophists as context. The political context of the trial (why Athens killed him — not just the formal charges but the background of Alcibiades and Critias). The *Crito* as a philosophical text. The question of what Socrates could have done differently.

**What to teach:**
- The elenchus in practical detail: how does a Socratic conversation actually work?
- The Sophist contrast: what is the difference between someone who teaches rhetoric and someone who pursues truth?
- The Alcibiades problem in more depth: knowing and doing. Aristotle's eventual critique.
- The *Crito* argument: why couldn't Socrates escape, and was he right?
- The "examined life" as a specific, daily practice — not just a phrase

**What to complicate:**
- Socrates's behavior at the trial: was his refusal to propose exile wisdom or arrogance? (No clean answer.)
- The daemon: what do we make of this? How does a rationalist explain an irrational inner signal?
- The charge about corrupting youth: was Socrates actually responsible for Alcibiades and Critias in any sense?

**Essential reading for this age:** Plato's *Apology* and *Crito* in full. They are short, accessible, and among the most important texts ever written. If your child reads only two texts from the Socratic corpus, these are the ones.

**Success at this age:** Your child can describe the elenchus in their own words, has read the *Apology*, can give a fair account of both sides of the "was Socrates right to refuse exile?" question, and has applied the examined-life practice to at least one significant belief of their own.

---

### Ages 15-18: The Full Philosophical Picture

**Core story:** Everything above, plus: the problem of sources (Plato vs. Xenophon vs. Aristophanes). The Socratic paradox and Aristotle's critique. The political philosophy of the trial (Athens, democracy, philosophical martyrdom). The philosophical legacy (Cynics, Cyrenaics, Stoics, Academy, Lyceum). The examined life as a specific philosophical commitment with costs.

**What to teach:**
- The epistemological problem: can we know the historical Socrates? What does it mean to work with filtered, interpreted sources?
- The Socratic paradox in detail: virtue is knowledge; no one does wrong willingly. Evaluate this carefully.
- The *Apology* as a philosophical document: what argument is Socrates actually making? What does he refuse and why?
- The *Crito* as a text in political philosophy: what is civic obligation? When is it owed? When, if ever, is it not?
- The Alcibiades and Critias cases in full: what do they tell us about the limits of philosophical education?
- The legacy: Stoicism, the Academy, intellectual martyrdom as a Western cultural archetype

**What to assign:**
- *Apology*, *Crito*, *Meno*, and Alcibiades's speech in *Symposium* (215a–222b) — all essential
- Gregory Vlastos, *Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher*, chapters 1-3
- I.F. Stone, *The Trial of Socrates* — read this specifically to challenge Socrates; argue back
- Pierre Hadot, *What Is Ancient Philosophy?*, Part I — on philosophy as way of life

**Success at this age:** Your child can make a serious, fair-minded argument about whether Socrates was right to refuse exile; can explain the Socratic paradox and Aristotle's objection; has read the *Apology*, *Crito*, and Alcibiades's speech; has applied the examined-life practice to a significant belief with genuine openness to revision.

## The Central Teaching Tension

The deepest tension in teaching Socrates is this: he taught that you cannot simply give someone knowledge. The knowledge must be drawn out, through dialogue, from what the student already, in some sense, contains. The teacher's role is not to fill but to midwife.

But you are teaching your child from a curriculum. You are presenting them with a series of great men and telling them to study these figures. This is not, on the surface, Socratic. It looks more like the Sophist model: expert presents content; student absorbs it.

The resolution is in how you present it. The Socratic question to ask at every point — about every figure in this sequence — is: *Is this true? How do we know? What would have to be the case for this to be false? What do you actually believe, after thinking it through?*

If your child walks away from the Leonardo profile with a set of memorized facts about Leonardo's methods, they have received content. If they walk away having genuinely asked whether Leonardo's work is great, what greatness means, whether they believe cross-domain curiosity produces better thinking, and whether they want to apply any of this to their own life — they have done something Socratic.

The curriculum provides the material. The Socratic habit provides the depth. Socrates is the figure who makes the difference visible.

## The Hardest Teaching Challenge

The hardest thing about teaching Socrates is that the content of his teaching is inseparable from the practice of it. You cannot teach "ask good questions" by giving a lecture about asking good questions. You teach it by asking good questions, by modeling the practice, by demonstrating in conversation what it looks like when someone is genuinely curious rather than performing curiosity.

This means that this week — perhaps more than any other week in the Nova Schola sequence — the parent's own practice matters as much as the child's. If your child sees you modeling the "How do I know this?" habit in your own thinking, in your own responses to news and claims and conventional wisdom, the lesson will land far deeper than if they read about Socrates and you then assign activities.

The practical suggestion: this week, pick one belief you hold confidently — something about politics, or parenting, or education, or history — and genuinely examine it in front of your child. Ask yourself, out loud, *How do I know this? What would have to be true for this to be wrong?* If you can reach genuine uncertainty about something you were certain of, and let your child see that — *that* is Socratic education.

## Connections to the Sequence

**Backward:**
- **Plato** (Day 3) — now you return to the source. Plato's entire philosophical project is downstream of Socrates's death. His distrust of democracy, his theory of the philosopher-king, his metaphysics — all of it is shaped by what Athens did in 399 BCE. Your student now understands the event that formed the thinker they've already studied.
- **Aristotle** (Day 4) — Aristotle's critique of the Socratic paradox (virtue is knowledge; no one does wrong willingly) is one of the founding moves of his ethics. His habituation theory — virtue is formed by repeated action, not just correct understanding — is precisely the Aristotelian correction to the Socratic position. Your student can now understand that debate from the inside.
- **Marcus Aurelius** (Day 5) — The Stoic tradition (Epictetus → Marcus) is Socratic in its core structure. The dichotomy of control is a Stoicized version of the Socratic priority of soul over external goods. Marcus's journal practice is Socratic self-examination adapted for daily life in a military camp.

**Forward:**
- **Isocrates** (Day 7) — the great rival educational tradition. Isocrates believed that rhetoric — not Socratic philosophy — was the proper foundation of civic education. His quarrel with the Platonic/Socratic tradition is one of the defining debates in the history of Western pedagogy. Your student will encounter it with far more depth now.
- **Cicero** (Day 8) — who said Socrates "brought philosophy down from the heavens and into the cities," making ethics and political philosophy the central philosophical concerns. Cicero is attempting to be Socrates for Rome.

## A Note on the Alcibiades Problem and Your Child

At some age — different for every child — your student will recognize themselves in Alcibiades. Not in Alcibiades's specific choices (hopefully), but in the general condition he describes: knowing what is right, and choosing something else.

This recognition can be painful. It can also be profoundly productive. The child who sees themselves clearly — who can say "I understand what I should do and I am choosing not to do it, and this is a real choice I'm making" — is in a far better position than the child who doesn't see the choice at all. The first step toward doing the right thing is seeing that you are currently not doing it.

Handle this gently. The Alcibiades discussion is not an opportunity for the parent to score points about what the child should be doing better. It is an opportunity for genuine, mutual examination — one where the parent also acknowledges the ways they, too, know what's right and choose otherwise. If Socrates taught anything it is that philosophical examination works best when both people are genuinely examining, not when one person is performing wisdom for the other.

## A Final Note on Sources

If your child is working through the sources at the 15-18 level, the tension between Plato's portrait and the historical Socrates is a real philosophical problem, not just a scholarly curiosity. The question "how do we know what Socrates believed?" is itself Socratic — it requires exactly the kind of careful source evaluation and epistemic humility that Socrates practiced.

You can use this productively: when reading the *Apology*, ask your child how they know whether this is what Socrates said. Discuss the difference between *Apology* (probably close to what was said) and *Republic* (probably Plato's own views). Ask: *Does it matter? If we can't perfectly reconstruct Socrates, what do we do with his image?*

The honest answer: we use the image as an invitation to examination, knowing that the invitation is more important than the historical accuracy. Even if Plato's Socrates is partly a construction, the invitation he embodies — *examine your life* — is real, and worth accepting.

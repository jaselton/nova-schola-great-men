# Socrates: Student Lesson (Ages 8-10)
## The Man Who Asked Questions Until Athens Killed Him

---

## The Story

Almost two thousand five hundred years ago, in the city of Athens, there lived a man who was famous for being ugly.

His name was **Socrates**. He had a wide flat nose, big bulging eyes, a bald head, and a big belly. He shuffled when he walked. He went barefoot every day, even in the cold, even in the snow. He wore the same rough old cloak, summer and winter.

He never combed his hair. He never dressed up. He never tried to look important.

But he was the most interesting person in Athens.

---

### The Smartest Soldier in the Army

Before Socrates became famous for asking questions, he was a soldier.

Athens was at war with another Greek city called Sparta and its allies. This war went on for almost thirty years. Socrates served in the army three different times.

He was a very strange soldier.

One time, during a long campaign far from home, something happened that everybody talked about for years. In the early morning, Socrates stood completely still — just standing there, thinking hard about something. And he kept standing there. All morning. Then all afternoon. Then all evening. The other soldiers noticed. Some of them set up their sleeping mats nearby to watch him, curious to see what he would do.

He stood there all night.

When the sun came up the next morning, Socrates said a quiet prayer to the rising sun, and then walked away, as if nothing unusual had happened.

He had been thinking so hard about a question that he didn't notice a whole day and night had passed.

---

### The Visit to the Oracle

One day, a friend of Socrates named Chaerephon did something unusual.

He traveled to the famous **Oracle at Delphi** — a holy place where, people believed, the god Apollo spoke through a special priestess. You could ask the Oracle a question, and the Oracle would give you an answer.

Chaerephon asked the Oracle: *"Is there anyone in Athens wiser than Socrates?"*

The Oracle answered: *"No."*

Chaerephon rushed back to Athens to tell Socrates.

Socrates was confused. He didn't think he was wise at all! He spent most of his time feeling confused about things he didn't understand. How could he be the wisest person in Athens?

So Socrates decided to investigate. He would find someone wiser than himself — and then the Oracle would be proven wrong.

---

### Looking for Someone Wiser

Socrates started with the **politicians** — the men who ran Athens. Surely they were wise!

He went to a famous politician and talked with him. The politician seemed very confident. He spoke with great certainty about justice, and right and wrong, and how to run a city.

But when Socrates asked him questions — simple questions, like "What exactly *do* you mean by justice? Can you explain that more carefully?" — the politician couldn't answer. He got confused. He started contradicting himself. He didn't actually know what he was talking about. He had just *thought* he did.

The politician was very embarrassed. He became angry at Socrates.

But Socrates thought: *Hmm. He thinks he knows things he doesn't really know. At least I know what I don't know.*

He went to the **poets** — the people who wrote beautiful plays and stories. Surely they were wise?

But again: the poets could write beautiful things, but when Socrates asked them to explain what their poems meant, they couldn't do it. They wrote by inspiration — like a sort of magic — but they didn't really understand their own work. And they thought, because they could write beautifully, that they were wise about everything. They weren't.

He went to the **craftsmen** — the potters and blacksmiths and builders. They were a little better. They genuinely knew how to make things. But they also thought that because they were good at making pots, they understood justice and goodness and the meaning of life. They didn't.

Socrates came to a conclusion. The Oracle was right — but for a surprising reason.

He was the wisest man in Athens **because he was the only one who knew what he didn't know**.

Everyone else was confident. Everyone else thought they understood things they didn't actually understand. Socrates was confused — but his confusion was honest. That, it turned out, was a kind of wisdom.

---

### The Question Man

Socrates decided his job was to help the people of Athens see what they didn't know.

He went to the marketplace — the **agora** — every day. He talked to anyone who would talk with him: generals, politicians, young men, craftsmen, visitors from other cities. He asked questions. Always questions.

- "What is courage? You say soldiers should be courageous — but what exactly *is* courage?"
- "What is justice? You say Athens is a just city — but how would you explain what justice actually means?"
- "What is a friend? You say you love your friends — but what makes someone a true friend?"

When people gave answers, Socrates asked more questions, gently, politely, until they realized their answers had a problem. Then he'd ask about the problem. Then another question. And another.

Almost always, the person he was talking with ended up saying: *"I don't actually know."*

That was the point.

Socrates believed that the very worst thing a person can do is act confidently on something they haven't actually examined. If you think you know what is good — but you haven't really thought about it — you will probably do all sorts of bad things, convinced you're doing the right thing.

The first step to wisdom is **admitting what you don't know**.

---

### The Young Men Who Loved Watching

Athens was full of young men from rich families who had nothing better to do than watch Socrates work.

They loved it.

There was nothing more entertaining than watching the most famous philosopher in Athens expose a pompous general as unable to define "courage." The young men laughed. They started copying Socrates, going around asking the same kinds of questions.

The politicians and generals did not find this funny.

They got angry. They said Socrates was embarrassing them. They said he was filling young men's heads with bad ideas. They said he was teaching the young to disrespect their elders and their city.

---

### A Friend Who Made a Big Mistake

The most famous of the young men who followed Socrates was a brilliant, handsome young man named **Alcibiades**.

Alcibiades was one of the most talented people in Athens. He was beautiful, smart, charming, rich, and everyone said he could be the greatest general the city had ever produced. He adored Socrates. He once said that Socrates was the only person who ever made him feel ashamed of himself — who helped him see that he wasn't nearly as good as he thought he was.

But Alcibiades didn't want to feel ashamed. He liked being admired. He wanted to be famous and powerful and celebrated. So he kept running away from the uncomfortable truths that Socrates showed him.

Alcibiades became a great general. He also became a traitor. He switched sides in the war three times, working for Athens, then for the enemy Sparta, then for Persia. He caused enormous harm to Athens. Later, he was murdered far from home.

Socrates showed Alcibiades what was true. But **you can show someone the truth — you cannot make them choose it**. Alcibiades chose something else.

---

### The Trial

When Socrates was about seventy-one years old — very old — three men decided they had had enough of him.

They brought him to court and charged him with two things:

1. **Not honoring the gods of Athens properly**
2. **Corrupting the youth** (filling the minds of young people with bad ideas)

Five hundred citizens would serve as the jury. They would listen to the charges, then vote.

Socrates spoke in his own defense. He did not apologize. He did not grovel. He did not weep or beg. He told the jury that he had been doing exactly what the god Apollo wanted him to do — questioning Athens and helping people see their own ignorance. He said this was a service to the city, not a crime. He said he would rather die than stop doing it.

The jury voted: **280 to 220** to convict him.

The jury then decided on the punishment. Socrates was supposed to suggest a penalty. He suggested that since he had been serving Athens his whole life, perhaps they should give him free meals for life — the honor they gave to Olympic champions. (This did not help his case.) Eventually, under pressure from friends, he suggested a fine. The jury decided on death.

---

### The Last Days

Socrates was put in prison to wait. His friends came to visit him. One friend — named **Crito** — had arranged for Socrates to escape. Guards were bribed. A house in another city was ready. All Socrates had to do was walk out.

Socrates said no.

"I have lived my whole life under the laws of Athens," he said. "Even if those laws are being used unjustly against me, I cannot break them now. That would be wrong."

His friends wept. Socrates kept talking, right up to the end — talking about whether the soul lives after death, talking about what wisdom is, talking about how to live well.

When the time came, the executioner brought in the cup of **hemlock** — a plant poison that would slowly stop his muscles from working, from the feet up.

Socrates drank it calmly.

His friends were crying. He told them not to cry.

As he lay down and the coldness moved up his body, he said his last words to his friend Crito: *"We owe a cock to Asclepius. Pay it — don't forget."* (It was a small debt to a god of healing. Even in his last moments, he was reminding a friend to do what was right.)

Then he was still.

---

## Why Socrates Matters

Socrates teaches us three very important things:

### 1. The most dangerous kind of wrong is being confidently wrong.

The politicians Socrates questioned were not bad people. They were confident people who hadn't examined what they believed. When confident people don't know what they're doing, they do a lot of damage — because they won't stop to check whether they're right. Socrates spent his life helping people slow down and ask: *"Wait — do I actually know this is true?"*

### 2. A good question is worth more than a wrong answer.

It might seem like it's better to have an answer — even if it's not quite right — than to have a question that doesn't get answered. Socrates believed the opposite. A bad answer that you're sure about will lead you to bad actions. A real question — honestly held, honestly investigated — leads to real understanding, eventually. He said the unexamined life is not worth living. He meant it.

### 3. Knowing what is right and doing what is right are two different things.

Alcibiades knew what Socrates was teaching. He understood it. He even agreed with it. He just... couldn't do it. He kept choosing what felt good over what was good. This is not just Alcibiades's problem. It is a human problem. Knowing is only the beginning. The second step — choosing to act on what you know — is where character is made.

## Things to Do This Week

**1. Play the Socrates Game.** Choose one thing you believe is true — something you are sure about. Then ask yourself: *How do I know this? What would it look like if this were wrong?* Try this once each day this week. Write down one belief and one honest question about it.

**2. Memorize this:**
> *"The unexamined life is not worth living."*
— Socrates

Talk with a parent about what it means. "Examined" means *looked at carefully, thought about*. What parts of your life do you look at carefully? What parts do you just assume?

**3. Try the "What do you mean by that?" practice.** When someone says something important — especially something that seems obviously true — try asking, politely: *"What do you mean by that, exactly?"* Pay attention to how the conversation changes.

**4. Write this in your notebook:** *"Something I am not sure I actually understand, even though I think I do."* Write at least one thing. Be honest. This is the most Socratic exercise there is.

**5. The Alcibiades question.** Think of something you *know* you should do — but that you keep not doing. Write it down. Why do you keep not doing it? Don't try to fix it this week; just see it clearly. That is already a step.

---

## A Question to Think About

Socrates said: *"I know that I know nothing."*

Does that seem like a strange thing for a wise man to say? What do you think he meant? Can knowing what you don't know be more valuable than thinking you know things you don't actually know?

---

## One Last Thing

Socrates never wrote a single word. Everything we know about him was written down by his students, especially Plato. Every conversation we have about him, every book we read, every idea about examined life that ever reached a classroom — all of it came through Plato, who was about twenty-nine years old when Socrates drank the hemlock.

Plato never got over it. He spent the rest of his very long life — he lived to be eighty — writing down everything he could remember and imagine about his teacher, over and over, in dialogue after dialogue.

The man who wrote nothing turned out to be impossible to forget.

That is what happens when a person actually believes what they teach, and teaches it with their whole life, all the way to the end.

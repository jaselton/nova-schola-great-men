# Socrates: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Socrates to one or more children. It is designed to be used by a parent or teacher at home, with no specialized materials beyond a notebook — though for older students, a paperback copy of Plato's *Apology* and *Crito* is strongly recommended. Total time commitment: roughly **45–75 minutes per day**, divisible into morning and afternoon sessions.

The plan is calibrated for **ages 8-14** as its default, with extensions and adjustments for ages 15-18 marked throughout.

The week moves through: **The Oracle and the Mission → The Method in Action → The Trial → The Death → The Students He Made**. This follows the logical development of Socrates's contribution: first his discovery of conscious ignorance, then the practice that discovery generates, then the test that practice was put to, then the philosophical consequences.

The most important structural feature of this week: **Socrates is the figure who asks you to examine the curriculum itself**. Every day includes at least one moment where the student is invited to question not just Socrates but the whole project they are engaged in. This is not destructive; it is the Socratic method applied to itself.

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for each student — this week's notebook is the **"Examined Life" journal**. It will be different from the Marcus notebook in one important respect: Marcus's notebook was about reviewing the day; the Socratic notebook is about examining beliefs.
- A separate notebook for the parent (strongly recommended — you will model the practice alongside the student)
- For ages 11-18: a paperback copy of Plato's *Apology* and *Crito* (often published together; any translation; G.M.A. Grube/Hackett is reliable and cheap; Benjamin Jowett is more literary)
- For ages 15-18: optionally, *Meno* (Hackett) and the *Symposium* — specifically Alcibiades's speech at 215a–222b
- A map of ancient Athens and Greece (optional but useful — locate Delphi, Athens, Potidaea, Delos)

### Choose Your Age Band

Select the student lesson appropriate to your child(ren):
- [Ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md)
- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

If you have multiple children across bands, each reads their own lesson; the week's activities are adjustable.

### The Examined Life Journal Setup

On Day One, establish the journal practice. The format is different from the Marcus notebook:

- Marcus's journal was: what went well, what went badly, what will I do better tomorrow.
- Socrates's journal is: **one belief I hold, and what I actually know to support it**.

Explain this to your student: *"Socrates's whole work was about finding the difference between believing something and actually knowing it. This week we're going to practice that. Every day, you'll write down one thing you believe — something you're fairly confident about — and then ask yourself honestly: how do I actually know this is true?"*

The journal entries should be short (four to eight sentences) but honest. The goal is not to become a skeptic about everything but to develop the habit of checking.

## Monday: The Oracle — Conscious Ignorance as the Beginning of Wisdom

### Morning: Read Together and Discuss (25–35 min)

Read the age-appropriate student lesson aloud, or have the student read it independently. Focus today on the Oracle story and the investigation that follows.

**Key moments to pause on:**

- The Oracle's declaration: *"How would you feel if someone told you that you were the wisest person in your city? Would you believe it? Would you try to test it, the way Socrates did?"*

- The investigation of politicians: *"Socrates found that the politician was confident but didn't actually know what he was talking about. Can you think of a time you were very sure about something, and then found out you were wrong? How did that feel?"*

- The conclusion: *"Socrates said he was wisest because he knew what he didn't know. Why would that be better than thinking you know things you don't actually know?"*

For 15-18, additionally discuss: the political and intellectual context. The Sophists were charging high fees to teach rhetoric — how to win arguments. Socrates charged nothing and tried to find truth. What is the difference between winning an argument and finding what's true? Can you do both at the same time?

### Afternoon: First Examined Life Journal Entry (20–30 min)

Open the new notebook. Write the date and the heading: *"My Examined Life Journal."*

Today's entry: **One thing I am confident is true — and what I actually know to support it.**

Template:

*I believe: [state the belief clearly]*
*I think I know this because: [give at least two specific reasons]*
*When I examine this more carefully: [anything uncertain, any exception, any thing you're less sure of than you thought]*
*After examining: [am I more confident, less confident, or differently confident?]*

Example (from a student, not a model answer):
*I believe: eating breakfast makes you do better in school.*
*I think I know this because: my parents always said so, and I read it in a health class.*
*When I examine this more carefully: I don't actually know if this has been studied carefully. I don't know if it's true for everyone. I might just be used to eating breakfast.*
*After examining: I'm less sure than I was, but I'm not sure it's wrong either.*

The point is not to disprove the belief. The point is to see the difference between "I believe X" and "I know X." Noticing that difference is the whole Socratic lesson.

**For 8-10:** Keep it very simple. The belief can be small and concrete: "Dogs are smarter than cats." Have them give one reason and one uncertainty. The habit matters more than the depth at this age.

**For 15-18:** The belief should be something genuinely important — a moral or political belief, not a factual one. *"I believe democracy is the best form of government."* Have them work through the template seriously, including sources and possible objections.

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight before bed, each person writes a second, very brief entry: one thing they assumed today that they didn't check. Two or three sentences. This establishes the daily rhythm immediately.

## Tuesday: The Method in Action — What the Elenchus Actually Looks Like

### Morning: The Method in Detail (25–35 min)

Today's focus is the mechanics of the Socratic method. Walk through the structure of a Socratic conversation:

1. Someone makes a confident claim.
2. Socrates asks a simple follow-up question.
3. The follow-up reveals a problem with the original claim.
4. A revised claim is offered.
5. Further questions reveal further problems.
6. Honest uncertainty — *aporia* — is reached.

Demonstrate this with your student, using a concrete example. Choose something age-appropriate:

**For 8-10:** *"What is a friend?"*
- "A friend is someone who likes you."
- "What if someone likes you but always wants to do what they want and never considers your needs — are they your friend?"
- "Well... a friend is someone who is nice to you."
- "What if someone is always nice to you but never tells you when you're making a mistake — are they a good friend?"
- Continue until the student arrives at: *"I don't know exactly what a friend is — it's more complicated than I thought."*

**For 11-14:** *"What is fairness?"*
- "Fairness means everyone gets the same."
- "If everyone gets the same amount of food, but some people are larger and need more — is that fair?"
- "Well, maybe fairness means everyone gets what they need."
- "If a person is lazy and doesn't work, and a person works hard, and they both get what they need — is that fair?"
- Continue toward *aporia*.

**For 15-18:** Use *"What is justice?"* — the question from the *Republic*. Walk through Thrasymachus's definition ("justice is the advantage of the stronger"), Socrates's examination of it, and the problems that emerge. This is a 30-minute seminar-style discussion, not a quick exercise.

### Afternoon: Play the Socratic Game (30–40 min)

Now the student tries the method.

Choose a topic — preferably something the student has been thinking about this week, or something from the curriculum. Have the student ask Socratic questions while the parent plays the role of the confident interlocutor.

Guidelines:
- The "interlocutor" (parent) gives confident answers without being deliberately evasive
- The "questioner" (student) asks only questions — no statements, no answers, only questions
- The goal is to reach genuine uncertainty, not to "win" or embarrass
- Pause when the conversation gets interesting; notice the logical move being made

After 15-20 minutes, switch roles.

Debrief: *"What happened to your confidence in your original belief? How did you feel when the question revealed a problem with your answer? Did the question feel aggressive or friendly?"*

**For 8-10:** Keep it to 10 minutes per side. The topic should be concrete and fun ("What is a game? What is winning?"). The parent should give obviously oversimplified answers so the child can find problems easily.

**For 15-18:** Keep going until genuine *aporia* is reached. Don't stop at the first complication. The method is most valuable — and most difficult — when you push past the first revision of the original claim.

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight's journal entry: write down one thing from today's Socratic game that surprised you. Did a question reveal something you hadn't thought of? Did reaching uncertainty feel uncomfortable? Write honestly about what the experience was like.

## Wednesday: The Trial — What Happens When Questions Are Dangerous

### Morning: The Trial Story (30–40 min)

Today is about the trial. Read or retell the trial story, using the age-appropriate student lesson as the text.

**Key moments:**

- The charges (impiety and corrupting the youth): *"Why do you think Athens was specifically worried about those two things? What does it mean to corrupt the youth? Do you think Socrates was actually corrupting them?"*

- Socrates's defense — especially the moment he says he would rather die than stop philosophizing: *"Why couldn't Socrates simply agree to stop asking questions? What would it mean to 'stop doing philosophy' for him? Is there anything you believe strongly enough that you wouldn't give it up even if it cost you something real?"*

- The counterpenalty (free meals for life): *"Was this arrogant? Was it honest? Was it both? Could it have been politically smarter — and does 'politically smarter' matter if you are trying to be truthful?"*

**For 15-18:** Read the *Apology* in full today, either as a morning assignment or as the morning session. Treat it as primary text, not summary. Pause at 17a (the opening), 26e–28a (the examination of Meletus), 28e–29a (the refusal to stop philosophizing), 35b–35e (the refusal to beg), and 40a–42a (the response to the death sentence). Discuss each.

### Afternoon: The "Examine the Curriculum" Session (25–35 min)

This is the most Socratic session of the week, and the most unusual.

Today, apply Socratic method to this curriculum.

Prompt the student:

*"We've been studying a series of great men. Leonardo, Alexander, Plato, Aristotle, Marcus Aurelius, now Socrates. Let's do what Socrates would do and examine our assumptions about this project."*

Questions to work through:
- *"What do we mean by 'great'? What makes someone great rather than merely famous or merely powerful?"*
- *"Is 'greatness' a good thing to study and imitate? What are the risks of only studying great men?"*
- *"Is there anything in the profiles we've studied that you believe because we presented it, rather than because you've actually thought it through?"*
- *"Can you study great men without being influenced to think you need to be like them? Should you be trying to be like them? Or should you be trying to be something different?"*

These are hard questions. Don't answer them for the student. Sit with the uncertainty. The goal is not to reach answers but to notice that the questions are real.

**For 8-10:** Simplify to: *"Why do we study these people? What are we supposed to get from it?"* and *"Is everything we've said about them true? How do we know?"* Take the second question seriously — say you're not certain about everything, because you're not.

**For 15-18:** Extend this into a full hour. Add: *"Is a curriculum about great men itself a kind of Sophistry — presenting impressive figures to produce admiration, rather than asking whether admiration is warranted?"* This is a sharp question. Take it seriously.

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight's journal entry: *"Something about this curriculum I am now less certain about — and why that's not necessarily bad."*

## Thursday: The Death — When Word and Deed Finally Meet

### Morning: The *Crito* and the Death Scene (30–40 min)

Today is about the last days: the refusal to escape, and the death itself.

For **ages 8-10**: retell the Crito story from the student lesson. Focus on: why Socrates wouldn't escape even though he could. Ask: *"If you were Socrates's friend and you had arranged the escape, how would you feel when he said no? Do you think he was right?"*

For **ages 11-14**: read the *Crito* together, or summarize and discuss it closely. The core argument of the Laws (the city speaking to Socrates about civic obligation) should be worked through carefully. Then: *"Is Socrates right? Should he escape? What would you do if you were him?"* — but do not reach a quick answer. Sit with it.

For **ages 15-18**: read the *Crito* in full as a primary text today. Then discuss: the argument that doing wrong in response to wrong is never justified. The argument about implicit civic obligation. The question of whether this is a satisfying response to Crito's practical concern. I.F. Stone's counterargument — that Socrates *should* have escaped, and that his refusal was theatrical or stubborn — is worth raising. Have the student defend Socrates against Stone or Stone against Socrates.

**The death scene.** Read aloud, for all ages, the closing paragraphs of the *Phaedo* — the description of Socrates drinking the hemlock. Plato writes it as a present witness. Whether or not he was actually there, it is one of the most powerful passages in Western literature.

Pause after reading. Don't immediately move to discussion. Let it sit.

Then: *"He spent his last day arguing about philosophy. He kept talking until the hemlock made him stop. What does that tell you about him?"*

### Afternoon: The Consistency Exercise (25–35 min)

Socrates at the trial, Socrates refusing to escape, Socrates drinking hemlock — these are all the same character we see at Potidaea and Delium. The argument is not in the *Apology*; it is in the whole shape of the life.

Today's exercise: **Character Consistency Mapping**.

Have the student choose one figure from their own life — a parent, teacher, coach, or historical figure — who they believe has genuine character (or who they're not sure about). Then identify:

1. A moment of **small decision** — when the cost of the right thing was low. How did they act?
2. A moment of **medium pressure** — when the cost was moderate. How did they act?
3. A moment of **high pressure or real cost** — when the right thing was genuinely costly. How did they act?

Do all three moments show the same person? If the character changes under pressure, what does that tell you?

Now, apply the same framework to yourself. Where have you been consistent? Where have you been inconsistent? Where are you uncertain?

**For 8-10:** Keep this very concrete. Use simple, recent examples from school or home. The question is just: *"Do you act the same when it's easy as when it's hard?"*

**For 15-18:** Apply this framework to a figure in the curriculum — Aristotle and Alexander, Marcus Aurelius and Commodus, Plato and the Academy. Where did they hold? Where did they break?

### End-of-Day Practice

Tonight's journal entry: *"Something I say I believe — and a moment when I didn't act on it. What was the gap? Why was it there?"*

---

## Friday: The Students He Made — The Limits and the Legacy

### Morning: Plato and Alcibiades — The Range of What a Teacher Can Produce (30–40 min)

Today is about outcomes. One teacher. One method. A range of results that spans Plato (who built a philosophical institution that ran for nearly a thousand years) to Alcibiades (who used philosophical sophistication to justify whatever he wanted) to Critias (who used it to justify murder).

**Discussion structure:**

1. Present Plato's response to Socrates's death: he left Athens, returned after a decade, built the Academy, spent his life writing the dialogues. *"Why do you think Plato responded to the loss of his teacher by building an institution? What was he trying to protect?"*

2. Present Alcibiades. Use his speech from the *Symposium* (even for younger students, a paraphrase is enough): he knew exactly what Socrates was showing him about himself, and he kept choosing the crowd and the applause instead. *"Is there anything admirable about Alcibiades's honesty about his own failure? Or does honesty about failure mean nothing if you don't act on it?"*

3. Present Critias. Brief, direct, without melodrama: he learned philosophical sophistication and used it in the service of political murder. *"What does this tell us about what philosophy can and can't do? Can thinking clearly make you a worse person, if your character is already oriented toward bad things?"*

4. The conclusion: **the teacher's job is to make the choice available and clear. The choice belongs to the student.** *"What does that mean for this week? For this curriculum? For your formation?"*

**For 15-18:** This conversation should be more demanding. Raise the following: Aristotle argued that Socratic method (virtue = knowledge) was insufficient because it didn't account for weakness of will — the gap between knowing and doing. He proposed that virtue must also be *habituated* — trained through repeated practice, not just correct understanding. Is Aristotle right? What does that imply about the relationship between Socrates and Aristotle in the curriculum — is Aristotle's habituation theory a correction to Socratic method, or a complement to it?

### Afternoon: The Week's Summation (25–35 min)

Return to the Examined Life journal from the week. Have the student reread all five days' entries privately.

Then write a final entry addressing:

1. *"What did I learn about Socrates this week?"*
2. *"Is there anything I believe differently after this week than I did before — about anything?"*
3. *"Will I keep the habit of asking 'How do I know this?' after this week? Why or why not?"*
4. *"What question from this week do I most want to keep thinking about?"*

The fourth question is important. The examined life does not end at the end of the week. It produces live questions that stay with you. Naming the question you will carry forward is itself a Socratic act.

**For 15-18:** In addition to the journal entry, assign one of the following essays (to be written this weekend or over the following week):

- *"Socrates argued that the unexamined life is not worth living. Defend or refute this claim. What would it mean for a life to be 'worth living'? Who has the standing to declare a life unworthy?"*
- *"Was Athens right to kill Socrates? Make the best argument you can on each side, then take a position."*
- *"The Socratic paradox says virtue is knowledge and no one does wrong willingly. Aristotle says this is wrong — weakness of will is real. Evaluate both positions. Which do you find more accurate about human moral psychology, and why?"*
- *"Alcibiades knew everything Socrates was showing him. He chose wrong anyway. What does this tell us about the limits of education? Does your answer change anything about why you are studying these figures?"*

### End-of-Day Practice

The final journal entry of the week. Three sentences:
1. *"The most important thing Socrates taught me."*
2. *"One question I am now more uncertain about than I was before."*
3. *"One practice from this week I want to continue."*

## After the Week

### The Sustaining Practice

Socrates's core practice does not require daily journaling (though the Marcus notebook can continue alongside it). It requires only one habit:

**Before acting on an important belief, ask: How do I know this is true?**

This can be practiced in the car, at the dinner table, in conversation about news and events, whenever your family encounters confident claims. The parent who models it — who openly asks "How do I know this is true?" about their own beliefs in front of their child — does more for this practice than any amount of assigned exercises.

If the Socratic habit takes root, it will be the most durable thing the curriculum installs. It will outlast the facts about any particular figure. It will change how your child evaluates every claim they encounter for the rest of their life.

### Further Reading

**For ages 8-10:** No required reading beyond the student lesson. If the child wants more, read them short passages from the *Apology* — the opening (17a) and the closing statement about the examined life (38a). Two sentences. Discuss.

**For ages 11-14:** *Apology* and *Crito* in full. Then, optionally, *Meno* — which is about whether virtue can be taught, the exact question this curriculum faces. It is accessible and important.

**For ages 15-18:** The full program: *Apology*, *Crito*, *Meno*, Alcibiades's speech in *Symposium*, *Laches* (on courage — a model of how the elenchus works on a specific virtue). Over the following months, Plato's *Phaedo* and *Republic* Books I–IV. Vlastos's *Socrates* for scholarly grounding. Pierre Hadot's *What Is Ancient Philosophy?* for the life-as-practice dimension.

### Connections to the Rest of the Sequence

Socrates is the root of the sequence from here on.

- **Isocrates** (Day 7): the rival. Isocrates built the other great Athenian educational tradition — rhetoric-centered, civic, practical — in explicit opposition to the philosophical tradition. The debate between these two pedagogies (Platonic/Socratic vs. Isocratean) is the founding debate of Western educational theory.
- **Cicero** (Day 8): "Socrates brought philosophy down from the heavens." Cicero attempted to be the Roman Socrates — civic philosopher, not academic one. How well did it work?
- **Boethius** (Day 10): writing *The Consolation of Philosophy* in prison under death sentence, Boethius is the Christian Socrates. The parallel is exact and not accidental.
- **Every subsequent figure**: ask, for each one — did they examine their lives? Did they know what they didn't know? Did their word and deed match at the critical moment?

The Socratic question — *Have you examined this?* — is the recurring question of the entire sequence.

---

## A Final Word to the Parent

This week is the week in which you can most productively examine yourself, because Socrates invites everyone to examine themselves. Not your child — you.

Before the week begins, ask yourself: What do I believe about this curriculum? Why am I doing it? What do I hope happens? What am I afraid won't happen? Have I examined these beliefs, or have I assumed them?

The parent who goes into this week with examined motives will teach it better than the parent who is performing a curriculum they haven't questioned. Socrates would say: the difference between those two parents is the whole game.

He might also say: the fact that you are doing it — even imperfectly, even with unexamined motives — is better than not doing it. The effort toward examination is already the practice.

The week begins Monday. Bring your notebook.

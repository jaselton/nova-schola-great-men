# Socrates
## The Man Who Asked Questions Until Athens Killed Him

**Born:** c. 470 BCE, Athens (deme of Alopece)
**Died:** 399 BCE, Athens (age ~71, execution by hemlock)
**Era:** Classical Antiquity (High Classical Athens)
**Domain:** Philosophy (ethics, method), civic life, education

---

## The One-Minute Story

Socrates was the son of a stonemason. He received a basic education, learned his father's trade, served as a soldier in three campaigns of the Peloponnesian War, and was known for standing motionless on a battlefield for twenty-four hours thinking through a philosophical problem. He was notoriously ugly — bald, wide-nosed, bug-eyed, potbellied. He went barefoot in all seasons and wore the same rough cloak every day.

He never wrote anything down.

At some point in midlife, his friend Chaerephon visited the Oracle at Delphi and asked whether anyone was wiser than Socrates. The Oracle said no. Socrates spent the rest of his life trying to prove the Oracle wrong. He went to the politicians and found they didn't know what they thought they knew. He went to the poets and found the same. He went to the craftsmen — who at least knew *their* craft — but found they mistook skill with tools for wisdom about life.

His conclusion: the Oracle was right, but for a strange reason. He was the wisest man in Athens only because *he knew what he didn't know*. Everyone else was certain. He was honestly uncertain. That turned out to be wiser.

So he made it his mission — a divine mission, he said — to help Athens see the limits of its own confidence. He talked to everyone. He asked questions. He exposed false certainty wherever he found it: in generals, poets, politicians, craftsmen, and ambitious young men. He charged no fees. He took no money. He built no school.

He did produce Plato — who built a school that ran for nearly a thousand years and whose writings contain more philosophy than any other human being has ever produced.

He also knew, and apparently failed to form, Alcibiades — the most brilliant and disastrous Athenian general of the age — and Critias, who became the most violent of the Thirty Tyrants.

In 399 BCE, three men charged him with impiety and corrupting the youth of Athens. Five hundred citizens voted 280–220 to convict him. When asked to name his punishment, Socrates suggested the city give him free meals for life, as a service to Athens — then, under pressure, proposed a fine. The jury sentenced him to death.

He was offered escape. He refused. He drank hemlock at age seventy-one with the composure he had shown on the battlefield at forty-eight. His last words were a reminder to pay a small debt to a friend.

He is the founding figure of Western philosophy and the first person in Western history to die specifically for the act of thinking.

## Why He Matters for Your Child

**Socrates teaches:**

- That asking good questions is more important than having correct answers — and that the habit of questioning your own assumptions is the beginning of real education
- That the most dangerous kind of ignorance is *confident* ignorance — believing you know something you do not actually understand
- That character revealed in calm, small moments (standing motionless at Potidaea, walking without hurry at Delium, refusing hemlock without drama) is the same character revealed in great ones
- That you cannot give another person genuine knowledge — you can only help them discover what they already, in some sense, know; this is as true of parents teaching children as of Socrates in the agora
- That there is an enormous difference between knowing what is right and actually doing it — Alcibiades knew what was right; he could not bring himself to do it; the lesson is sobering and permanent

**The formation challenge Socrates poses:**
Are you teaching your child to receive answers — or to ask better questions? Both matter. But if the questions are absent, the answers are only borrowed. Socrates insists they must be owned.

## In the Nova Schola Sequence

Socrates appears in the sequence after Plato and Aristotle — his own students — because you understand a teacher most clearly by studying what his teaching produced. By the time your student reaches Socrates, they have already seen the fruits: Plato's Academy, Aristotle's Lyceum, Alexander's empire. Now they return to the root.

Connections:
- **To Plato:** Socrates's death is the traumatic event that drives Plato's entire political philosophy. Plato never forgave democracy for killing his teacher, which is why the *Republic* is so suspicious of popular rule.
- **To Aristotle:** Aristotle's critique of Socratic ethics (particularly the claim that virtue is knowledge) is one of the founding moves of his moral philosophy. He thought Socrates underestimated weakness of will.
- **To Marcus Aurelius:** Marcus was formed on Epictetus, who was formed on Stoicism, which grew directly from Socratic ethics. Marcus's evening journal practice — reviewing whether his actions matched his beliefs — is Socratic self-examination adapted to a Roman emperor's tent.
- **Forward:** Cicero (next in the sequence) will say that Socrates "brought philosophy down from the heavens and into the cities." Every civic philosopher after Socrates works in his shadow.

## Formation Signature

> *The unexamined life is not worth living.*
— Socrates, *Apology* (as reported by Plato), 399 BCE

**Core practice:** Structured self-questioning — not to produce doubt, but to distinguish what you genuinely know from what you have merely assumed. The daily practice is simple: before accepting any belief you act on, ask *How do I know this is true?* before you act.

**The irreducible lesson:** A person who has learned to question their own assumptions honestly is harder to deceive, harder to manipulate, and harder to govern badly. This is why Athens killed Socrates, and why the question he asked — *Have you examined this?* — is still the most subversive thing you can teach a child.

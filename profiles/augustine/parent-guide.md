# Augustine of Hippo: Parent Teaching Guide

---

## Overview: What This Profile Teaches

**Core Lesson:**  
A human being is shaped not only by what he knows, but by what he loves. Augustine teaches that education without formation of desire produces brilliance without peace.

**Secondary Lessons:**
- Small sins can reveal deep disorder
- Friendship and imitation shape the heart
- Success does not guarantee inward health
- Repentance is intellectually serious, not anti-intellectual
- Civilizations cannot survive on external power alone

---

## Age-Appropriate Emphases

### Ages 8-10: Peace, Friendship, and Small Choices
**Focus on:**
- Augustine as a boy who felt restless inside
- The pear theft as a lesson in doing wrong with friends
- The idea that what we love shapes us
- The difference between temporary fun and real peace

**Avoid:**
- Detailed treatment of sexual sin
- Complex theological debates about grace
- Ecclesiastical controversies and coercion

**Key Activities:**
- Memory work: "Our hearts are restless until they rest in You"
- Restless vs. peaceful habits chart
- Friendship discussion
- Simple narration of the conversion story

---

### Ages 11-14: Desire, Wisdom, and the Difference Between Cleverness and Goodness
**Focus on:**
- Augustine's classical education and ambition
- The pear theft as moral psychology, not just rule-breaking
- The difference between being smart and being ordered
- Why false systems attract serious people
- The crisis in Milan as a struggle of the will

**Complicate:**
- Show that Augustine genuinely wanted truth
- Don't reduce his life to "bad youth, good adult"
- Help students see that inner disorder can coexist with visible success

**Key Activities:**
- Journaling on dominant loves
- Comparison of cleverness vs. wisdom
- Guided reading from *Confessions*
- Friendship audit and discussion

---

### Ages 15-18: Conversion, Grace, and Political Theology
**Focus on:**
- Augustine's analysis of the will and disordered love
- *Confessions* as prayer + psychology + theology
- The redemption rather than rejection of classical rhetoric
- *The City of God* as a response to civilizational crisis
- Augustine's strengths and his real limits

**Philosophical Questions:**
- Is human failure mainly ignorance or disordered love?
- Can rhetoric serve truth without becoming manipulation?
- What can politics accomplish, and what can it never accomplish?
- How should we judge a great thinker who is profound and limited at once?

**Key Activities:**
- Primary source reading and annotation
- Comparative essay with Marcus Aurelius, Cicero, or Aquinas
- Analysis of modern restlessness through Augustine
- Structured debate on grace, will, and moral responsibility

## Common Misconceptions to Address

### Misconception 1: "Augustine was just a guilty man who hated pleasure"
**Reality:** Augustine does not hate created goods. He argues that good things become destructive when loved in the wrong order.

**How to correct:** Ask, "Is friendship good? Is ambition ever good? Is beauty good? What happens when a good thing becomes the highest thing?"

---

### Misconception 2: "Conversion means turning off your brain"
**Reality:** Augustine's conversion came after years of intellectual struggle. He did not abandon thought; he stopped using thought to protect disobedience.

**How to correct:** Walk students through the long path: Cicero, Manichaeism, skepticism, Platonism, Ambrose, Scripture.

---

### Misconception 3: "The pear theft is too small to matter"
**Reality:** Augustine uses the small act precisely because it reveals motive. He wants students to see that the moral life is built in habits, appetites, and social influence.

**How to correct:** Ask, "What small choices have big effects over time?" Relate it to gossip, cheating, showing off, secret scrolling, or cruelty done for laughs.

---

### Misconception 4: "If Augustine became great later, the early mistakes do not matter"
**Reality:** Augustine's greatness partly comes from taking his earlier disorder seriously enough to understand it. The mistakes mattered because they formed him—and because he later told the truth about them.

**How to correct:** Emphasize confession as understanding, not self-hatred.

---

### Misconception 5: "Because Augustine was a saint, every judgment he made was right"
**Reality:** Augustine's acceptance of coercion against religious opponents is a real limit. Greatness does not erase fallibility.

**How to correct:** For older students, model reverent criticism: admiration without naïveté.

## Discussion Facilitation Tips

### For Younger Students (8-10)
**Use concrete examples and stories:**
- Don't: "Augustine diagnosed disordered desire"
- Do: "Augustine did not need the pears. So why did he steal them?"

**Ask open-ended questions:**
- "What can make a heart feel restless?"
- "What kinds of friends help us do right?"

**Connect to their experience:**
- "Have you ever done something silly just because a group was doing it?"
- "What helps you feel peaceful after a hard day?"

---

### For Middle Students (11-14)
**Introduce complexity:**
- "Can someone be very smart and still unwise?"
- "Why do false beliefs sometimes attract serious people?"
- "Why is belonging so powerful?"

**Use comparison:**
- Compare Augustine's ambition with Cicero's public ambition
- Compare Augustine's inward struggle with Marcus Aurelius' Stoic self-command

**Encourage analysis:**
- "What did Augustine actually want most at each stage of his life?"
- "What finally made him stop postponing obedience?"

---

### For Older Students (15-18)
**Push philosophical depth:**
- "Is the will weak, divided, or misdirected?"
- "Does Augustine improve on classical moral psychology or distort it?"
- "Can politics produce order without ultimate meaning?"

**Demand evidence:**
- "Show me where in *Confessions* Augustine links knowledge and desire."
- "What passage from *The City of God* supports your claim?"

**Connect to their decisions:**
- "What does your own restlessness reveal about your loves?"
- "Where are you using intelligence to protect a habit you already know is weak?"

---

## Sensitive Topics & How to Handle Them

### Augustine's Sexual Life
**Why it matters:** Augustine's disorder was not only intellectual; it was moral and embodied.

**How to present it:**
- Ages 8-10: "He made some selfish choices about pleasure and self-control."
- Ages 11-14: "He had relationships and habits that were not rightly ordered and knew they were part of his struggle."
- Ages 15-18: Full, sober discussion of concubinage, desire, continence, and how Augustine interprets these in *Confessions*.

**What to avoid:** Sensationalizing. The goal is moral seriousness, not voyeurism.

---

### Grace and Free Will
**Why it matters:** Augustine becomes central in later debates because he emphasizes the need for grace.

**How to present it:**
- Younger students: "We need God's help to become truly good."
- Older students: Introduce the Pelagian controversy carefully, as a debate about what human beings can do on their own.

**What to avoid:** Turning this into an abstract system too early.

---

### Coercion and Religious Controversy
**Why it matters:** Augustine's support for coercion against Donatists is morally significant.

**How to present it:**
- Ages 8-10: Omit.
- Ages 11-14: Briefly note that Augustine fought strongly against false teaching.
- Ages 15-18: Explain that he accepted state pressure against religious opponents and that this should be judged critically.

**Teaching opportunity:** Great figures must be read with admiration and discernment.

## Extension Activities (Beyond Core Lessons)

### Additional Reading

**For Ages 8-10:**
- Simplified retellings of Augustine's conversion and childhood
- Storybook biographies from Christian publishers

**For Ages 11-14:**
- Excerpts from *Confessions* Books I, II, and VIII
- Peter Kreeft's accessible introductions to Augustine

**For Ages 15-18:**
- Augustine, *Confessions* (full or selections)
- Augustine, *The City of God* Book XIX
- Peter Brown, *Augustine of Hippo*
- James O'Donnell, *Augustine: A New Biography*

---

### Project Ideas

**For Ages 8-10:**
- Draw the pear tree story as a comic strip
- Make a "peace habits" poster
- Memorize and illustrate the restless-heart quote

**For Ages 11-14:**
- Keep a one-week journal tracking moments of restlessness
- Write a letter from Augustine to a modern teenager about peer pressure
- Compare one of Augustine's desires with a modern temptation

**For Ages 15-18:**
- Essay on disordered love in modern life
- Compare Augustine and Marcus Aurelius on self-mastery
- Seminar on whether politics can save a civilization
- Annotated reading of *Confessions* Book VIII

## Assessment: How Do You Know They've Learned?

### Younger Students (8-10)
**Look for:**
- Ability to retell the pear story and its lesson
- Understanding of restless vs. peaceful choices
- Awareness that friends shape behavior
- Honest reflection on little choices

---

### Middle Students (11-14)
**Look for:**
- Ability to explain why the pear theft matters
- Recognition that intelligence and goodness are not identical
- Thoughtful reflection on desire and friendship
- Willingness to identify inward motives, not just outward acts

---

### Older Students (15-18)
**Look for:**
- Clear understanding of Augustine's concept of disordered love
- Ability to connect *Confessions* to moral psychology and education
- Nuanced judgment about Augustine's strengths and limits
- Application of Augustinian insights to modern restlessness

**Don't accept:**
- Simplistic "religion fixed him" summaries
- Lazy hero-worship
- Reduction of Augustine to guilt or mere introspection

---

## What Success Looks Like

### Short-term (During the lesson/week)
- Student can explain Augustine's main lesson in age-appropriate terms
- Student reflects honestly on one area of restlessness
- Student recognizes peer influence more clearly
- Student begins distinguishing pleasure from peace

### Medium-term (Month after)
- Student notices how loves and habits shape behavior
- Student uses Augustine's story to interpret friendships and choices
- Student becomes more willing to confess motives honestly

### Long-term (Year+ after)
- Student has internalized the question: "What do I love most?"
- Student sees moral formation as deeper than rule-following
- Student understands that inner order matters for outward greatness
- Student is less easily fooled by cleverness without character

## Troubleshooting Common Challenges

### "My child thinks Augustine is too gloomy"
**Response:** Augustine is serious, but not gloomy. He is honest about disorder because he believes peace is possible.

**Try:** Keep returning to the theme of rest rather than guilt alone.

---

### "My child thinks being smart should automatically make people good"
**Response:** Augustine is the corrective. He shows why talent without formation can intensify disorder.

**Try:** Compare real-life examples of clever people who made foolish moral choices.

---

### "The material feels too theological"
**Response:** Start with universally human questions: Why do people do what they know is wrong? Why doesn't success satisfy? What makes a friendship good or dangerous?

**Try:** Enter through the pear story and the idea of restlessness before moving into doctrine.

---

### "My child wants clear villains and heroes"
**Response:** Augustine helps mature moral imagination. He is admirable and flawed. That complexity is part of the educational value.

**Try:** Ask, "What should we imitate? What should we question?" Keep both answers on the table.

---

## Final Thoughts for Parents

### What Augustine Really Teaches

Augustine's life is not mainly about becoming religious. It is about becoming truthful.

He teaches that the real battlefield of education is the heart: what we love, excuse, pursue, and refuse to surrender. He also teaches that confession is not the enemy of greatness. In his case, confession is one of the reasons greatness became possible.

This makes Augustine especially valuable in an age of image management, self-branding, distraction, and restless performance.

He asks the question many modern systems avoid:

**What is your child learning to love?**

If you keep that question alive, Augustine is already doing his work.

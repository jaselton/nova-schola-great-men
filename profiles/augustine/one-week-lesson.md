# Augustine of Hippo: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

**Target:** All ages (adjust depth by age band)  
**Time commitment:** 45-60 minutes/day  
**Structure:** 5 days, Monday-Friday  
**Goal:** Introduce Augustine's life, conversion, and central lesson that we become what we love

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed
- Notebook or journal
- Pencil or pen
- Bible (for Romans 13:13-14)
- Optional copy of *Confessions* or selected excerpts
- Index cards for memory work

### Choose Your Age Band
- **Ages 8-10:** story, friendship, peace, small choices
- **Ages 11-14:** desire, wisdom, peer pressure, honesty
- **Ages 15-18:** conversion, will, grace, rhetoric, political theology

---

## Monday: The Restless Heart

### Morning: Read Together (20-30 min)

**Ages 8-10:** Read the [student lesson ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md) together  
**Ages 11-14:** Read the [student lesson ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)  
**Ages 15-18:** Assign the [student lesson ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md) or read key sections aloud

**Pause to discuss:**
- Why was Augustine restless even when he was successful?
- What does it mean to have a heart pulled in different directions?
- What is the difference between excitement and peace?

---

### Afternoon: Memory Work + Journal Start (15-20 min)

Write Augustine's famous line on an index card:

> "Our hearts are restless until they rest in You."

**Journal prompt:**
- Ages 8-10: "What helps me feel peaceful?"
- Ages 11-14: "When do I feel restless, and why?"
- Ages 15-18: "What kinds of restlessness in my life might reveal disordered loves?"

---

## Tuesday: The Pear Tree

### Morning: Tell the Story (15-20 min)

Retell Augustine's pear theft in simple language.

Key point: he did not steal because he needed pears. He stole for the thrill, the group, and the pleasure of doing wrong.

**Discuss:**
- Why do people do wrong when they do not need to?
- How can friends make bad choices feel easier?
- Why do small sins matter?

---

### Afternoon: Motive Exercise (20-30 min)

**Ages 8-10:** Draw the pear tree scene and label the motives: excitement, friends, showing off, bad choice.

**Ages 11-14:** Write one paragraph: "Why Augustine thought the pear theft mattered."

**Ages 15-18:** Write a short reflection comparing the pear theft to a modern act of pointless transgression—online cruelty, cheating for the thrill, vandalism, or performative rebellion.

---

## Wednesday: The Conversion

### Morning: Read the Turning Point (20-30 min)

Tell or read the Milan conversion story:
- Augustine in inner turmoil
- the voice saying "take and read"
- Romans 13:13-14
- his final surrender

If appropriate, read the passage from Romans aloud.

**Discuss:**
- Why had Augustine delayed so long?
- What changed in that moment?
- Is knowing the truth the same as obeying it?

---

### Afternoon: Copywork / Reflection (15-25 min)

**Ages 8-10:** Copy:  
> "Our hearts are restless until they rest in You."

**Ages 11-14:** Copy and reflect:  
> "Late have I loved you, Beauty so ancient and so new..."

Write 3-4 sentences: What is Augustine sorry about here?

**Ages 15-18:** Copy Romans 13:13-14 or Augustine's "Late have I loved you" passage. Then write: "What excuses do people use when they do not want to change yet?"

---

## Thursday: Augustine and the Big Questions

### Morning: Socratic Discussion (20-30 min)

**Ages 8-10:**
1. What gives peace that lasts?
2. What kind of friends help us become better?
3. Why is being smart not enough?

**Ages 11-14:**
1. What's the difference between cleverness and wisdom?
2. Why do bad groups make wrong choices feel normal?
3. What do you think Augustine loved most at different stages of his life?

**Ages 15-18:**
1. Is the human problem mainly ignorance or disordered love?
2. Can rhetoric serve truth, or does it always risk manipulation?
3. Can politics or success ever solve the deepest human problem?

---

### Afternoon: Comparative Activity (20-30 min)

Compare Augustine to one earlier figure already studied.

Options:
- **Marcus Aurelius:** inner life, but Stoic rather than Christian
- **Cicero:** rhetoric and public life vs. rhetoric ordered to confession and theology
- **Socrates:** pursuit of truth, but different accounts of moral failure

**Prompt:** What does Augustine see that the earlier figure sees less clearly?

---

## Friday: Narration and Application

### Morning: Narration (15-25 min)

**Ages 8-10 (oral):**  
"Tell me Augustine's story and the biggest lesson you learned from him."

**Ages 11-14 (written):**  
Write one page: "What Augustine learned about the heart."

**Ages 15-18 (analytical):**  
Write 2-3 pages: "Why Augustine thinks knowledge alone cannot save a person."

---

### Afternoon: The One Thing (20-30 min)

End the week by choosing one concrete practice.

**Possible commitments:**
- [ ] Notice one restless habit this week and interrupt it
- [ ] Tell the truth quickly without excuse-making
- [ ] Step back from one friendship pattern that pulls me downward
- [ ] Spend 10 minutes daily in silence, prayer, or journaling
- [ ] Read one short passage from *Confessions* each day
- [ ] Other: _______________________

**Parent:** Ask at the end of next week, "What did this practice reveal about what you love?"

---

## Extension Ideas (If You Want More)

### Read More
- *Confessions* Book I — childhood and the restless heart
- *Confessions* Book II — the pear theft
- *Confessions* Book VIII — the conversion
- *The City of God* Book XIX — peace and the highest good

### Deepen the Week
- Compare Augustine's confession with a modern memoir
- Map the timeline of his search for truth
- Discuss how modern restlessness resembles Augustine's

### Family Integration
- Use the phrase "What are you loving most right now?" in ordinary family conversations
- Notice where the family tends toward hurry, vanity, distraction, or comparison
- End one dinner with each person naming one thing that brings peace and one thing that creates restlessness

---

## Assessment: How Do You Know They Learned?

Look for:

✓ Ability to explain Augustine's story in age-appropriate terms  
✓ Recognition that motives matter, not just outward actions  
✓ More awareness of peer pressure and belonging  
✓ Understanding that success is not the same as peace  
✓ Willingness to ask, "What do I love most right now?"  
✓ One concrete practice chosen and attempted

**The real goal:** Not that students memorize dates, but that they begin to recognize the moral formation of desire in their own lives.

---

## What's Next?

After Augustine, consider studying:

- **Boethius** — wisdom under imprisonment and civilizational collapse
- **Aquinas** — the high synthesis of faith and reason Augustine helped make possible
- **Marcus Aurelius** — contrast in accounts of the inner life
- **Cicero** — rhetoric before Augustine's conversion of rhetoric

Augustine helps students see that greatness is not only what a person builds outwardly, but how deeply he learns to tell the truth about the soul.

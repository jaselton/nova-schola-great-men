# Plato: One-Week Lesson Plan

---

## Overview

This is a five-day lesson plan for teaching Plato to one or more children. It is designed to be used by a parent or teacher at home, with no specialized materials (though a few books help). Total time commitment: roughly **45–75 minutes per day**, divisible into morning and afternoon sessions.

The plan is calibrated for **ages 8-14** as its default. Extensions and substitutions for ages 15-18 are marked throughout.

The week moves through: **The Teacher → The Method → The Loss → The Institution → The Legacy**. This is Plato's own life sequence, compressed into a week of learning.

---

## Before You Begin

### Materials Needed

- A notebook for the child (the "Plato notebook" for the week)
- Pencils, pens
- String or yarn (for the Cave demonstration)
- A flashlight or phone flashlight
- Small objects and a white wall (for the Cave demonstration)
- Optional: modeling clay, paper for a scroll, simple craft supplies
- A printed copy of the *Crito* or *Apology* (freely available online)
- Ideally: a copy of Hackett's *Five Dialogues* or the *Republic* in a readable translation

### Choose Your Age Band

Pick the student lesson appropriate to your child(ren):
- [Ages 8-10](./student-ages-8-10.md)
- [Ages 11-14](./student-ages-11-14.md)
- [Ages 15-18](./student-ages-15-18.md)

If you have multiple children across bands, each can read their own lesson; the week's activities below work across ages with minor adjustment.

---

## Monday: The Teacher — Socrates and Plato's Formation

### Morning: Read Together (20–30 min)

Read the age-appropriate student lesson aloud, or have the student read it independently. Then discuss.

**Key moments to pause on:**
- Plato's aristocratic family and the political expectations around him — *what were your parents' expectations for you?*
- His encounter with Socrates — *what made Socrates so compelling to Plato?*
- The decade of *synousia* — *what does it mean to "spend time near" a serious mind?*

**For 15-18:** Additionally read the opening of the *Apology* (sections 17a–24b) — Socrates' initial statement to the jury. Notice together Socrates' voice: ironic, dignified, unhurried.

### Afternoon: The *Synousia* Question (15–20 min)

Sit with your child. Ask, and let them think seriously:

1. *"Who is the most formative adult in your life — someone whose way of thinking has shaped yours?"*
2. *"When was the last time you spent real, uninterrupted time with them?"*
3. *"What would it take to get more of that kind of time?"*

If the answer to #1 is "I'm not sure" or "no one" — *this is the most important diagnostic in the whole week*. The child who cannot name a formative adult has revealed the single most important thing about their current formation: there isn't enough of it.

Do not panic. The rest of the week will begin answering the question *what do we do about that?*

### Homework

In the Plato notebook:
- Write today's date, then: "Day 1: The Teacher."
- Answer, in at least a paragraph: *"If I could spend one full day with any person I've known — just to watch how they think — who would I pick, and why?"*

---

## Tuesday: The Method — Dialogue, Questions, the Examined Life

### Morning: Memory Work (5 min)

Learn and recite together:

> **"The unexamined life is not worth living."**
> *— Socrates, in Plato's Apology*

Repeat three times, slowly. At the end of the day, ask the child to recite from memory.

**For 11-14, add:** *"Let no one ignorant of geometry enter."*

**For 15-18, add:** *"Until philosophers rule as kings or those who are now called kings and leading men genuinely and adequately philosophize, cities will have no rest from evils."* — Republic 473c-d

### Afternoon: The Dialogue Reading (30–45 min)

Read aloud, with voices, one of the following — entirely:

- **For 8-10:** Read aloud the Cave allegory (from Republic Book VII, 514a–520a), simplified if needed. About 10 minutes.
- **For 11-14:** Read aloud the entire *Crito* (about 15 pages, 30–40 minutes aloud). Parent reads one character, child reads the other. Swap halfway.
- **For 15-18:** Read aloud the entire *Apology*, Parts I–III (about 20 minutes for the prosecution-phase speech alone). If time allows, continue.

**After reading:**

- *"What did you notice about the conversation — not the content, but the way the people were talking?"*
- *"Where did one person's argument shift because of what the other said?"*
- *"How is this different from reading a textbook or a speech?"*

Then ask the core dialogue-form question:

- *"Why do you think Plato wrote his philosophy as conversations instead of as essays or lectures?"*

Let the child articulate it. The insight they reach themselves is worth ten times an insight you tell them.

### Homework

Begin the **Three-Whys Discipline**:

For the rest of this week (and ideally longer), every time the child wants something, believes something, or decides something, ask three "why?" questions in a row — of themselves, in their head.

In the notebook, at the end of the day, they should note:
- One moment when three whys changed their mind about something
- One moment when three whys confirmed what they already thought — but gave them a better reason

## Wednesday: The Loss — The Trial and Execution of Socrates

### Morning: Read the Trial (20–30 min)

**For 8-10:** Tell the story of the trial, simply. Use the account in the student lesson. Let the unfairness land without rushing to soften it.

**For 11-14:** Read aloud sections 24b–28a of the *Apology* (where Socrates questions his accuser Meletus about the charges). This is one of the most pedagogically dense passages in Plato — Socrates turning his trial *into* a Socratic dialogue.

**For 15-18:** Read the closing of the *Apology*, sections 38c–42a (after the death sentence, Socrates' address to the jury about what death is and why an examined life is worth living even unto its end). This is among the most important passages in Western literature.

### Afternoon: Socratic Discussion (30 min)

Sit with your child. Work through these questions in order:

**For 8-10:**
1. Was it fair for Athens to kill Socrates? Why or why not?
2. If Socrates hadn't been killed, would we know about him?
3. What should Plato have done after his teacher was killed?

**For 11-14:**
1. Socrates could have saved his life by apologizing. He refused. Was that courage or stubbornness? How can we tell the difference?
2. In the *Crito*, Socrates refuses to escape from prison even when his friends offer to bribe the guards. His argument: he has benefited from Athens' laws for seventy years, so he cannot flee their unjust verdict now without betraying the relationship. Is this convincing?
3. What is the difference between *obeying an unjust law* and *complying with injustice*? Are they ever the same thing?

**For 15-18:**
1. Socrates practiced civil disobedience *against the Thirty* (refusing to arrest Leon) and civil obedience *to the democracy* (accepting his death sentence). How do we explain the asymmetry?
2. Is Socrates' argument in the *Crito* a good argument, or a rationalization of a decision he had already made for other reasons?
3. Plato was "ill" and absent from Socrates' death (per the *Phaedo*). What does that absence tell us about Plato himself, at twenty-eight?

### Afternoon Activity: The Cave Demonstration (15 min)

Set up a physical Cave. You'll need: a dark room, a flashlight, small objects, and a white wall.

Darken the room. Point the flashlight at the wall. Pass small objects between the flashlight and the wall — a toy, a fork, your hand. Watch the shadows.

Have your child turn and face the wall directly (the "prisoner" position). They watch only the shadows. Then have them turn around — see the light source, see the objects being passed. Then have them step outside the room into the ordinary light.

Now ask:
- *"How would you explain what's outside this room to someone who has only ever seen the shadows on the wall?"*
- *"If you told them the truth, would they believe you?"*

### Homework

In the notebook:
- Write one paragraph: *"A shadow I used to mistake for reality is ______."*
- (All ages can do this; the depth of the answer will vary by age.)

## Thursday: The Institution — Building the Academy

### Morning: The Academy Story (20 min)

Read (or retell from the lesson) the founding of the Academy. Emphasize:

- Plato's twelve years of wandering before returning
- The purchase of the specific piece of land
- The physical features — the grove, the paths, the gymnasium
- The curriculum — math in the morning, dialectic in the afternoon
- The 900-year duration
- The student who would come at seventeen and stay twenty years (Aristotle)

**Key question (all ages):**

*"Why did Plato build a school instead of just writing books about Socrates?"*

Let them reach this answer: *because schools are places, and places outlast people*.

### Afternoon: Build a Little Academy (45–60 min)

This is the main project of the week. As a family, commit to the following:

1. **Pick a place.** A corner of a room. A specific chair and a desk. The back patio. A spot at the kitchen table. Name it. Write its name on a small card and put it there.

2. **Write a short "Academy charter."** Two or three rules, agreed to together. Examples:
   - No screens inside the Academy
   - Only reading, thinking, writing, drawing, or real conversation happens here
   - When someone is in the Academy, they are not interrupted except for actual emergencies
   - One hour a day, minimum, for one month

3. **Assemble a small shelf.** Two or three books that will live there. Include at least one Plato dialogue (the *Crito* or *Apology* or *Republic*). Include one math book. Include one book the child has chosen.

4. **Put a notebook there.** The "Academy notebook" — for questions, sketches, problems, things noticed.

5. **Hold the first session.** Everyone in the family spends 15 minutes in (or near) the Academy, doing something consistent with the charter. Silence is fine. Conversation is fine. No screens.

**For 15-18:** In addition to the family Academy, have them start designing something of their own — a reading group, a conversation circle, a regular salon with friends. The Academy is a thing they need in their own life, separate from the family.

### Homework

The Academy is now alive in your home. For the rest of the week (and ideally the rest of the month), the child spends at least 30 minutes a day there, no screens, doing something serious.

## Friday: The Legacy — The Mentor Chain and the Forward-Looking Question

### Morning: The Chain (20 min)

Draw on a large piece of paper (or a whiteboard, or print it out):

**Socrates → Plato → Aristotle → Alexander**

Talk through it:

- Socrates taught Plato.
- Plato built the Academy, where Aristotle studied for 20 years.
- Aristotle tutored Alexander at the school at Mieza.
- Alexander reached the edge of the known world.

**Ask:**
- *"Plato died about thirteen years before Alexander was born. How is it that Plato shaped Alexander?"*
- *"What does this tell you about the relationship between teachers and students across time?"*

Then put this paper on the wall where the child can see it for weeks.

### Afternoon: Narration and Reflection (30 min)

**Narration (10–15 min):**  
The child retells Plato's life story, from memory, aloud. Start at Plato's aristocratic childhood. Move through meeting Socrates, the trial, the wandering years, the Academy, the writings, Syracuse, the legacy.

Listen without interrupting except for gentle prompts. After they finish, ask: *"What part of his life will you remember most clearly ten years from now?"*

**Reflection (15 min):**  
In the notebook, the child writes (depending on age):

**For 8-10:**
- *"The Great Teacher in my life right now is ______."*
- *"One thing I learned from Plato that I want to remember is ______."*
- *"In my Academy, I will ______."*

**For 11-14:**
- *"The most important thing I learned from Plato this week is ______."*
- *"The cultural 'shadow' I want to test is ______."*
- *"The person I want to spend more *synousia* time with is ______."*
- *"If I had to build one small institution this year, it would be ______."*

**For 15-18:**
- A single-page reflection essay: *"Why Plato matters to me right now, at this specific age, in this specific world."*

### The Commitment: The One Thing

At the end of the week, the child picks **one thing from Plato's life they will carry forward**.

Examples:
- *"I will ask three 'why?' questions in a row before I make big decisions."*
- *"I will spend one hour a month with [named formative adult]."*
- *"I will continue using the Academy for the next three months, no screens."*
- *"I will read one full Platonic dialogue a month until I have read them all."*
- *"I will write one dialogue of my own before summer."*
- *"I will design and begin one institution — a group, a tradition, a reading circle — before the end of the semester."*

They write it at the top of a new page in the notebook, dated.

Refer back to this page once a month for the next year.

## Extension Ideas (If You Want More)

### Art & Images
- Find Raphael's *School of Athens* (1509–1511, Vatican). Study the central figures — Plato pointing up (to the Forms), Aristotle pointing out (to the world). This image encodes the whole Plato/Aristotle relationship.
- Find images of the archaeological remains of the Academy in Athens (the site is still visible).
- Look at Hellenistic sculptures of Plato — idealized, older, meditative.

### Books
- **For 8-10:** Children's Plato picture books (several exist; preview)
- **For 11-14:** Rebecca Goldstein, *Plato at the Googleplex* (the biographical chapters); Bryan Magee, *The Story of Philosophy*
- **For 15-18:** Plato, *Five Dialogues* (Hackett); *Republic* Books I, V, VII; *Seventh Letter* (in Cooper's *Complete Works*); Rebecca Goldstein, *Plato at the Googleplex* (full)

### Field Trip Ideas
- The nearest university campus — notice what the Academy pattern has become
- A monastery, church, or historic college in continuous operation for centuries
- A museum of Greek antiquities
- A public lecture with live Q&A — watch the Socratic dynamic (or its failure) unfold

### Deep Dive
- Listen to the BBC's *In Our Time* episode on Plato (free podcast)
- Listen to the *In Our Time* episode on the Cave allegory specifically
- For 15-18: Watch MIT OpenCourseWare's ancient philosophy lectures (free)

## Assessment: How Do You Know They Learned?

By the end of the week, the child should be able to (at their age band):

**All ages:**
- Tell the story of Plato meeting Socrates, losing Socrates, and building the Academy
- Recite *"The unexamined life is not worth living"* and explain it in their own words
- Explain what the Cave allegory is and identify one "shadow" in their own experience
- Name at least one person they consider a formative adult in their life
- Describe the mentor chain: Socrates → Plato → Aristotle → Alexander

**Ages 11+:**
- Explain why Plato wrote philosophy as dialogues instead of treatises
- Describe the Academy as an institution (place, community, curriculum, duration)
- Explain why Plato failed at Syracuse while Aristotle (partially) succeeded with Alexander
- Articulate why *synousia* — being-near a serious mind — matters for formation

**Ages 15-18:**
- Make a substantive argument about the *Republic*'s political meaning (blueprint or thought experiment)
- Apply the Cave allegory honestly to assumptions they themselves share with their own community
- Design an institution for something they currently hold individually
- Have at least begun a sustained *synousia* commitment with a specific adult

If they can do this, the week has worked. If they cannot yet, do not panic — give the material time. You are stocking a soul. Plato returns to adults throughout their lives; your child is being introduced to a friend they will meet again.

---

## What's Next?

Plato opens onto the rest of the classical tradition. The next profiles follow naturally:

- **Aristotle** — the Academy's greatest graduate, founder of the Lyceum, systematizer of knowledge, tutor of Alexander
- **Socrates** — the teacher who never wrote, now readable through the man who loved him most
- **Marcus Aurelius** — the philosopher on the throne, the other end of the philosopher-king thought experiment
- **Isocrates** — Plato's rival in Athens, a different theory of education worth studying for contrast
- **Cicero** — the Roman Plato, who brought the Academy's method into Latin political life

Between this profile and the end of the classical section, the student will see the whole arc: the method, the institution, the king, the emperor, the Roman inheritor. Each profile is a return to Plato in a different register.

Plato is the hinge. Everything before him (Socrates, in the curriculum) and everything after (the whole classical tradition through Augustine and beyond) pivots here.

Teach him well. Then keep going.
